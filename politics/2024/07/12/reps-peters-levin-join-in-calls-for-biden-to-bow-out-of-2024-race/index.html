Reps. Peters, Levin join in calls for Biden to bow out of 2024 race

SAN DIEGO (FOX 5/KUSI) — Two Democratic lawmakers in San Diego County have joined calls on President Joe Biden to step aside as the party's nominee in the upcoming presidential election following last month's disastrous debate performance.

Rep. Scott Peters (CA-50) and Rep. Mike Levin (CA-49) have been the only two members of San Diego's Democratic congressional delegation to directly push for Biden to carry the party at the top of the ticket in the wake of the June 27 debate with former President Donald Trump.

The debate performance ignited a firestorm of scrutiny over the 81-year-old's age and mental acuity, fracturing Democratic leadership, lawmakers and top donors over how to move forward.

Peters joined the conversation last week, saying in an interview on CNN's "The Situation Room" that he was "getting pretty close" to calling on Biden to withdraw from the race. He added he hoped the campaign would acknowledge concerns as legitimate and offer a plan to address falling poll numbers "to win this election."

By Thursday night, Peters solidified his stance about Biden's candidacy in a statement officially asking the presumptive nominee to withdraw.

"The stakes are high, and we are on a losing course," he said. "My conscience requires me to speak up and put loyalty to the country and to democracy ahead of my great affection for, and loyalty to, the President and those around him."

In the event Biden backs out of the election, Peters added he would like to see the next nominee decided in a "fair and transparent process" at the Democratic National Convention next month.

"[W]hen we leave our convention next month in Chicago, we must rally together and fight like hell," Peters said.

Hours later, on Friday morning, Rep. Mike Levin (D-Oceanside) echoed Peters' call, saying he was "naturally concerned about President Biden's performance in the recent debate" and that he believes the party should move forward "with a new leader."

"First, let me say that President Biden has been an outstanding leader, not only of our nation, but of the entire free world," he said. "I have deep respect for President Biden’s five plus decades of public service and incredible appreciation for the work we’ve done together these last three and a half years. But I believe the time has come for President Biden to pass the torch."

The representative went on to say he believes a new candidate would be better equipped to take on Trump in the general election, describing the former president as an "incalculable threat" to American institutions.

According to a report from The Hill, Levin took this directly to Biden on Friday, when he used a question offered by the president to the congressmember during a Zoom call of the Congressional Hispanic Caucus' campaign arm to ask him to step down as a candidate.

Biden responded saying his administration has done more in three years than most other presidents aside from Franklin Roosevelt, according to a source familiar with the conversation. Levin replied saying, "that was great when you were felling good, Biden, are you OK now?"

"It's a legitimate concern for people, but that’s why I think it’s important I gotta get out and show people everything from how well I move to how much I know and that I’m still in good charge," he continued.

The statement issued by the Oceanside lawmaker calling for Biden to "pass the torch" came hours after this conversation, the report noted.

Levin, who is running for reelection in a district considered to be one of the most competitive in the state, is in a slightly different position compared to Peters.

When there is a presidential race, that election drives turn-out. If registered Democrats are unenthusiastic about voting or sit out due to the top of the ticket candidate, that could pose a challenge for Democrats in down-ballot races — especially those vying for competitive seats.

Despite this political turmoil over the last few weeks, however, Biden has been adamant about staying in the race.

During highly anticipated press conference on Thursday at the end of the NATO summit in Washington D.C., Biden forcefully said he believes he is "the most qualified person to run for president" despite anxiety amongst Democrats and key allies.

When asked about why he no longer views himself as a "transitional" figure, the president pointed to the "gravity of the situation [he] inherited" — between economic, foreign and domestic issues.
