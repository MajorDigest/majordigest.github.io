DeSantis against Biden's proposal for Supreme Court term limits, but would support limits for Congress

TAMPA, Fla. (WFLA) -- Florida Gov. Ron DeSantis shared his stance on President Biden's proposal to make changes at the U.S. Supreme Court, including setting term limits for the Justices following years of controversy with the court.

During his press conference in Tampa on Monday, DeSantis claimed he does not support Biden's decision to "go after" the Supreme Court by introducing an 18-year term limit, replacing the lifetime appointment limit, but said he would support change in Congress.

"Supposedly, now Biden's got all these new ideas he's trying to come up with and one of them is, he's really going after the [United States] Supreme Court and one of the things he's proposed is term limits for Justices of the Supreme Court," DeSantis said. "My response is why aren't we doing term limits for members of Congress? That's what would make the biggest difference. Come on!"

The White House detailed Biden's court proposal on Monday, which appears to have little chance of being approved by a closely divided Congress with less than 100 days to before the 2024 Election.

Among the term limits, Biden's bold reforms to "restore trust and accountability to the court and our democracy" include no more immunity for crimes a former president committed while in office and a binding code of conduct for the Supreme Court.

"Here's the thing. Honestly, on the 18-year term limit for Justices. So, if you go back, the Founders said you're going to serve during good behavior, which meant lifetime appointment unless you did something to get you impeached or removed from office. They thought that was important because they wanted the judiciary insulated from the passions of the day," he said.

"An 18-year limit, you'd serve for 18 years, you'd have a fixed term, you wouldn't stand for retention or anything like that. [Democrats] don't like the court now because they're on the wrong side of rulings."

While DeSantis doesn't support Biden's new proposal, he did acknowledge that Florida has its own term limits for state Supreme Court Justices. The Sunshine State is among the 15 states whose Justices have a 6-year term limit. Justices are appointed by the governor.

"When these things happen, you think you're doing it because you think it'll help generate more liberal jurisprudence, and in reality, a lot of times, it backfires. It may end up, if you had 18 years, it would force liberals off the bench and then maybe Republicans would be able to place it, so you just don't know how it's going to shake out."

Florida Democratic Chair Nikki Fried responded to DeSantis' comments.

"First of all, luckily, for America, Ron DeSantis isn't the one cutting any deals because he has no power in this conversation," Fried said. "Part of our democracy is growing with the times and to make sure that there's trust back into this very large pillar of our democracy."

Fried said she doesn't think this could be accomplished under this Congress. She finds the president is making this call following Roe v. Wade being overturned, adding how the high court is right-leaning, tipping the scales of democracy.

"We see that the Supreme Court is underwater, that people have no faith and no trust in this institution," Fried said. "So, being able to have those checks and balances even on the Supreme Court is essential to regain that trust that the American people so desperately need in the judicial system."

The White House is looking to tap into the growing outrage among Democrats about the court, which has a 6-3 conservative majority, issuing opinions that overturned landmark decisions on abortion rights and federal regulatory powers that stood for decades.

"If [Democrats] are willing to support term limits for members of Congress, that would be a trade that I would make," DeSantis said. "I don't think the stuff in the Supreme Court and the 18-year limit would ultimately make a positive difference, but I would be willing to trade that for term limits for members of Congress."

Fried reminds voters, Congress is up to them to choose.

"People ultimately make those decisions, whether to put somebody back into office, but unfortunately, with the Supreme Court, once they are there, there is no more checks and balances on how they operate once they are in that court," Fried said.

According to the Associated Press, the first three justices who would potentially be affected by term limits are on the right. Justice Clarence Thomas has been on the court for nearly 33 years. Chief Justice John Roberts has served for 19 years, and Justice Samuel Alito has served for 18.

Supreme Court justices served an average of about 17 years from the founding until 1970, said Gabe Roth, executive director of the group Fix the Court. Since 1970, the average has been about 28 years. Both conservative and liberal politicians alike have espoused term limits.

There have been increasing questions surrounding the ethics of the court after revelations about some of the justices, including that Thomas accepted luxury trips from a GOP megadonor.

Justice Sonia Sotomayor, who was appointed during the Obama administration, has faced scrutiny after it surfaced her staff often prodded public institutions that hosted her to buy copies of her memoir or children’s books.

Alito rejected calls to step aside from Supreme Court cases involving Trump and Jan. 6 defendants despite a flap over provocative flags displayed at his homes that some believe suggested sympathy to people facing charges over storming the U.S. Capitol to keep Trump in power. Alito says the flags were displayed by his wife.

The Associated Press contributed to this report.
