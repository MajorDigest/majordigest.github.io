If Mitch Haniger isn’t an everyday player, SF Giants have a $43.5 million problem

LOS ANGELES — Gabe Kapler penciled in Mitch Haniger’s name into the fifth spot on the Giants’ lineup card Friday.

Ten months ago, when they inked him to the richest free-agent deal of their offseason, it was unfathomable to think that would be a notable occurrence. Haniger and Michael Conforto, their other prized addition, were expected to provide everyday stability to the lineup. Yet, one day earlier, Haniger was held out of the lineup for a second straight day, reduced to a part-time player in year one of a three-year, $43.5 million commitment.

Asked Thursday about Haniger’s absence, Kapler sighed and said their best option to win that day’s ballgame didn’t include him in the starting lineup.

“No, there’s nothing going on there,” Kapler said. “At some point I’ll have that conversation with Hanny also, which is really just about what gives us the best chance to win every baseball game. Sometimes that means him coming off the bench and taking down a big at-bat.”

The Giants lost 7-2. Haniger didn’t come off the bench or take down a big at-bat. He has played exactly as many games as he did in his injury-plagued final season in Seattle with a fraction of the production. He is batting .214, a career-low, just like his .271 on-base percentage, .378 slugging percentage and .649 OPS.

Haniger was not available in the 50 minutes the clubhouse is open to reporters before Friday’s game.

But Kapler said he got a chance to talk over the situation with him.

“One of the things that we discussed was that, for the moment, there might be other guys that get the reps,” he said. “I think Mitch just wants to know from a long-term perspective that those opportunities are coming. And the answer to that question is absolutely, they always do.”

But in such an underwhelming first season, for his hometown club no less, what gives Kapler confidence Haniger can rediscover the player he was during his five seasons in Seattle, when he was an above average offensive performer each year?

“Injuries, preparation, accountability, work ethic and talent,” Kapler said. “These are things that Mitch has a ton of. …

“What I’ll say about Mitch is that he’s about as accountable and thoughtful and aware as any player that I’ve been around. This is a guy that was our biggest free-agent acquisition and hasn’t played as much as he’d like to. In his perfect world, I think he plays just about everyday if not everyday. He understands that … there’s no manager that doesn’t want to put the best players in the lineup every single day to win baseball games. I think he understands that concept very well and is very accountable to it.”

Over the final two weeks of the season, those opportunities are likely to go to young players. On Wednesday in Arizona, it was Luis Matos. Thursday in Los Angeles, it was Tyler Fitzgerald, who made his major-league debut in center field.

“A guy like Tyler Fitzgerald,” Kapler said, “if he’s able to spark our offense or spark our defense a little bit, he’s gonna get these reps.”

Haniger, who arrived with a reputation for struggling to stay on the field, started the season on the injured list after straining his oblique in spring training. He didn’t make his debut until April 24, almost a full month into the season. His first home run at Oracle Park didn’t come until September, a little more than a week after returning from a two-and-a-half month absence with a fractured forearm.

It has been a season of fits and starts, which could explain why Haniger’s struggle to establish a rhythm offensively. He hasn’t stayed on the field for more than six weeks at a time.

With two more years on his contract, though, and the Giants’ offense stuck in a slump that has encompassed the second half of the season, it’s imperative that he shows up next spring ready to be the everyday contributor San Francisco thought it was signing last winter.

“I think he really wants to be able to trust all that work and that preparation and then ultimately trust his body. It’s hard when it fails you,” Kapler said. “I think there’s some degree to which, along with all those things, you still want to get as many reps as possible so you can get into your rhythm and your timing. … These things are not forever, and these opportunities tend to come back around and come back around quickly.”

Webb has ‘good talk’ with Kapler

It was a long day of one-on-one conversations for Kapler, which isn’t out of the ordinary for the manager who prefers the intimate interactions to addressing the team in a group setting. Only these talks were more public than usual.

None more so than the lengthy and often animated discussion he was having with Logan Webb in the outfield about four hours before first pitch.

Barefoot, the manager and his ace paced back and forth on the grass for approximately 15 minutes. Webb motioned with his arms and seemed to be doing most of the talking. Kapler shrugged his shoulders. When they returned to the dugout, they shook hands and went their separate ways.

Neither party opted to share the contents of the discussion.

“Good talk, good talk,” Webb said.

“One of the things that I’ve learned over the last several years is that there’s nothing better than open-ended conversations without a rush at the end of them,” Kapler said. “So I don’t have anything really specific to share, just that whenever we can find that kind of time … a lot of fruitful conversations happen. I think that was a good example of one.”
