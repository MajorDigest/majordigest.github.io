Yastrzemski’s homer, Manaea’s gem lead SF Giants to win over Dodgers

LOS ANGELES — A night after Mike Yastrzemski called on his teammates to “play with a little more edge,” “take a little more pride” and “hold each other to a higher standard,” he atoned for his own mental mistake in Thursday night’s series-opening loss with a big homer to lead the way in a 5-1 win Friday.

With two outs in the fourth inning, Yastrzemski hooked a two-run homer around the right-field foul pole that broke a scoreless tie and powered the Giants’ win over the Dodgers (94-59), only their second in eight games on this final road trip of the season, pulling them back even in the win-loss column.

Yastrzemski’s homer, his 15th of the season, followed a pinch-hit double by Joc Pederson, and was padded by two more long balls, a solo shot from Thairo Estrada in the sixth and two-run number from rookie Tyler Fitzgerald in the ninth, the first of his career.

That was all the offense needed behind Sean Manaea’s most impressive showing since joining the Giants, though Camilo Doval was called on to record a four-out save, his 38th of the season, after Tyler Rogers loaded the bases with two outs and walked in a run.

Manaea limited a dangerous Dodgers lineup to three hits over seven shutout innings.

“That was huge for us to just give us that confidence that we didn’t have to come from behind,” manager Gabe Kapler said. “One of the themes of today, I think, is preparation and being pretty relentless about processes and controlling the things we can control. Over the course of time, that stuff is going to show up. Sean Manaea has been a good big-league pitcher in the past. I think he’s a good big-league pitcher now.

“I think the same could be said for Yaz. After last night, which was a challenging night for him and all of us, the preparation leads to the home run, the good at-bats. We try to reward as often as possible that consistent preparation and work ethic because we think over the course of time it’s going to show up in moments like tonight.”

With the win, the Giants (77-77) avoided falling two games below .500 for the first time since May. However, their elimination number still fell to six with the Cubs’ win earlier in the day. Improving to 7-13 in September, they still have to win half their remaining eight games to avoid a losing record, possibly more to hold off the streaking Padres, who are one game on their heels and threatening to send the Giants to their lowest divisional finish under Farhan Zaidi and Gabe Kapler.

As the Giants ponder options for next season, Yastrzemski is almost assuredly in their plans. In addition to being the Giants’ fourth-best offensive performer, by wRC+ (116, league average: 100), and his Gold Glove-adjacent defense, he is considered a clubhouse leader and has two more years of arbitration remaining. With a $6.1 million salary this season, he should be in line to earn around $9 million in 2024.

As for Manaea? It’s not such a given that he opts in to his $12.5 million salary for next season. Ross Stripling, who signed an identical contract, has indicated his intentions are to pick up his option. But, unlike Stripling, Manaea regained his spot in the rotation and continued his showcase Friday night.

“Obviously we would love to have Sean back,” Kapler said. “He’s been great for us since, like, the middle of the season on.”

With seven shutout innings, Manaea lowered his season ERA to 4.51. But that doesn’t capture his effectiveness for the final three-quarters of the season. Since his initial move to the bullpen in mid-May, Manaea has a 3.47 ERA — and FIP a half-run lower — including three straight starts of three or fewer runs since returning to the rotation.

It was all the more notable coming at Dodger Stadium, which has been a house of horrors for Manaea. He had been 0-5 with a 9.00 ERA in nine games against Los Angeles, including a 9.78 mark in five previous games in Chavez Ravine. The last time he started a game here, last year with San Diego, he allowed eight runs on three home runs over 4⅓ innings.

“I mean, I have nightmares about coming to this place and facing these guys,” Manaea said. “Obviously they’re very good. They’ve been like that for a long time. I haven’t had much success, if any, here, so to do that, dreams do come true. It feels really good.”

Throwing 61 of his 85 pitches for strikes, Manaea was the most efficient and effective that he has been in a Giants uniform. He limited a dangerous Dodgers lineup to three hits, retiring nine straight before Will Smith poked a single into right field to start the seventh, LA’s first base runner since the third. He was immediately erased on a 5-4-3 double play from the next batter, J.D. Martinez, and a prideful Manaea walked off the mound after retiring Amed Rosario for the final out of the inning and his outing.

Manaea completed seven innings for the first time this season, and tossed seven shutout for the first time since April 8 of last year, his first start for the Padres in a year that went downhill from there and ended as the worst of his career. He has been on the opposite trajectory this season, which could be rewarded with a more lucrative multi-year contract if he chooses to test the market.

“I’m not really worried about any of that stuff,” Manaea said. “We’ll cross that bridge when we get there.”

Notable

— For the second straight game, Marco Luciano recorded some of the Giants’ hardest-hit balls of the night. In fact, his two hits — a 107.8 mph single past shortstop Miguel Rojas in the seventh and a 111.8 mph single up the middle with two outs in the ninth — were the two best pieces of contact by either team all night. He followed his first hit by swiping second base a few pitches later for the first stolen base of his career, and joined Yastrzemski as the only Giants with multiple hits.

— Fitzgerald’s milestone homer followed Luciano’s second hit of the night, and his former minor-league teammate was waiting for him as he crossed home plate. “That was pretty special,” Fitzgerald said. “It’s happened a lot in Sac and throughout the minors.” After working a 2-0 count against Ryan Yarbrough, the 26-year-old rookie fouled off two fastballs before whacking a curveball out to left. “It was unbelievable. I kind of blacked out a bit. I think I rounded the bases a little too fast.”

— Freddie Freeman notched his 200th hit of the season with a single through the right side of the infield, tipping his helmet to the 52,887 fans chanting his first name. Freeman is also four home runs away from reaching 30, a milestone the Giants witnessed J.D. Martinez reach Thursday night. San Francisco has not had a 200-hit season since Rich Aurilia in 2001, nor a 30-homer season since Barry Bonds in 2004.
