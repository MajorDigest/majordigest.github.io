Timeless Kershaw flummoxes SF Giants one more time in loss to Dodgers

LOS ANGELES — Clayton Kershaw has faced the San Francisco Giants more than any other team, and on Saturday took the mound at Dodger Stadium, possibly for the final time. The 35-year-old future Hall of Famer’s status after this season is uncertain. He’s battled injuries. His fastball isn’t what it once was. He’s a different pitcher.

But, in front of 52,704 on hand to watch him potentially one last time (at least in the regular season), the results were all too familiar for his longtime foes.

Without so much as a fastball that registered 90 mph on the radar gun, Kershaw easily dispatched his flailing opponents, blanking the Giants for five innings while the Dodgers sent them to their seventh loss in nine games on this road trip, 7-0. His hottest heater was clocked at 89.7 mph.

“I think what I find most interesting about Clayton is he doesn’t necessarily have to throw 94,” manager Gabe Kapler said afterward. “I was actually talking to some of the younger players about the honor of facing a pitcher of that caliber, one of the best pitchers of our generation, one of the best pitchers of all-time, but he does it with a craftiness that is really second to none.”

The clearest sign of Kershaw’s mortality didn’t come from anything of the Giants’ doing, mustering only two hits and two walks in his five innings, but the fact that his night was over after only five frames and 76 pitches. It was his 12th time in 56 career starts against the Giants that Kershaw left without allowing a run but the first of those starts that his exit came before the sixth — five times taking it all the way.

“The slider-curveball-fastball combination keeps you off balance and spots it up, even at 88-90 mph, can shut you down like he did today,” Kapler said.

Five shutout innings were precisely what Kershaw needed to lower his ERA in 58 career games against the Giants below 2.00. His career mark now sits at 1.99. He should face them once more next weekend at Oracle Park, where he has owned them to an even larger degree, with a 15-6 record and 1.64 ERA.

“I don’t know how old he is, but he’s been doing it forever,” said Jakob Junis, the Giants’ intended bulk-innings pitcher until he left with neck tightness. “You can see his numbers are still insane. Arguably one of the greatest pitchers of all time. He’s got really good numbers against us, too, unfortunately.”

Kershaw has faced off against four active players more than Brandon Crawford but was robbed of another battle when Crawford landed on the injured list prior to this series. The next generation of Giants shortstops, though, got off to a better start than Crawford, who has 14 strikeouts, no extra-base hits and a .125 average in 51 plate appearances against Kershaw.

Rookie shortstop Marco Luciano was 6 years old when Kershaw made his MLB debut in 2008. Asked postgame if he was familiar with Kershaw’s dominance against the Giants, Luciano smiled and responded with one word: “No.”

He lined a double off Kershaw to lead off the fifth inning, the only one of the Giants’ two hits to leave the infield. For the second straight game, Luciano was also responsible for San Francisco’s best-struck ball, leaving the bat at 107.4 mph.

“Of course I know who he is,” Luciano said of Kershaw through a Spanish-language interpreter. “But once the game starts, I just see him as just another pitcher. I know he’s really good. In my mind, I just want to go ahead and compete.”

The Giants had two on and no outs after Kershaw walked Luis Matos for a second time, but Tyler Fitzgerald struck out, Patrick Bailey grounded out to third and David Peralta made a sliding grab, turning a potential extra-base hit from Austin Slater into the third out of the inning.

Kershaw raised both his arms in the air and pointed to left field. His day was done.

The Giants also had runners at first and second in the third, after Kershaw’s first walk to Matos and an infield single from Bailey, but they also weren’t able to score. Austin Slater watched strike three (which appeared to be an inch or two below the strike zone), and Thairo Estrada grounded out to third. They went hitless in their five opportunities with runners in scoring position, stranding four men on base, and put only one man on base in four innings against the Dodgers bullpen.

“What we didn’t do well enough was make sure that fastball was middle of the plate,” Kapler said. “(Kershaw) does such a good job keeping that slider in (and) off or down below the zone, you really have to lay off that pitch. We just didn’t do a good enough job. We’re gonna have to do better than that if we’re gonna beat the best.”

Paired with the Cubs’ win earlier in the day, the Giants’ elimination number fell to four. They are four games back with seven to play, closer to falling into fourth place in the NL West than overtaking Chicago for the final wild card spot. The Padres lost for the first time in nine games, or else they would have pulled into a tie.

Opposing Kershaw, Kapler opted for a bullpen game, but things went haywire quickly.

John Brebbia, the original opener performing the duties for the first time since his lat strain in June, breezed through a 1-2-3 first inning but was sent back out for a second. The first batter, J.D. Martinez, deposited a first-pitch slider into the right-field bleachers, his second homer of the series. Martinez struck again against Jakob Junis, this time doubling home a pair of runs put on base by Taylor Rogers in the third inning.

Junis settled in to retire the next four hitters, and the plan was for him to pitch deeper, but he was forced from the game with neck tightness. It had been bothering him since the club’s trip to Chicago at the start of this month but acted up enough to force him from the game.

“Pretty much every throw I made, it was grabbing and I could tell it was messing with my mechanics,” Junis said. “It stiffened up, and I just didn’t feel like it was worth risking going out there again for that next inning.”

Ross Stripling took over to begin the fifth and allowed the first four batters he faced to reach base, eventually surrendering four more runs on seven hits and three walks while taking down the final four innings.

The Giants wrap up the series and their road slate this season Sunday night on ESPN (4:10 p.m. PT). They are 6-27 in their past 33 road games and 34-46 overall away from Oracle Park. Lance Lynn will start for Los Angeles; San Francisco will start Ryan Walker, with Tristan Beck a likely candidate to take down bulk innings behind him.
