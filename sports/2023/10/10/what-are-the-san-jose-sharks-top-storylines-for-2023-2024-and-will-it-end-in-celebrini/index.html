Top San Jose Sharks top storylines for 2023-2024: Will one end in Celebrini?

SAN JOSE – Those who follow the San Jose Sharks can see that the organization — for the last year or two — has been trying to put in place some building blocks for the future.

Forwards Thomas Bordeleau and William Eklund, drafted in 2020 and 2021, respectively, will be starting the season in the NHL. Defensive prospects Henry Thrun, Shakir Mukhamadullin, and Nikita Okhotiuk, acquired last season in a pair of trades, are all 22 or younger with upside.

Recent early-round draft picks like Will Smith, Filip Bystedt, Quentin Musty, Mattias Havelid, and Cameron Lund project to be in San Jose within the next one to three years. There’s also the potential for an elite forward prospect like Macklin Celebrini or Cole Eiserman to be added to the franchise next year.

But the chance at a bright future can’t hide the real possibility that the Sharks will be the worst team in the NHL this season.

The Sharks are now without their leading goal scorer (Timo Meier), point producer (Erik Karlsson) and winningest goalie (James Reimer) from last season’s team that went 22-44-16, the fourth-worst mark in the NHL. Prognosticators and oddsmakers peg the Sharks to not only miss the playoffs but finish at or near the bottom of the league’s 32-team standings.

In other words, another year of upheaval is in the works, with next season’s roster possibly bearing only a small resemblance to the 2023-24 team.

“We’re obviously a team that’s in transition, so, naturally, there’s going to be a lot of new faces moving in and out every year,” Sharks center Nico Sturm said. “It was like that last year at the (trade) deadline with a lot of movement. It might be like this again this year, depending on how we do, and then, next summer, probably the same thing.

“That’s just the nature of where we’re at as an organization.”

Here are five Sharks storylines for the upcoming season.

Eklund’s development

It’s one thing for Eklund to start the season in the NHL. It’s quite another for him to remain in the league for six months and have the type of season that makes people believe he can be a top-six forward for years to come.

The Sharks took it slow with Eklund’s development, returning him to Sweden in 2021 and starting him in the AHL last season. Now he’s getting an opportunity, at least to start, to play in the NHL for a full 82 games. If he has success, that’s one less question the Sharks will have going forward as their rebuild continues. Otherwise, other recent draft picks will need to develop into elite forwards to take up some of that slack.

But indications are the Sharks have the right guy, as Eklund in camp showed the same qualities that made him the seventh-overall pick two years ago: terrific hockey sense, elite playmaking skill, competitiveness, and tenacity. Now it’s a matter of doing that over a full season.

Bordeleau is in that same category, as the Sharks would love to see him become a steady, middle-six centerman over the next several years to help with the rebuild.

“There are going to be shifts where you don’t touch the puck. Can you still be an effective player when that happens?” Sharks coach David Quinn said. “That’s the challenge for a lot of young players and that’s a challenge for both (Bordeleau and Eklund) that during a shift, if you don’t touch the puck, are you an effective player? That’s what an NHL player does.”

Sign or sell?

General manager Mike Grier figures to have some tough decisions to make with his pending unrestricted free agents. Does he re-sign forwards like Anthony Duclair and Alexander Barabanov or ship them to other teams for valuable assets? He has until the March 8 trade deadline to decide.

Before training camp began, Grier didn’t dismiss the idea of re-signing either Duclair, 28, or Barabanov, 29, thereby having more players to build around and fewer holes to fill. Barabanov has said he wants to stay in San Jose, and Duclair is keeping his options open. More than likely, Grier’s decision will come down to how both players perform, whether they can reach agreements that make sense for the organization, and what other teams might be offering in terms of a trade.

Other pending UFAs include forwards Kevin Labanc and Mike Hoffman, goalie Kaapo Kahkonen, and defensemen Radim Simek and Jacob MacDonald.

Hoffman figures to be moved, particularly if he has a strong start to the season, and Labanc will be available. But the Sharks have only one salary retention spot left after using the other two in the Karlsson and Brent Burns trades, and Grier might have to keep money on the books to move Hoffman or Labanc, who have cap hits of $4.5 million and $4.725 million, respectively.

Futures of Couture, Hertl

Logan Couture and Tomas Hertl understand where the Sharks are at as a team right now, but that doesn’t make being a part of a rebuild any easier to digest. Will they want to follow Burns, Meier, and Karlsson out the door, or are they willing to wait for brighter days ahead, perhaps as soon as the 2025-2026 season?

Couture, 34, has said he wants to remain with the Sharks and be a part of the solution but has also indicated that he doesn’t have the appetite to finish the rest of his contract, which expires after the 2026-2027 season, without another playoff appearance. That deal contains a modified no-trade clause in which he would only accept a trade to one of three teams.

Hertl said at the start of training camp that he doesn’t want to think about his long-term future in San Jose, instead focusing solely on this season. Hertl’s deal lasts another seven seasons and has a full no-move clause this season and next.

Grier said he’s not looking to move either Hertl or Couture but would listen if either one of them came to him seeking a move to more of a win-now team. To trade either one, or both, would have to make sense for the Sharks, first and foremost.

Attendance at the Tank

The Sharks enjoyed hundreds of sellout crowds for the first 25 years or so of SAP Center’s existence. That’s no longer the case. Of the 40 games at the Shark Tank last season, there were only 18 announced crowds of 17,000 or more (capacity was 17,562).

As they marched toward a fourth-straight year without a playoff spot, the Sharks averaged just 13,912 fans per game last season and were the only team in the NHL to play to less than 80 percent of capacity. The Sharks still have their share of diehard fans, but when it comes to buying season tickets, several are choosing partial plans as opposed to full-season packages.

As of May, the Sharks had sold about 9,500 full-season equivalents. For instance, besides a 41-game regular season package, two 20-game packages are a full season equivalent, as are four 11-game packages or five eight-game packages.

Can the Sharks re-establish the same level of local enthusiasm for the team as before when playoff appearances were the norm? As much of a challenge that the hockey operations department has in front of it, the business side might have a bigger hill to climb.

Draft lottery

Connor Bedard was the big prize at last season’s NHL draft lottery. This year it’s Celebrini, a Vancouver native who spent two seasons with the Jr. Sharks from 2018 to 2020 after his dad, Rick, joined the Warriors as their director of sports medicine and performance.

Celebrini, at 6-foot and 190 pounds, had shoulder surgery in May but is expected to begin playing for Boston University sometime this month. From a skill standpoint, he checks just about every box.

Celebrini coming to the Sharks would be serendipity considering his ties to San Jose, the Bay Area and the Sharks organization, with Quinn and Grier being proud BU alums. If the Sharks were to finish with the fewest points in the NHL this season, they would have a 25.5 percent chance of drafting first overall, giving them an opportunity to grab their biggest building block yet.
