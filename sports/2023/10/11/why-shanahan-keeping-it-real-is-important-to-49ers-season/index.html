Why Shanahan ‘keeping it real’ is important to 49ers’ season

SANTA CLARA — Kyle Shanahan did an unusual thing during final preparations for what would be a 42-10 annihilation of the Dallas Cowboys.

The 49ers’ head coach addressed the possibility of losing.

It’s not the usual modus operandi of a football coach, but Shanahan has his own way of doing things and being frank is included in a style developed over 13 years as an NFL assistant and seven years in charge of the 49ers.

So rather than go on a testosterone-fueled Saturday night rage about leaving it all on the field and no one coming back alive in a heated battle with the hated Cowboys, Shanahan accurately addressed the challenge in front of his players against a quality opponent with a 3-1 record.

It’s the NFL, so the likelihood is the 49ers are going to lose a time or three this season, regardless of how good they are. And five games into the season was no time to attach everything to a game that would have made the difference between being 5-0 and 4-1.

Shanahan is only 43, but he has seen plenty since starting in the NFL as a quality control assistant under Jon Gruden in Tampa Bay and winding his way through assistant coach and coordinator jobs in Houston, Washington, Cleveland and Atlanta before being hired by the 49ers in 2017.

“I’ve been in the NFL way too long, been 5-0 and haven’t made the playoffs,” Shanahan said after the 49ers blew out the Cowboys. “I told our team last night whether we win or lose, I expect it to make us better. If we go out there and play like we’re capable, we should feel that and be able to carry it on to the next game.

“And if we go out there and get beat, as long as we do it the right way, I think it will make us better going forward . . . I’m glad we did it the other way, being 5-0 feels good, but it means nothing until you get to the end.”

“Keeping it real” is a familiar refrain from 49ers players regarding Shanahan. When Shanahan and John Lynch received recent contract extensions, defensive end Nick Bosa talked about how the head coach treated starters and practice squad players the same.

“People talk about it a lot about how real he is, about how authentic he is as a guy and as a coach,” linebacker Fred Warner said at the time. “Players relate to that.”

Mind you, playing for Shanahan doesn’t include hearts and flowers. Last season when Shanahan was discussing how well Brock Purdy accepted criticism, he recalled his initial experiences with Matt Schaub.

Schaub had his best seasons in Houston with Shanahan as offensive coordinator, but once stopped the coach mid-sentence when he was running down the list of plays which could have been executed better.

“All right, you played really good except for plays nine, 12 and 13,” Shanahan said.

Schaub asked, “What about the plays I played good on?”

Shanahan replied, “Those don’t matter. That’s what we expect.”

Jimmy Garoppolo, the 49ers’ quarterback from 2017 through 2022, wasn’t always a fan of Shanahan’s exactitude at the expense of simply “playing football.”

Shanahan shares blunt critiques which aren’t personal but very direct, and it’s no coincidence that his father Mike operated in a similar fashion. Gray areas don’t exist as to what’s expected on and off the field. Motivation doesn’t come with a college-style Knute Rockne pep talk.

Instead, Shanahan prefers a needle filled with sarcasm, like the time he suggested to Bosa last season the linebackers had been outperforming the D-line, and got a dominant performance as a reward.

Or when he told Warner how good the Dallas defense was going into last weekend’s Cowboys game and received a career night.

Most of the time though, Shanahan makes corrections and moves on. For something other than play execution, he’ll invite a player into his office like he did with Deebo Samuel last offseason.

Shanahan was prepared with video for Samuel from a disappointing season which came after a huge contract extension. Samuel listened to Shanahan and took the message to heart to such an extent that he told the media all about it and how he wanted to atone for his “awful” season.

He didn’t put Samuel in a “doghouse” or have the press be a messenger about how one of his star players underperformed — a strategy that could have backfired. Instead, Shanahan used a closed-door meeting and film evidence to make his point, presumably without invoking the contract and instead appealing to Samuel’s pride.

Samuel came back in peak condition and was voted a captain by his teammates, a distinction which had eluded him in 2022. Samuel’s numbers aren’t spectacular, but he’s a different guy so far in 2023 in terms of athletic explosion and attitude.

The 49ers have a lot of self-motivated veteran stars who are given detailed assessments of their performance and treated like adults. A weekly Richter scale of emotions isn’t compatible with a 17-game NFL season.

Shanahan didn’t like what he saw from his team going into the Dallas game because of a manufactured rabid intensity. He liked what he saw because they went about their business.

“We’ve got a real good team in that way,” Shanahan said Monday. “Just going into that game against Dallas, with how hyped it was, they weren’t making that much out of it. It was kind of like any other game and any other week.”

If the 49ers had treated the week any other way and won, then the problem becomes selling the next game against Cleveland as equally important in the big picture. And if they had lost, there’s the crushing disappointment of a game to which a false sense of significance had been attached.

Instead, Shanahan kept it real, coaching a team of professionals who know the score five games into a long season and the importance of staying on schedule for a prize that is still three months away.
