Source: 49ers trade for Chase Young to reunite with Nick Bosa, spark pass rush

SANTA CLARA – Defensive end Chase Young is joining the 49ers to form an electric pass-rush tandem again with Nick Bosa, five years after their Ohio State days.

Bolstering a sagging defense, however, is the over-arching goal and crying need for an NFC contender stewing over a three-game losing streak this bye week.

The 49ers acquired Young, a pending free agent, from the Washington Commanders for a third-round draft pick, a team source confirmed less than an hour before the NFL’s trade deadline. Young must pass a physical for the deal to become official; neither he nor the 49ers commented on the deal, though general manager John Lynch will hold a Wednesday morning media call.

Young’s career opened with a similar path as Bosa’s: Both were No. 2 overall picks (Bosa in 2019, Young in 2020), both won NFL Defensive Rookie of the Year honors, and both sustained anterior cruciate ligament injuries in their second seasons.

Now Young and Bosa reunite to try leading the 49ers (5-3) out of their pass-rushing funk. Bosa has just three sacks but an NFL-best 19 quarterback hits since signing the NFL’s richest contract for a non-quarterback in Week 1.

Young, 24, produced five sacks this season before the Commanders (2-5) opted Tuesday to unload both him and fellow defensive lineman Montez Sweat, who was to the Chicago Bears.

When Young tore the ACL in his right knee on Nov. 14, 2021, Bosa made a FaceTime call into Washington’s locker room after that game against Tampa.

“I told him he’ll be fine. It’s going to be a long process. And it gets lonely sometimes,” Bosa told reporters a few days later; Bosa tore the ACL in his right knee as a senior in high school, then the ACL in his left knee in Week 2 of the 2020 season.

Young (6-foot-5, 264 pounds) is in the final year of his rookie contract after the Commanders failed to exercise his fifth-year option. Young’s prorated salary will cost the 49ers just $561,000 for the remainder of this season. He has 14 career sacks in 34 games. His ACL and patellar tendon tears cost him the final half of the 2021 season and all but the final three games of last season, when he debuted in a Christmas Eve road loss to the 49ers.

At Ohio State, Bosa and Young played together in 2017 and the first two games of the 2018 season, from which Bosa bowed out with a core muscle injury. They combined for five sacks against Rutgers in their final game together, a 52-3 win at Ohio State.

The 49ers’ pass defense is middling in terms of yards per game (228.9, ranking 19th), and a key factor in that generosity is their inability to cash in with high-priced pass rushers.

The 49ers are averaging 2.25 sacks per game, with Bosa and Javon Hargrave each accounting for just three of the team’s 18 sacks. Bosa ended his training camp holdout upon landing a record contract (five years, $170 million) a few days before the season opener, and Hargrave defected from the Philadelphia Eagles in free agency (three years, $39 million).

Young is the second pass rusher the 49ers have acquired this month. On Oct. 6, they traded a sixth-round pick to the Denver Broncos for defensive end Randy Gregory, with a seventh-round choice also coming in return. Gregory has played 30 percent of the defensive snaps in his three games with the 49ers, producing a sack and four quarterback hits while working into a rotation with starter Clelin Ferrell and second-year reserve Drake Jackson.

The 49ers’ defensive front thus includes five first-round picks — Bosa, Young, Ferrell (2019, Raiders), Arik Armstead (2015, Javon Kinlaw (2020) — and a pair of second-rounders in Jackson (2022) and Gregory (2015, Cowboys). Jackson has no sacks after three in the season-opening win at Pittsburgh, and his playing time has tailed off to just 60 snaps over the past four games.

Washington general manager Martin Mayhew, three years after leaving the 49ers’ front office, parted with Young for a 2024 compensatory third-round pick, which the 49ers were to receive through the NFL’s minority hiring incentives, for Ran Carthon (Tennessee Titans general manager) and DeMeco Ryans (Houston Texans coach). Another compensatory third-round pick could come from Mike McGlinchey’s free agency exit to Denver.

If Young does not sign an extension with the 49ers and leaves in 2024 free agency, that exit will likely yield the 49ers a 2025 third-round compensatory pick, Sports Illustrated’s Albert Breer reported.

General manager John Lynch is no stranger to October trades for defensive linemen, having also pulled off deals in recent seasons for Jordan Willis (2020) and Charles Omenihu (2021).

A year ago, the 49ers made one of the best trades in franchise history to get running back Christian McCaffrey, sending four draft picks to the Carolina Panthers. McCaffrey, a Stanford product, has matched an NFL record by scoring in his past 17 games, including last year’s three playoff games. Five years ago on Halloween, the 49ers made official another memorable trade, for quarterback Jimmy Garoppolo.

Here are the projected quarterbacks on the 49ers’ remaining schedule, in order: Trevor Lawrence (Jags), Baker Mayfield (Bucs), Geno Smith (Seahawks), Jalen Hurts (Eagles), Smith, Kyler Murray (Cardinals), Lamar Jackson (Ravens), Sam Howell (Commanders) and Matthew Stafford (Rams).

Cornerback and the interior offensive line are spots where the 49ers also could have used help before the trade deadline.

Options behind Charvarius Ward, Deommodore Lenior and nickel back Isaiah Oliver remain Ambry Thomas and Anthony Brown, but Sam Womack and Darrell Luter Jr. eventually could be activated off injury lists.

On the offensive line, left guard Aaron Banks emerged from Sunday’s loss with turf toe, and that comes with a vague recovery timeline other than a likelihood he’ll miss the next couple of games. Veteran Jon Feliciano is Banks’ backup, with Nick Zakelj also on the roster.

Because Young’s deal is pending a physical, the only moves announced by the 49ers Tuesday were the release of cornerback Anthony Brown (two games, no defensive snaps, three special teams snaps), and the practice-squad transactions in which they signed offensive lineman Jesse Davis and released cornerback Kendall Sheffield.
