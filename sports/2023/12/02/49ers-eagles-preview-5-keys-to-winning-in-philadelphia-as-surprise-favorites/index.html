49ers-Eagles preview: 5 keys to winning in Philadelphia as surprise favorites

PHILADELPHIA — Seven months ago, when the NFL announced the 49ers’ NFC Championship Game rematch would be this Sunday in Week 13, coach Kyle Shanahan expressed appreciation for the scheduling.

“I am glad we’re not playing Philly Week 1,” Shanahan said. “I want to play them at a better time. I want to make sure our whole team is right.”

Brock Purdy’s surgically repaired elbow is indeed right, and the 49ers (8-3) are rightfully focused ready for their return engagement at Lincoln Financial Field. They’ve downplayed this as “just another game,” but they’re well aware that a win would be a step toward wresting the No. 1 seed from the Eagles (10-1) come playoff time.

The game’s stakes, although high, don’t match those last January when the teams played for a Super Bowl berth. You know the rest of the story: Purdy gets hurt on his sixth snap, backup Josh Johnson sustains a concussion right after halftime, Purdy is forced to return but can’t throw the ball, and the Eagles roll to a 31-7 win.

“Neither team finished the job so it’s not like they’re looking back and reminiscing on an NFC championship win, either,” Nick Bosa said, referencing the Eagles’ loss to Kansas City in the Super Bowl.

Purdy’s right arm recovered so well he’s leading the NFL in passer rating, and the 49ers are riding a three-game win streak, with their defense surging behind a star-studded frontline and an improving secondary.

The Eagles are off to a 10-1 start for the second straight year, and Sunday night’s 37-34 overtime win over the Buffalo Bills marked their fourth straight comeback from a halftime deficit.

Here are the 49ers’ keys to victory:

That means more than just blocking Haason Reddick better, whether that task falls on starting tackles Colton McKivitz and Trent Williams, or on tight ends George Kittle and Charlie Woerner. Or even on Shanahan’s play-calling.

When Reddick raced around tight end Tyler Kroft on the play that destroyed Purdy’s elbow, Purdy had waited an extra beat for Brandon Aiyuk to come open. There’s no need to buy time this game.

Purdy should get the ball out quickly to Christian McCaffrey to exploit mismatches against the Eagles’ thin linebacker corps. That goes for passes, handoffs or laterals. McCaffrey, the NFL’s leading rusher, has scored in three straight games (all wins) since his 17-game touchdown streak was snapped.

Ultimately, yes, the 49ers’ offensive line must counter a still-superior Eagles defensive front.

Guard Jon Feliciano, who faced the Eagles three times last year while with the Giants, sees a different unit. Last season, the Eagles had D-line depth. This year they have an ascending interior punch with Jordan Davis and Jalen Carter. As for Reddick, Felicano said: “His athleticism and frame might lull you to sleep. You might not think he has power as well. That’s kind of what makes him a great player.”

Eagles quarterback Jalen Hurts rightfully deserves praise for converting short-yardage runs via the “Brotherly Shove.” He has 11 rushing touchdowns, eight on 1-yard shoves.

To stay out of that situation, they need to win the early downs; creating third-and-long situations that will limit short-yardage opportunities for the Eagles’ signature play. Hurts also can go long with his feet, problematic “because those plays are super deflating for defenses,” Bosa said.

The 49ers are stronger up front this time around. They not only poached defensive tackle Javon Hargrave from the Eagles in free agency, but they also brought in a complementary collection of defensive ends in Clelin Ferrell, Chase Young and Randy Gregory. Defensive tackle Arik Armstead’s foot injury is a concern, but he says he’ll play through that pain.

Linebacker Fred Warner wasn’t surprised when told that Hurts has fumbled in five consecutive games. (Three resulted in turnovers.)

“Some of those, it’s just bad luck, like last game, when they tried to make a draw and the handoff got fumbled around,” Warner said. “I’m sure they want to limit those turnovers, and it’s our job to force them.”

The 49ers have recovered a fumble in four consecutive games, five fumbles in all, and have generated nine turnovers in all in those four games. They lead the NFL with 15 interceptions. (For the sake of contrast, in 2018, they intercepted only two passes all season.) They have a plus-11 turnover differential, tied with the Pittsburgh Steelers for tops in the league.

Hurts already has thrown 10 interceptions, a single-season career-high. The Eagles are a minus-2 with 16 turnovers against 14 takeaways.

The Eagles are the first 10-1 underdog playing at home in at least 20 years. The 49ers are 3-point favorites.

“Going into last year, nobody thought we’d be any good. It’s OK to be the underdog,” Eagles coach Nick Sirianni said. “Before I ever stepped foot in this city all I knew was ‘Rocky vs. Apollo Creed.’ This city plays the underdog well.”

The 49ers have been favored in every game since the NFC Championship Game when the Eagles were 2 ½-point favorites at home. The 49ers were favored by at least a touchdown at Cleveland and at Minnesota, both of which dissolved into losses. Their 31-13 win last game in Seattle saw them make good on their biggest spread (7 points) in 23 visits to Lumen Field.

Shanahan on this week’s odds being in the 49ers’ favor: “It doesn’t make a lot of sense to me, but I don’t really know how that stuff works. So, I don’t really know why. It is random.”

The 49ers’ three-game win streak has them full of confidence, they’re much healthier and rested than the Eagles. This time they also have two backups to Purdy in case of emergency.

One thing the 49ers haven’t done is come back to win in the fourth quarter. It’s something the Eagles do routinely. Last Sunday night they won 37-34 in overtime after allowing 505 yards at home against the Bills.

The 49ers are 0-37 when down by eight or more points since 2017 under Shanahan, according to The Associated Press.

The Eagles, on the other hand, have rallied for halftime deficits in their past four games. As 49ers defensive coordinator Steve Wilks said, Hurts has “an ability to never be out of it. He can bring those guys back.”

Shanahan’s goal to counter that: “You’ve got to be able to weather the storm when they get you. Let it be a first down and not a touchdown. Play very disciplined, very sound, and when you get your opportunity to make plays you can’t miss them.”
