White Sox in 'serious' discussions to build new ballpark at prime South Loop location, according to new report

CHICAGO — Another potential relocation spot for the Chicago White Sox is being discussed, but this move would not be far and would keep the MLB franchise in the city.

According to a new Sun-Times report, the White Sox are considering building a new ballpark in what could become Chicago's next neighborhood.

The newspaper reported Wednesday night the team has its eye on a huge piece of undeveloped land near the South Loop, known as "The 78," to be the location for a baseball-only stadium. According to the report, sources familiar with the talks, who asked to remain anonymous, say the negotiations are "serious."

White Sox management has talked about moving out of Guaranteed Rate Field, which the franchise has called home since 1991, when the team's lease there expires after the 2029 season. During MLB's Winter Meetings in December, White Sox owner Jerry Reinsdorf met with Nashville, Tenn., Mayor Freddie O'Connell, prompting speculation of a relocation there.

However, a move to "The 78" would keep the franchise in Chicago for the foreseeable future.

Reinsdorf and Chicago Mayor Brandon Johnson issued a joint statement in response to Sun-Times inquiries. The statement made no mention of the possibility the Sox would move from their current spot at Guaranteed Rate Field to "The 78."

"We met to discuss the historic partnership between the team and Chicago and the team’s ideas for remaining competitive in Chicago in perpetuity," the statement read. "The partnership between the city and the team goes back more than a century, and we are both committed to continuing this dialogue moving forward."

Measuring 62 acres and located at Roosevelt Road and Clark Street, just south of downtown Chicago, the potential site being discussed has been called some of the best undeveloped land in the city. The ballpark would be strategically located, with access to the expressway and public transit systems and room for restaurants and bars to surround it.

The parcel is known as "The 78" for its potential to become Chicago's 78th neighborhood.

Should the White Sox move there, or elsewhere from its current spot, that would end the franchise's more than century-long residence in the Bridgeport neighborhood on the city's South Side.

"If they were to move, I think it would be kind of a hit to the community and a lot of businesses around here," Bridgeport resident and lifelong White Sox fan Tyler Chichy told WGN on Thursday.

Guaranteed Rate Field has been updated and renovated since it first opened, but according to White Sox fan Mario Tormey, Chichy's friend and fellow Bridgeport resident, ongoing violence in Bridgeport could be motivating the team to move out.

"I think they want to get out of here, try to put (a ballpark) into a place where it would bring more people, or more people would want to come," Tormey told WGN on Thursday.

But there would be hurdles to clear before a possible move to "The 78" could become a reality.

The Sun-Times reports that the Illinois Sports Facilities Authority, which owns and financed Guaranteed Rate Field, hasn't been involved in the discussions, according to the authority's CEO, Frank Bilecki. The Sun-Times says the stadium authority would have to get involved at some point in determining the future of Guaranteed Rate Field and potentially in building a new ballpark, if it's publicly funded.

The potential site for a new ballpark at "The 78," reports the Sun-Times, is owned by the company Related Midwest. Per the Sun-Times, Related Midwest president Curt Bailey, as well as a White Sox spokesperson and a senior adviser to Mayor Johnson, all refused to comment. Related Midwest also had no comment to an inquiry from WGN about "The 78" being discussed as a possible location for a new White Sox ballpark.

Additionally, the state of Illinois already has a project slated for "The 78." The University of Illinois is supposedly set to break ground there this year on a technology, research and innovation center called Discovery Partners Institute.

The Sun-Times reports the institute is expected to use about four acres on the southern part of "The 78," and that it's the only project currently planned for the entire parcel.

Bill Jackson, executive director of Discovery Partners Institute, told the Sun-Times he's heard the White Sox have interest in "The 78" and that a new ballpark and the institute would work well together sharing the land.

However, a Chicago developer familiar with the property and requesting anonymity told the Sun-Times the institute has been trying to get out of the deal and that there wouldn't be enough room for both the institute and a new ballpark within "The 78," anyway.

But veteran Chicago-based stadium consultant Marc Ganis disagreed with that assessment, telling the Sun-Times that about 12 to 14 acres would be required to build a baseball-only stadium that seats 35,000 or so, plus another three acres for a parking structure that would hold around 7,000 cars.

Based on those measurements, Ganis projects to the Sun-Times, "The 78" would have enough space for a new ballpark and Discovery Partners Institute.

With the White Sox in ballpark discussions, two of the city's major professional sports franchises are now involved in such talks. The NFL's Bears, of course, have been in much-discussed, ongoing talks for a new stadium.

"The 78" could feasibly be a spot for a new Bears stadium, but the Sun-Times reports sources said the team isn't interested in that location.
