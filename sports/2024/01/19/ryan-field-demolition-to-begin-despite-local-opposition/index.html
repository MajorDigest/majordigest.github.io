Ryan Field demolition to begin despite local opposition

EVANSTON, Ill. — The chain link fence is about to go up, and the demolition is about to go down at Northwestern University's (NU) Ryan Field, starting in nearly a week-and-a-half.

According to a report from The Daily Northwestern, NU officials and project contractors announced Thursday that the demolition of Ryan Field will begin on Jan. 29, with fences, trailers and other equipment being brought in for set-up starting next week.

After Evanston City Council voted to approve the project in late November, the university set a timetable in motion where demolition is planned to take four months, with further excavation possibly taking even longer, Alderwoman Eleanor Revelle (7th Ward) and Dave Davis, NU's senior executive director of neighborhood and community relations, said at the announcement meeting Thursday.

Turner Construction senior superintendent Michael Musial — representing one of two contractors employed to handle the Ryan Field demolition Thursday, alongside Walsh Construction — said the demolition project will employ roughly 150-200 total employees, with work hours set for 7 a.m. - 7 p.m. during weekdays, and 8 a.m. - 5 p.m. on Saturdays.

"The staging of the steel will be right next to my house. They didn't share any of that," Evanston resident Ken Proskie told WGN News. "Our experience in the past, when they've had construction and allowed workers to park here, there's a lot of noise before seven."

Musial also said the demolition will not use explosives or wrecking balls, but will instead use a piece-by-piece excavator where crews will mist air and equipment at the construction site to reduce the accumulation of dust.

As the demolition goes on, NU will conduct weekly analyses of vibrations caused by the demolition project across the site, according to Andrew San Roman, Evanston's building official and plan reviewer. San Roman added city permitting for the demolition will also likely wrap up some time next week.

Residents were able to make public comment at the meeting Thursday, where they raised concerns over the back-and-forth of trucks, people, and debris, as well as asbestos and lead paint degrading the safety of the area around the demolition site during the project.

“If there are any major concerns on site, the city is prepared to stop work,” San Roman told The Daily Northwestern. “But we’re confident that the vibration monitoring that Northwestern will do will help in that regard.”

“[Asbestos and lead paint concerns have] been identified by consultants, as being addressed per the regulatory protocols that will be completed prior to any demolition,” Musial added, with officials saying that more details on asbestos removal, truck movements and other items of interest to the community will come as the project continues.

NU officials said Evanston residents with demolition-related concerns can file a service request through the City of Evanston's 311 system.

The announcement comes after months of pushback from local residents, highlighted by a complaint filed in the Chancery Division of the Circuit Court of Cook County, and protests raising resident's concerns over rezoning the stadium to allow for a handful of concerts.

The complaint, which is still pending in Cook County Circuit Court as of Friday, Jan. 19, alleges a backroom deal where Evanston Mayor Daniel Bliss and Evanston City Council members agreed to disregard laws and evidence in exchange for monetary contributions from the university.

Back in Sept. 2023, protestors marched on Evanston City Hall and Land Use Commission meetings to object to the stadium's rezoning, which would allow for up to six concerts to be hosted at the new Ryan Field once the project is completed.

Shortly before the September demonstrations, NU officials reduced the number of proposed concerts from ten to six, limited community activities at the stadium to just 60 days a year, and added in financial commitments that included $10 million directed toward a technology upskill program for adults, and a $500,000 commitment to local public schools through an event ticket surcharge to proposed concerts at the new Ryan Field.

As of Evanston City Council's vote to approve the Ryan Field stadium renovation project in November, NU had offered up a benefits package to the City of Evanston valued in total around $175 million over 15 years.
