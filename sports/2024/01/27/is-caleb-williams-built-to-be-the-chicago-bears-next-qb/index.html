Is Caleb Williams built to be the Chicago Bears' next QB?

CHICAGO — The history of the Chicago Bears is littered with blue collar gritty-ism's and hard-nosed football, but the often mentioned peak of the franchise's existence — the 1985 Bears — was a team as flashy and star-studded as it was based in being able to do the dirty work needed to win, which begs the question...

Is Caleb Williams actually what Chicago needs to restore them to heights comparable of the 1985 Bears?

A snapshot of Williams reveals an individual driven to unique levels, intent on becoming one of the game's best quarterbacks, but also shows someone born at an intersection of time that not only carries intentions of capitalizing on his stardom outside of football, but is also fully capable of doing so.

A 2023 GQ profile of Williams encapsulated this well.

From the get-go, Williams always hated to lose, the GQ profile noted.

He also wasn't always a quarterback. At first, he was a two-way player who starred at running back and linebacker, but after one game that ended with the opposition taking home the victory, Williams' hatred of losing came to a headway, leading to tears and the decision for him to become a quarterback — so that stinging sensation of defeat never happened again.

Williams was 10 years old, and he's been preparing to become one of the NFL's best quarterbacks ever since.

As a high schooler who grew up near Washington D.C., he was doing hot yoga and media training during the school year, then zigzagging across the country to work with the best quarterback coaches in the United States over the summer.

Every day featured predawn workouts, private tutoring in quarterback play, temperature-controlled rooms to maximize recovery, and a constantly-updated spreadsheet ranking college programs Williams and his inner circle thought could best develop him into the world's next great quarterback.

The process got the rinsed and repeat treatment everyday until he became the country's top-ranked quarterback recruit, and committed to play for Lincoln Riley at the University of Oklahoma.

Williams took a 66-yard scamper to the house on his first snap against the University of Texas in the vaunted Red River rivalry, sparking an epic 55-48 comeback victory over the Longhorns. Williams finished 2021 with 2,354 total yards and 27 total touchdowns in 11 games played.

After the end of the season, Williams followed Riley to the University of Southern California, where his star shined even brighter.

With home games now at LA Memorial Coliseum, Williams — nicknamed "Superman" — accounted for more than 4,900 total yards and 52 touchdowns on his way to winning the Heisman Trophy as a sophomore at USC in 2022.

Six weeks into this past college football season, he remained a candidate to win a second Heisman — before his statistics (and team performance) fell off — but he still finished with 3,769 total yards, 41 total touchdowns and a better passer rating than he had during his Heisman Trophy campaign (170.1 compared to 168.5).

"The charismatic gunslinger is a certified baller with a knack for elevating the performance of the players around him," said Fox Sports NFL analyst Bucky Brooks of Williams in a Jan. 2024 article. "Whether it is the pinpoint dimes seemingly dropped from the heavens into the hands of speedy pass-catchers streaking down the seams or impromptu scrambles that mask the offensive line's deficiencies, Williams' superpowers can expand the playbook for a creative playcaller."

Brooks is far from the only man who's marveled at the talents Williams possesses, while also trying to characterize the type of quarterback he can become in the National Football League.

Scouts from coast-to-coast have lauded him as one of the rarest talents they've ever had the privilege of scouting.

"He is one of the best football players, flat out, I've ever scouted," One AFC scout, who has worked in the league for more than a decade, told ESPN's Matt Miller in an Oct. 2023 article. "He's consistently the best player on the field."

Summarized, Williams is a mobile-and-then-some quarterback with the confidence, instincts, athleticism and arm talent to make any throw from every imaginable angle, while also using those abilities to elevate the play of his teammates.

Former NFL quarterback and The QB School's JT O'Sullivan broke down a touchdown pass Williams made against the University of Nevada that illustrated how each of those traits came together for one of Williams patented off-schedule plays.

The play in question can be found at the 19-minute mark. As O'Sullivan noted, after the ball was snapped, Williams calmly progressed through three reads (while maintaining proper base and footwork) before the buzzer went off in his head to go into scramble mode.

He secured the ball with both hands, evaded two pass rushers, gave a slight peek-behind to make sure he had space to roll out into, and fired a frozen rope fading to his right to Brenden Rice, who settled into an open window in the end zone for a Trojan touchdown.

"I don't know what you do here besides hope you have better pass rush that can get home," O'Sullivan said during the video breakdown.

Mechanics, athleticism, arm talent, instincts and confidence all came together in holy matrimony on one play to display the type of quarterback Williams is at his peak — a taste of his sky-high potential that also doubles as an example of why he is so marketable off the field.

With the NCAA's adoption of Name, Image and Likeness (NIL) deals in July 2021, Williams has been able to parlay his California college quarterback superstardom into sponsorship and equity deals with companies like Beats by Dre, Dr. Pepper, Neutrogena, Playstation, United Airlines, Wendy's and more.

The only other piece of the pie that compares to Williams' talents on the field — and his growing collection of large scale sponsorship deals off of it — is the USC Trojan quarterbacks' personal brand of style.

His confidence often permeates from his uniform on the field, to the outfits and style choices he makes off it.

He paints his nails before every game, sometimes in vivid color combinations, and other times with messages that have drawn the ire of sports social media (the message he painted on his nails before playing Utah, being one example).

He is hip to modern fashion culture and embraces designer clothes fit for fashion runways in Paris, New York and Milan that plenty in Chicago may consider outlandish, or even outside the mainstream expectations held of how athletes should dress.

All things considered, the facets of Williams' life tied together culminate in the arrival of the next-generation superstar athlete — one born smack dab in the middle of a digitally-infused Gen Z, armed with the confidence and talent to go where no athlete has gone before, and push boundaries that weren't previously known to exist.

In a way, doesn't it sound familiar Chicago?

38 years ago today, the Bears obliterated the New England Patriots, 46-10, on their way to the franchise's first and only Super Bowl victory — becoming the epitome of superstardom in the process, and redefining the level of popularity football's best team could achieve.

A collection of players formed "The Chicago Six" — a music group responsible for the iconic Super Bowl Shuffle, and several live performances over the years following Super Bowl 20.

The Bears' head coach, Mike Ditka — known for his hard-nosed, gritty demeanor — became so synonymous with Chicago sports culture after the Super Bowl victory, that people still dress up in his image every Halloween around the city.

Walter Payton raced through, around and over NFL defenses to the tune of one of the greatest nicknames in sports history — Sweetness — while also gracing the covers of Time Magazine and the Wheaties box, and becoming one of football's most well-known humanitarians.

While Payton was iconic in nickname, he was also iconic in work ethic. He pioneered intensive offseason workouts — like training on 'The Hill,' as it was coined, and using bales of hay as hurdles during cardio workouts — to keep himself in football shape during a time where that was basically unheard of.

“Tough times go away, tough people do not,” Payton once said.

By way of nicknames, Payton also wasn't the only Monster of the Midway known by a slick pseudonym either. In fact, he was far from it.

Almost everyone on the defensive line had their own catchy nickname. Steve "Mongo" McMichael, William "The Refrigerator" Perry, and Dan "Danimal" Hampton terrorized defenses (and in the case of Perry, punched in touchdowns on trick plays) before seemingly rampant stardom followed them off the field.

McMichael went on to become a professional wrestler and color commentator after his pro football career, while "The Fridge" appeared in commercials for Kraft Macaroni and Cheese and Mr. Big Paper Towels, and even had his own G.I. Joe action-figure. Hampton was a bassist and vocalist in The Chicago Six during their playing days before moving on to a sports broadcasting career of his own — one where he's still a co-host of the television show Pro Football Weekly, which recently completed it's 20th season on-air.

While some of Williams' outfits are a departure from the same glamour burned into the brains of Bears fans when they relive the memories of glory days gone by, the way he carries himself on and off the field is not.

His work ethic is reminiscent of some of Chicago's all-time greats, and the buzz that follows him now, could develop into a similar buzz that brought the Bears fame and fortune 38 years ago today.
