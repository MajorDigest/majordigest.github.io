New Bears OC Shane Waldron confident in adaptability of his offensive system

LAKE FOREST, Ill. — At his introductory press conference Thursday, new Chicago Bears offensive coordinator Shane Waldron emphasized building an offense focused around creativity and teaching ability, but tap danced around questions concerning the Bears' quarterback position.

"You want to be creative," said Bears head coach Matt Eberflus at the beginning of the press conference. "You want to be curious to really make the scheme go, and make it go with the skillsets we have, and that’s important on offense and defense.”

Creativity and cohesion were at the heart of Waldron's first soirée with local media as the Bears' OC, where he said his tenets as play-caller with the Bears will align with many of the same buzzwords Eberflus has continually hammered home since taking control of the ship in Chicago back in Jan. 2022.

Adaptability. Adjustments. Putting players in the right situations to succeed.

“A lot of the conversation revolved around adaptability," Waldron said. "What you can do with different pieces of the puzzle depending on each year. Each year in this league is going to be its own individual year, and I think we’re priding ourselves on having an offensive system, a group of coaches who can adapt and adjust the scheme to our players’ skillsets.

"It’s our job first to be great teachers, and second, to put guys in the right position, the best position for their own individual success, to lead to our team’s success."

Waldron told media, since being hired, conversations between him and Bears leadership have centered on much more than just the quarterback situation in Chicago. They expanded to talk about every position group touched by the offense, and worked their ways back to one of those A-words — adaptability — and how they would need to continue to adapt as other teams and players around the NFL do the same.

The center of his scheme, he said, will revolve around developing relationships with his fellow coaches. That way, everyone is on the same page and using the same verbiage to make building around each player's skillset as easy as possible, including the quarterback position.

When asked if he thinks any quarterback could thrive in his offensive system — whether it be Justin Fields or a rookie the Bears take in the upcoming NFL draft — Waldron was confident in his approach, no matter who's under center.

“I totally believe that, yeah. I think that in my past experiences with different quarterbacks with different experience levels, whether I was in the coordinator role or in a role as a position coach, it felt that way," Waldron said. "It felt like different quarterbacks have been able to step foot into the system and learn it quickly. That starts with us, being able to teach it in a good and efficient manner where they understand it, and then being able to go and adjust because each guy’s going to have a different skillset."

Under NFL policy, players and coaches can't work together in person until phase one of offseason workouts begin in April, but Waldron told media that he and Fields have "exchanged messages" to begin building a relationship with one another.

When asked specifically about the quarterback many expect to go first overall in the upcoming draft — Caleb Williams — Waldron deflected and spoke in vague generalities, offering praise for the play of college quarterbacks this year and the product we saw on display across college football.

“I think my impression of a lot of these college quarterbacks is what an incredible college season we just came off of," Waldron said. "With some of these guys making these dynamic plays down the stretch, whether it was leading into the championship games or the bowl games leading up to that.

"Obviously for me, being out on the West Coast, I got a little bit more exposure to the Pac 12 … in terms of that, its been more of seeing these guys perform at a high level under the national spotlight, especially when there’s been multiple quarterbacks who have already performed on the highest stage or in big games, and obviously he’s one of them."

Waldron was the Seahawks’ offensive coordinator over the last three years, and was considered by many to not only be one of the Bears’ top options to fill their vacancy at offensive coordinator, but was maybe thee top option.

Eberflus said Thursday that 14 candidates were interviewed for their previously vacant coordinator roles, and Waldron was the only candidate they interviewed twice for either position.

Waldron’s three seasons in Seattle saw him call plays for an offense that was above-average-to-good every year during his tenure. The Seahawks ranked 16th, tenth and 12th in points-per-drive, 16th, tenth and 21st in total passing yards, and 14th, 13th and tenth in EPA (Expected Points Added) per play over the last three seasons.

Notable during Waldron’s tenure as OC is how he helped revive the career of Geno Smith, who threw for 4,281 passing yards and 30 TD’s in 2022 on his way to winning the Associated Press Comeback Player of the Year award.

"My experience with Geno was unbelievable," Waldron said. "I started with him as a player … You talk in terms of what guys can learn from him, is to have a unique ability to have a positive mindset."

The NFL Scouting Combine kicks off on Feb. 26 and lasts until March 4, with the NFL Draft set to take place in Detroit April 25-27.
