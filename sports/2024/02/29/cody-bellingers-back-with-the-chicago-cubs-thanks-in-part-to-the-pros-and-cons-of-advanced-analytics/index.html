Cody Bellinger's back with the Chicago Cubs, thanks in part to the pros and cons of advanced analytics

MESA, Ariz. — After three-and-a-half months of back-and-forth negotiations, Cody Bellinger is back in a Chicago Cubs uniform, thanks mostly to the ongoing discussions that surrounded how advanced analytics painted the likelihood of him replicating his success last year, in the future.

With a drawn out free agency window finally closed, the newly-resigned left-handed slugger, general manager Jed Hoyer and baseball mega-agent Scott Boras gathered Wednesday to detail how the trio arrived at this moment in time with Bellinger back in a Cubs uniform.

“I mean I didn't hide the fact internally that I did want to come back here and you know, I just love Wrigley Field," Bellinger said from a press conference Wednesday. "I love the fans. Me and my family enjoy Chicago and when it was coming towards the end and everything was kind of coming to light a little bit … This was definitely something I wanted, and both sides agreed on, and I’m super happy that it worked out the way it did.”

In the wee hours of early last Sunday morning, ESPN's Jeff Passan broke the news that Bellinger had signed a 3-year/$80 million deal to return to the North Side of Chicago, checking the box of what many said was the team's biggest need heading into the offseason.

Bellinger slashed .308/.358/.528 with 26 home runs, 97 RBI's and 20 stolen bases across 128 games in 2023, anchoring the center of the Cubs lineup while providing Gold Glove-level defense in center field and at first base, leading to Bellinger taking home National League Comeback Player of the Year honors.

Once it became clear that a long term deal nearing or exceeding the $200 million mark was out of the question this offseason, Bellinger said returning to Chicago became his top priority, citing how close the Cubs came to making the postseason a year ago, and wanting to experience what playoff baseball is like in Chicago.

"That's it, honestly," said Bellinger, when asked about the end of last season. "We were so close last year, really got banged up there towards the end [and] fought till the very end. We got so close.

"For me to come back here with almost the same team — to experience playoffs in Chicago is something I want to do and something I want to experience and is why I did want to come back here."

That's what Cubs fans (and Bellinger) are hoping he can help recreate through building on a successful 2023 campaign — a return to winning ways in the postseason.

The last time the North Siders made the playoffs was during the COVID year in 2020, and the last time they won a playoff series was in 2017 when the Cubs beat the Arizona Diamondbacks in the NL Division Series.

Caution may be a part of the reason so many other teams didn't line up as potential suitors for Bellinger's services though.

Compared to years past, 2023 was a resurgence for Bellinger — A breath of fresh air pumped into a career that had previously gone into an injury-riddled tailspin after reaching baseball's mountaintop back in 2019 as the NL's Most Valuable Player.

Where Bellinger tied for the team lead in home runs and lead the Cubs in slugging %, OPS (.881) and OPS+ (133) in 2023, his 2021 and 2022 seasons paled in comparison to the book ends of his last four campaigns (minus the 2020 COVID season)

In 2019, Bellinger played in all 162 games for the Los Angeles Dodgers, slashing .305/.406/.629 with a 1.035 OPS, 47 home runs and 115 RBI's to go with a Gold Glove for his defense played in right field.

From 2021-22, Bellinger played in 239 of 364 possible games, slashing .193/.256/.355 with a .611 OPS, 12 home runs and 54 RBI's while nursing a number of injuries — including complications surrounding the recovery of a dislocated shoulder that required surgery during the 2020 offseason.

Those injuries, combined with advanced analytics pointing toward Bellinger's tendencies for soft contact in 2023, painted a picture of having to roll the dice with the lefty slugger, rather than him being a slam dunk to repeat his performance from last season.

According to Baseball Savant, Bellinger was in the bottom third of baseball in average exit velocity (87.9 mph - 22nd percentile), barrel % (6.1% - 27th percentile), hard hit % (31.4% - 10th percentile) and chase % (31% - 33rd percentile) last year.

Boras pushed back against this narrative during the press conference Wednesday, saying the numbers haven't been put in the proper context.

“Those numbers have not been appropriately addressed," Boras said. "Because when we talk about soft contact, they don't talk about the counts when he has oh, a 1-0, 2-0, 3-1 counts. Check what his contact strength is. And then when he took a two-strike approach last year.

"All of a sudden he's now got — if not the best in the National League, but the second best in baseball — batting average with two strikes going through a more contact approach, rather than taking a customary, powerful swing as he would earlier in the counts. So, the data that's presented has not been talked about [when it comes to] the real benefits to major league teams.”

In 2023, Bellinger had a slugging % above .600 in 1-0, 2-0, 0-1, 1-1 and 3-1 counts, with him being most deadly in 0-1 and 3-1 counts where he notched a .694 and a whopping 1.125 slugging %, respectively.

Bellinger was also a far more proficient hitter in pitcher-friendly counts compared to the average MLB hitter last season. He slashed .296/.304/.407 in 0-2 counts and .265/.282/.337 in 1-2 counts — compared to the MLB average hitter — who slashed .159/.169/.238 in 0-2 counts and .163/.172/.252 in 1-2 counts.

Other advanced analytics, like Bellinger's sweet-spot % (38% - 86th percentile), whiff % (20.1% - 77th percentile) and strikeout % (15.6% - 87th percentile) last year, could be indicators of how he excelled by changing his approach, based on the count and situation.

Regardless of perceptions held one way or another on his statistics, Bellinger said he shrugs them off and will continue to do as he has always done — bet on himself to succeed.

“I definitely, obviously hear it and … kind of brush it off," Bellinger said on the advanced analytics conversation around his 2023 season. "I think that there's a lot of different variables that go into it and I know not to look at one certain stat to dictate a whole season.

"So you know, I definitely trust myself as a baseball player to go out every single day and put my best product on the field … I trust that the positive results are going to come. And like I said, I believe in myself.”
