Ayo Dosunmu creates own AAU teams for Chicago kids

CHICAGO - By now, we've come to know through his All-American high school and college careers at Morgan Park and Illinois that Ayo means "joy" in the Yoruba language of his Nigerian parents.

Ever since the blessing of being a second round draft pick by his hometown team, his and his family's Ayo Dos Foundation has worked to spread joy and support to others who are less fortunate.

"That's really the goal of our foundation - to help the kids know they can accomplish anything. The sky's always the limit. They can do anything. I think with me being here - helping them, talking to them, encouraging them - it gives them an extra sense of motivation at this young age. As they continue to grow, continue to get better, that motivation starts to stack. You start to get one percent better each day.”

The foundation previously held events that helped provide shoes that properly fit students, rewarding honor roll students with free Bulls tickets and a clinic to improve data literacy and computer science in young Black students.

But. it did not include basketball until now with the formation of Ayo Dos Elite AAU teams - as a 12U team was formed in January, before more recent tryouts for a 13-14U squad.

"Growing up, AAU was always pivotal to me, for my family and my growth and development. As I was continuing to grow as a player and as a person, I always knew that once I got to that level where I could give back - that was the first thing I wanted to do.

"You see a lot of times people have AAU teams and they don't even show up. They just give a task to someone else. I don't want that to be the case. Of course, I have a great team that's important to me and they help me with my schedule during the season. But, any time I have an opportunity to come, I want to be right there for them."

The foundation is less than two years old, established in the summer of 2022 after Ayo made Second Team All-Rookie NBA. The league has already recognized his work off the court, as he was named the winner of last off-season's Bob Lanier Community Assist Award.

"It meant a lot because it just shows the work we put in. It's a great honor to be recognized for the hard work you put in for the community. That was a huge award to win. It always pushes me and motivates me to keep going, keep striving and try to touch people's lives - just being there, being a role model, being someone you can count on and look up to."

While Ayo's AAU experience started at a much younger age, playing for his dad and later the Mac Irvin Fire, it helped shape a resiliency, toughness, drive and spirit that he exhibits on and off the court, which now helps benefit any participant within the foundation's programs.

"We're here to give advice and give different opportunities with different organizations that are not the same thing as basketball.  Different clinics - mental health clinics, schoolwork, anything - we're going to try to make it very diverse where we're out here not just teaching basketball because basketball is something you can learn, but it's not everything in life as you may know."

Source: Chris Boden, WGN 9
