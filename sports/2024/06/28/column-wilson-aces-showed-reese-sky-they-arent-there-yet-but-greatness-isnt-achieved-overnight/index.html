Column: Wilson, Aces showed Reese, Sky aren't there yet, but greatness isn't achieved overnight

CHICAGO — Angel Reese and the Chicago Sky are on a meteoric rise in popularity, with Reese setting rookie double-double records, and a past champion franchise working its way back into contention, but both stood little chance against A'Ja Wilson and the reigning WNBA champions Thursday.

And that's okay. Greatness isn't achieved overnight.

Reese turned in her most dominant performance to date Sunday in a matchup that featured fellow future face of the WNBA, Caitlin Clark, and the Indiana Fever.

The Baltimore native and former Terrapin-turned-Tiger dropped 25 points and 16 rebounds on 8-12 shooting in the one-point victory, becoming the first WNBA rookie to post such numbers since A'Ja Wilson in 2018, and setting the stage for Thursday night when the Las Vegas Aces came to town.

"A rookie with only 15 games of experience? Being compared to me?" Wilson must have thought, because she showed up to that stage ready to go, like she has for years, unlike any other player in the history of the WNBA before her.

Wilson scored or assisted on 17 of the Ace's first 21 points, and had 23 of her own by halftime, extending her league record for consecutive 20-plus point regular season games to 20.

After a well-spirited counter from the Sky to open the second half that saw them reel off seven unanswered to tie the game at 46, Las Vegas put their foot back on the gas pedal, and Wilson cruised to the finish line with 31 points by the game's final buzzer, a 95-83 win for the Aces.

"We let her get away in some cases so, we can't really have that," Wilson said of matching up against Reese, after the game. "She's too good in that area for us to let her get out."

Although the Sky lost, Reese's double-double streak continued.

She posted 18 points and 11 rebounds on 7-16 shooting, the ninth straight game she's registered a double-double, which tied Chicago-legend Candace Parker for the most consecutive double-doubles in WNBA history.

"[Angel]'s up to the challenge. She's definitely up to the challenge and it's only going to make her better," said Sky head coach Teresa Weatherspoon about Reese's matchup against Wilson. "It's only going to make her better because she's willing to guard the best post player she possibly can, and it's going to make her much better down the stretch."

"Down the stretch" can be used metaphorically to describe the Sky's future in several different ways.

Right now, there's a clear gap between the Sky and the Aces. After all, in most cases that's the natural progression of things, and is also true of the gap between Reese and Wilson.

The young and inexperienced sharpen their craft through consistent repetition, until one day they are the wily veterans the next contingent of young talent try to upend.

Reese is amidst a stretch where she's tasted success (more so from an individual standpoint than a team one) in the infancy of what has been a blossoming basketball career so far, with the emotions behind her successes coming out in the interactions she's had with her teammates.

The stank face after hitting and-1 layups. The exuberant words of support for teammates to keep shooting when she's on the bench. Or even in postgame interviews, where she's talked about how she's a dog "and you can't teach that."

She's putting in the work to lay the foundation, and letting the world know about it. Such is good for the game of basketball. In fact, if she keeps it up, she may have a WNBA All-Star selection to show for it less than a month from now.

As of late Thursday morning, Reese was seventh in WNBA All-Star voting with 118,490 votes, just over 500 votes behind the New York Liberty's Sabrina Ionescu, who sat in sixth place.

Wilson, on the other hand, is a player already at the mountaintop, personified by years of experience where she meticulously shaped herself into an all-time great player.

"She's [an] MVP for a reason," Reese said after practice Wednesday. "She does what she does effortlessly. She puts in the work for it so, it's going to take a team effort, it's not going to be one person to contain her."

After her 31 point, 6 rebound performance Thursday, Wilson is averaging a league-best 28.0 points and 11.3 rebounds per game, on top of being tied for first in the WNBA in blocks per game (2.4), and ranking sixth in steals per game (1.9).

The numbers are reflective of Wilson's commitment to her craft, and the rewards she has reaped from such dedication.

Back-to-back WNBA championships and Defensive Player of the Year awards. The 2020 and 2022 WNBA MVP's. Six straight all-star game selections — All indicative of the player she's now known to be.

Yet, to give her all the credit would be disingenuous, and not paint the entire picture of what's helped enable her success.

Wilson is surrounded by a talented, battle-tested group of players, who are led by one of the game's greatest coaches — Becky Hammon.

Together, they know when to pivot and how to adjust. When to ride a wave of momentum, and other times, when to jump ship and shift their strategy to squeeze out the best possible performance from one another, and Thursday was yet another example of them doing just that.

Wilson, alongside Hammon and Jackie Young at the postgame press conference, said games like their matchup against the Sky help teach them "how to be resilient."

"That's the biggest thing when it comes to just playing," Wilson said. "We're going to get everybody's best so, it's going to be hard for us to put teams away because they're going to have that edge. They're going to want to beat the champs.

"It puts us in a role where we have to control what we can control, and just keep doing us. We can't get too high with the highs. We can't get too low with the lows. We just got to be resilient on both sides of the basketball."

While the Aces' roster and their coaching staff have found their roles and execute them, the Sky are much younger and still feeling each other out from a team perspective, working to define their roles and where they need to improve in order to get to where Las Vegas already is.

Championship status.

But it's not like the bedrock isn't already there.

Plenty of time has already been spent discussing the early successes of Reese, who's averaging 13.5 points and an Eastern Conference-best 11.1 rebounds per game, which includes a WNBA-best 4.8 offensive boards per contest.

There is also her frontcourt mate, Kamilla Cardoso, who continues to find her own footing alongside Reese and the rest of Chicago's roster.

Together, the two are currently averaging 22.4 points and 18.4 rebounds per game, which already makes them one of the best rebounding tandems in the WNBA, and serves as precedence to the heights they can reach as they continue to grow together.

On the perimeter, the Sky have a triple set of guards with a reputation for past success in Marina Mabrey, Chennedy Carter and Diamond DeShields.

Mabrey and Carter represent outside shooting and dynamic speed off the bounce as double-digit scorers, While DeShields represents veteran leadership, being the lone holdover from the Sky squad that took home the WNBA Championship in 2021.

Then there is Weatherspoon, their ever charismatic head coach.

Weatherspoon has a wealth of basketball knowledge as one of the league's foremost founding players (she won the WNBA's first two defensive player of the year awards from 1997-98) and carries a fiery passion to match.

Anyone who's taken the time to sit down and listen to Weatherspoon can attest to the heart and intensity she has when it comes to the game of basketball.

If you haven't witnessed it yet, here's a reply she gave Sunday after being asked what fuels her, even after spending nearly three decades playing and coaching in the game of basketball:

"This is a hell of a question that I have not been asked," Weatherspoon said. "And it means a lot to me, for you ask that question because when you experience something in life — This is the way I feel — When you experience something in life and as much as I've experienced in the game of basketball. A beautiful part of it for me is to give back.

“You don’t experience things to keep, you experience things to give it back, and it's emotional for me sometimes when I think about it because a group of young women, they're under my leadership, and it's my duty — And I love my duty and I love my challenge — is to make sure that they are the best version of us.

"That they live the life that they want to live within this game of basketball, but most importantly, [it’s more about] what they do off the floor. It's not all about the X's and O's for me. It's about the relationship with them, the lines that won't be crossed, those expectations.

"On those expectations, there’s a demand, and they understand that. This is the beautiful part for me, is to be able to help influence their lives to be greater, even in the game of basketball. I appreciate your question because I do deal with it a lot. Those emotions of being able to do this. I'm thankful and I'm grateful to have this opportunity. I take nothing for granted. I take absolutely nothing for granted.”

When I hear Weatherspoon speak like that, the only other coach that comes to mind with an equitable level of fire is Dan Campbell for the NFL's Detroit Lions, and one could argue Campbell and Weatherspoon's journeys may wind down similar paths.

Campbell took Detroit from a record of 3-13-1, to 9-8, and then 14-6, coming a handful of plays away from making the Super Bowl in the span of three years behind a team motto of grit, biting kneecaps and fiery speeches from the man charged with leading the Lions.

Now, I'm not saying Weatherspoon's going to step to the microphone and tell local media that kneecaps are on the menu for the Sky's evening meal, but her team's will be epitomized by descriptors similarly used to characterize Campbell's team in Detroit.

At least in one instance I can find, they already are being used to describe some of the youngest players on the Sky roster, like Reese.

"No. 1, her confidence coming in. She was really passionate and does all the little things first," Mabrey said of what she first noticed about Reese, after their June 20 win over the Dallas Wings. "So, when the scoring, rebounding, assisting come, it's natural because she's always in there. She's in the right positions.

"She keeps going and doesn't stop fighting, even in bounce back games. If in some games, they get us, Angel comes back right away and does the same exact thing the next game and builds off that."

If Weatherspoon's squad keep fighting, and players like Reese, Mabrey and Cardoso remain passionate about doing "all the little things first," who knows, maybe we'll see another banner raised at Wintrust Arena.
