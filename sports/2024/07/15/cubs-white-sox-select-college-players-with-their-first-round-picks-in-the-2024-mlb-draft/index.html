Cubs, White Sox select college players with their first-round picks in the 2024 MLB Draft

FORT WORTH, Texas (AP) — While the players selected as MLB All-Stars head to Arlington, Texas, for Tuesday night's Midsummer Classic, and the rest head to wherever they're going for the All-Star break, front offices around the league are trying to acquire players they hope will succeed in the big leagues one day.

The three-day 2024 MLB Draft is in progress through Tuesday. It's being held in Texas, in conjunction with All-Star week.

The first two rounds were held Sunday night, with Rounds 3-10 on Monday and Rounds 11-20 on Tuesday. Days 2 and 3 can be seen beginning at 1 p.m. both days on MLB.com.

Here's a look at who the Cubs and White Sox selected on Day 1:

Cubs nab college slugger

With the No. 14 overall pick in Sunday's first round, the Cubs selected Cam Smith, a right-handed slugging infielder from Florida State.

Smith was ranked as MLB Pipeline's No. 14 draft prospect. He was among the Top 100 in 2022 coming out of Palm Beach Central High School in Florida, but he was not drafted that year and went on to play two seasons with the Seminoles.

Listed at 6-foot-3, 224 pounds, Smith is on the older side for prospects at age 21, which could have turned some teams off from him. But his age also means he's likely closer to MLB-ready than other prospects and could contribute at the big-league level before too long, provided he succeeds in the Cubs' minor-league organization.

His MLB Pipeline profile notes that Smith "already looks the part with plenty of 'now-strength'" and that "how much he hits will help dictate how consistently he can get to his raw-plus power, and he's answered that question well last summer and this spring."

Indeed, the MLP Pipeline profile notes that after struggling against the high level of collegiate competition as a freshman in 2023, Smith played in the prestigious Cape Cod League that summer with other elite college players and earned the league's Most Outstanding Pro Prospect honors after finishing with a .981 OPS.

Smith is listed as a third baseman, and his prospect report cites a strong arm that should translate well to making long throws across the diamond at the professional level. The combo of a strong arm, big frame and power bat would make Smith an ideal corner infielder.

A profile by MLB.com's Jordan Bastian, posted late Sunday night, also notes that Smith went on a tour of Wrigley Field as a little kid with his grandparents and that he has relatives from Indiana that are big Cubs fans.

Smith confessed he was too young to remember that trip to the Friendly Confines, but he'll sure remember his next one.

"It's going to be a treat, man," Smith told Bastian. "I assume we're going to get emotional again."

The Cubs also had a selection in the second round Sunday and took infielder Cole Mathis at No. 54 overall out of the College of Charleston. Mathis was ranked as the No. 84 draft prospect by MLB Pipeline.

By drafting a 21-year-old college player in the first round and a 20-year-old college player in the second, the Cubs continued their recent trend of taking older players early in the draft.

MLB.com notes that 11 of the Cubs' last 12 first-round picks have been college players. Their last four, including Smith now, have come from college, with infielder Matt Shaw out of Maryland in 2023, right-handed pitcher Cade Horton out of Oklahoma in 2022 and left-handed pitcher Jordan Wicks out of Kansas State in 2021.

Horton and Smith are now the organization's top two prospects, respectively, per MLB Pipeline and may not be far from big-league call-ups, while Wicks has already made it to the majors.

By taking another experienced college player in Smith on Sunday, the Cubs are likely seeking another first-round fast-riser through their system.

According to MLB.com, the slot value of the No. 14 pick, which the Cubs used on Smith, is set at just over $5.07 million, while the slot value of the No. 54 pick, which the Cubs used on Mathis, is set at just over $1.6 million.

White Sox take power lefty

With the No. 5 overall pick in Sunday's first round, the White Sox also selected a college player, left-handed pitcher Hagen Smith out of Arkansas by way of Bullard, Texas.

Smith, 20, was also the No. 5-ranked draft prospect by MLB Pipeline. He's listed at 6-foot-3, 225 pounds, and his MLB Pipeline prospect profile is quite intriguing.

The profile says, "(Smith) has better stuff than any southpaw in this draft and could be the first pitcher selected after finding more consistency this spring." He was indeed the first pitcher-only player taken in the draft, though two-say standout Jac Caglianone of Florida went No. 3 overall.

Smith's profile notes that he blew out his elbow as a high school sophomore and had Tommy John surgery but bounced back to tie a Texas prep record with seven no-hitters in 2021.

According to the profile, Smith pitches from a three-quarters crossfire delivery that makes his pitches difficult to pick up. He features a mid- to-upper 90s fastball that touches 100 and has riding action due to his unusual delivery, plus a mid-80s slider that he can speed up into a cutter and "destroys lefties," along with an upper-80s splitter as a change-of-pace pitch.

A story by MLB.com's Scott Merkin says that despite being just 20 years old still, Smith could be big-league ready as soon as next season.

He also gives the White Sox another piece to what could become a formidable pitching rotation in the near future, though that all depends on who the team — mired in one of the worst seasons in MLB history — may decide to trade before the July 30 deadline, or in the offseason.

The White Sox are getting a huge breakout season from lefty Garrett Crochet, who they drafted No. 11 overall in 2020 and was named an All-Star for the first time this season while currently leading MLB with 150 strikeouts.

Other pitchers high in the White Sox system are lefty Noah Schultz, the team's No. 2 prospect per MLB Pipeline and currently at Double-A, along with righty Drew Thorpe (No. 3) and righty Nick Nastrini (No. 8), who have both reached the majors.

Now, the selection of Smith adds another power arm to the mix, as the White Sox try to stock their organization in rebuilding the big-league roster. Smith hopes to be on that big-league roster before too long.

"I feel like I was one of the better pitchers in the draft going into it," he told MLB.com via Zoom on Sunday night.

"... You never know what's going to happen, what the organization wants me to do. But I definitely feel like I can do it in the near future."

According to MLB.com, the slot value of the No. 5 overall pick is just over $7.7 million.

In Sunday's second round, meanwhile, the White Sox selected prep infielder Caleb Bonemer, ranked as the No. 71 draft prospect by MLB Pipeline, at No. 43 overall from Okemos High School in Michigan.

Aussie goes No. 1 overall

In other MLB Draft News, Cleveland selected Australian second baseman Travis Bazzana on Sunday night with the No. 1 overall pick, and three players from Wake Forest were picked in the top 10.

A former cricket, rugby and soccer player who came to the United States to play baseball for Oregon State, the 21-year-old Bazzana hit .407 with 28 homers and 66 RBI this season. He became the first Australian and first second baseman taken No. 1 overall.

“An opportunity to make an impact on a lot of baseball players and a lot of people back home in Australia, and hopefully change the narrative for baseball there,” Bazzana said.

Bazzana hit .360 over three seasons at Oregon State, with 45 homers, 165 RBI, 180 walks and 66 steals.

Baseball's No. 1 selection this year has a slot value of $10,570,600 under the bonus pools system that began in 2012. Cleveland had the top pick for the first time since the draft began in 1965.

Just 10 high school players were among the 30 first-round picks.

Wake Forest right-hander Chase Burns went second to Cincinnati, while Demon Deacons first baseman Nick Kurtz was the fourth pick for Oakland and third baseman/outfielder Seaver King 10th for Washington. They became the eighth trio of teammates selected in the first round, the first among the top 10 since Rice right-handers Philip Humber, Jeff Niemann and Wade Townsend in 2004.

Humber authored a perfect game for the White Sox on April 21, 2012, one of 24 perfect games in MLB's Modern Era.
