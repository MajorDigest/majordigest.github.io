Caleb Williams and everything you need to know about the Chicago Bears rookie QB

CHICAGO — He paints his nails and rocks skirts at fashion shoots, but also slings footballs, loves his bulldog named "Supa," and is an avid Call of Duty player like many guys his age.

He is No. 1 overall pick Caleb Williams, the rookie starting quarterback and future of the Chicago Bears, and now that he's officially come to an agreement on a rookie deal with the team, this is everything you need to know about him.

Caleb Williams, The Football Player

As many already know — and if you didn’t already — Williams has been preparing to become the quarterback of an NFL team (that turned out to be the Bears) since he was in fifth grade.

A Sept. 2023 profile by GQ detailed the inception behind his decision to become a quarterback when he was just ten years old, and the meticulous process he underwent to usher that dream into reality.

Titled "The New King of College Football," Williams told GQ sportswriter Sam Schube his football career began with him being used as a two-way player — A bristling linebacker on defense and a bowling ball running back on offense — Complete with a passionate hatred of losing.

One rain-soaked evening following a loss with 10-year-old Williams on the bench, his disdain for defeat reached a headway. For hours afterward, he and his father devised a plan where Williams would become his team's quarterback, because that was the best way to avoid such a thing in the future.

And so, the master plan was set in motion.

Hot yoga and media training that started by high school. Summers spent crisscrossing the United States to work with the country’s best quarterback coaches. Temperature-controlled naps and nutritionists to guide eating habits. The whole nine yards and more was established as routine for Williams.

All those years of intensive preparation led him to the University of Oklahoma as the No. 1 quarterback prospect in the country coming out of high school.

A 66-yard touchdown run on the first play of his freshman year against the Sooner’s bitter rival, the Texas Longhorns, ignited his stardom, and soon after his head coach Lincoln Riley departed for the University of Southern California, Williams followed suit and found his way to the West Coast, where he went on to win a Heisman trophy after his sophomore season.

A year later in 2023, Williams saw a slight downtick in production as his Trojans regressed from 10-3 to 8-5, but he still added even more eye-popping plays to his highlight reel and did what most expected — Declare for the NFL Draft.

In two years at USC, Williams completed 599 of 688 passes (67.5%) for 8,170 yards, 72 touchdowns and 10 interceptions, to go with 210 carries for 524 yards (2.5 YPC) and 21 touchdowns on the ground.

This included his Heisman trophy campaign, where he completed 333 of 500 passes (66.6%) for 4,537 yards, 42 touchdowns and five interceptions, and ran the ball 113 times for 382 yards (3.8 YPC) and 10 touchdowns in 2022.

According to NFLDraftBuzz.com, Williams was their highest rated quarterback prospect in this year's draft cycle (93.5/100), and ranked ahead of past contemporaries like C.J. Stroud (92.0), Justin Fields (92.1) and Zach Wilson (91.6), but was also behind the likes of Trevor Lawrence (95.0) and Bryce Young (93.6).

Once it became clear Williams was headed to the NFL, comparisons started to pour in, with the most polarizing being to pro football's top dog at the quarterback position — Patrick Mahomes.

Kliff Kingsbury — Williams’ quarterbacks coach at USC, and Mahomes’ offensive coordinator at Texas Tech — Did nothing to squash the hype when he said it’s “eerie how similar” the two quarterbacks are, back in early February.

Several weeks later in his opening press conference at the 2024 NFL combine, Bears general manager Ryan Poles acknowledged the comparisons being made between Mahomes and Williams.

“There’s pieces that are similar. Obviously the one that stands out to everyone is just different arm angles. That’s a unique trait, not a lot of guys can do that,” Poles said on seeing the comparisons of his future QB to the NFL's best. “I’ll give Jeff King, he’s on my [scouting] team, credit. He painted a picture — there’s two types of quarterbacks. There’s artists and then there’s surgeons.

“Within that group, you can kind of see who’s the artist that’s really creative and doesn’t draw within the lines. Where there’s more of surgeons, like your typical [Tom] Brady’s and Peyton [Manning's].”

Around the same time, football statistician Kyle Morris and DaBearsBlog writer Robert Schmitz took a deep dive into the advanced analytic, time-to-throw (TTT).

Justin Fields, the Bears' most recent starting quarterback, held onto the ball, on average, longer than any other NFL quarterback (3.39 seconds) in 2023, who hit a minimum of 50% of 823 dropbacks.

Critics of Fields used that statistic to point toward a narrative he held onto the ball too long and struggled to progress through his reads, leading to sacks and turnovers more often than the jaw-dropping plays he was capable of putting on tape.

Then, when football fans, tv analysts and journalists alike all discovered Williams also had a tendency to hold onto the ball for an extended period of time, a panic lingered over a number of them.

According to Morris, Pro Football Focus showed Williams had an average TTT almost a full second longer than almost any other college quarterback on scrambles (6.58 seconds in 2023), and rarely did those scrambles actually result in him running the football.

Essentially, Williams is fiercely determined to use his legs to create as much time as possible to make a play with his arm, which according to Morris, makes him more comparable to Aaron Rodgers than Mahomes.

Williams — like Rodgers — possesses an elite ability to process plays quickly, but that’s also paired with a rogue-ish arrogance that leads to him holding onto the ball longer than most quarterbacks, which often results in those six-plus second, backbreaking highlight throws that have been so commonplace from Rodgers over the years, with a sprinkle of sacks and fumbles mixed in along the way.

Morris then found Rodgers’ most comparable season to 2023 Caleb Williams and the numbers backed up his comparison. In 2016, Rodgers had a 4.3% scramble rate from a clean pocket (Williams: 3.0%), 8.7% pressured scramble rate (Williams: 12.4%), a 5.7% overall scramble rate (Williams: 6.2%), and an average scramble time of 6.44 (Williams: 6.58) to go with a pressure to sack rate of 17.9% (Williams: 19.4%)

For those curious what Rodgers' counting stats were that year, he completed 401 of 610 passes for 4,428 yards, a league-leading 40 touchdown passes and 7 interceptions — Numbers a Bears quarterback has never come close to producing.

***Caleb Williams 40-yard dash time was taken from NFLDraftBuzz.com’s database and has yet to be compared to his 40-yard dash time at USC’s pro day, should Williams chose to run the exercise then.

Caleb Williams, The Person

Williams is a departure from the personality characteristics and personal stylings of Chicago's last starting signal caller, Justin Fields.

Where Fields was reserved and even-keeled to the point he seemed blanketed in an aura of emotionless monotony at times, Williams tends to wear his heart on his sleeve and often rides a wave of emotion.

In one moment, he'll portray charisma and confidence while holding up a bear claw to Chicago sports faithful, and in the next, he could display a youthful level of emotional vulnerability that's sure to short-circuit football old-heads.

Williams' sense of fashion is also a strong departure from that of Fields (and the lineage of Bears quarterbacks, in general).

In that same 2023 GQ profile, the Washington D.C.-native can be seen rocking a variety of designer fashion and accessory items, including a red Jil Sander skirt, banana yellow Gucci jumpsuit, and a see-through, perforated mesh tank top from New BedStuy.

Such is commonplace for Williams, who could very well follow suit in the extra-curricular pursuits of another renowned Chicago rookie athlete — Angel Reese — Who, at one point, went from basketball practice with the Chicago Sky, to appearing on the red carpet at the Met Gala in New York hours later.

Bears fans likely hope his on-field performance mirrors the success Reese has found on the court too, where present day, she is competing head-to-head with Caitlin Clark for WNBA Rookie of the Year as both sides jostle for playoff position.

Almost as loud as his fashion sense, is his preference to paint his nails on game day.

"It's a part of my routine during the week," Williams said of painting his nails during a 2023 video interview with GQ Sports on ten things he couldn't live without. "There's a lot of noise during the week. There's a lot of talking trash and when I go to the nail salon, I can throw my beats on, and I can sit down and put my head back and kind of just let the nail tech go at it."

Polarizing fashion choices and vibrantly-painted nails aside, there are still characteristics about Williams that endear him to both the average American and football fan too.

Among those ten things Williams said he couldn't live without, he was quick to mention his dog — A bulldog named "Supa," who he said should grow to be around 65 pounds and is a lovable ball of wrinkly skin who tends to drag his big, messy paws around the house.

Williams also mentioned a custom football that calls back to a 17-14 victory over Oregon State in 2022. A phrase and two distinct hashtags are written on the ball, with the phrase reading "Two-minute drive game winner," and the hashtags "#FightOn" and "#NeilonNudge."

Brett Neilon, who the #NeilonNudge refers to, was Williams' center during his 2022 Heisman Trophy campaign.

"This dude means the world to me," Williams said of Neilon during the GQ Sports interview.

Williams went on to describe an affinity for sour candy and sneaker culture, pulling out a bag of Trolli sour octopus gummies and saying he's "not a box guy. Shoes are meant to be worn."

In his free time, Williams also said he's an avid video gamer — It's a source of escapism akin to how fans often view watching football.

"[It's] one of the most precious things in my life," Williams said, holding up what appeared to be a PS5 controller. "It's something that gets me out of everything. You know, you put on your headphones, you get in the game, your name's not you.

"You just get out of everyday life. You can go lay down, relax your body, but also [there's] hand-eye coordination, teamwork, communication. I don't play any single player games, I like team games. We can go win and whoop up on some people. I play Call of Duty every day."
