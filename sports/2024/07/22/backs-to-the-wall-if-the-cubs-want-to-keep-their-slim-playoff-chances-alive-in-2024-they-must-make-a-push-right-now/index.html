Backs to the wall: If the Cubs want to keep their slim playoff chances alive in 2024, they must make a push right now

The Chicago Cubs did not start their post-All-Star break schedule on a high note, so their playoff hopes have not improved after heading into the break winning five of seven on the road.

With 61 regular-season games left entering Monday — and with the July 30 MLB trade deadline looming next week — it's probably safe to say it's now-or-never territory for the Cubs' 2024 postseason hopes.

The Cubs at least salvaged a game Sunday after losing the first two of their three-game series with the Arizona Diamondbacks at Wrigley Field coming out of the All-Star break.

Now 48-53, the Cubs continue their six-game homestand with a three-game set against the Milwaukee Brewers — who've led the National League Central virtually the entire season — Monday through Wednesday.

Nobody can say for certain what the Cubs front office plans to do — or not do — before the trade deadline next week Tuesday at 5 p.m. But if they don't take at least two of three games from the Brewers this week, it's hard to imagine them being in any type of buyer position.

Here's a look at where things stand for the North Siders:

Playoff picture

There's a good news-bad news component when assessing the Cubs' position in the NL playoff picture.

First, the bad.

It's hard to imagine the Cubs having any chance to win the NL Central now. They begin this week 10 games behind the first-place Brewers, so even a sweep would only get them within seven.

Now, there are plenty of examples in MLB history of a team going on a late tear and making up a big deficit on a division leader. It's not impossible.

But the problem for the Cubs is that not only are they 10 games behind the Brewers, the St. Louis Cardinals and Pittsburgh Pirates are also ahead of the Cubs in the division standings, and the Cincinnati Reds are just a half-game behind.

So even if Milwaukee falters enough for the Cubs to make up significant ground, it's hard to imagine everyone else in the division faltering, too.

The same predicament applies to the NL Wild Card standings.

The good news is that with three wild cards now in the playoff field from each league, far more teams can say they're in the postseason race late into the season. Even if you're languishing under .500, all it takes is something like a five-game winning streak to jump right back into playoff position.

All you need to do is look back at last season, when the Diamondbacks did just enough to finish a game ahead of the Cubs for the third NL Wild Card spot with 84 wins. Arizona then capitalized on that opportunity to win the NL pennant and go all the way to the World Series.

The Cubs enter play Monday 3.5 games behind the New York Mets, Diamondbacks and San Diego Padres for the third and final NL Wild Card spot, a deficit that's more than manageable with a full two months-plus left to play.

But, again, the bad news is how many other teams are ahead of or near the Cubs.

The Atlanta Braves and Cardinals hold down the top two NL Wild Card spots, respectively, entering Monday. Then, after the Mets, Diamondbacks and Padres, the Pirates and San Francisco Giants are both ahead of the Cubs in the NL standings, with the Reds and Washington Nationals only a half-game back.

Actually, rather than looking at who the Cubs are behind in the NL standings right now, it's a much shorter list to see who they're ahead of.

You can consider the Cubs, Reds and Nationals in a virtual three-way tie, since they each have 53 losses entering Monday. That means the Cubs are currently ahead of only the woeful Colorado Rockies and Miami Marlins in the NL.

A good way to think of it is this comparison: A golfer entering the final round of a tournament five shots behind the leader but alone in second place will tell you there's still a chance — albeit small — to make up the deficit and win.

But a golfer entering the final round of a tournament five shots behind the leader but tied for, say, seventh place will tell you there's about no chance to win.

Quite simply, even if the Cubs get hot, it's certain one or more of the NL teams also in playoff contention will get hot, too.

Playoff odds

Now, the difference between the Cubs and the golfer in that comparison is the Cubs don't have to "win" anything in the regular-season. They just have to finish with one of the top three records in the NL among non-division winners.

So if you want to quantify the Cubs' slim playoff odds with numbers, here are some, entering play Monday:

  • FanGraphs: 2.0% to win division, 6.4% to clinch wild card, 8.4% to make playoffs.
  • Baseball Reference: 0.5% to win division, 8.8% to clinch wild card, 9.3% to make playoffs.
  • ESPN: 3.7% to win division, 13.9% to make playoffs.

You obviously don't have to be a mathematician to see that the Cubs' playoff chances aren't great. But they're certainly not zero.

Remaining schedule

If Cubs fans are looking for one positive to cling to, here you go.

According to at least one metric, that of Power Rankings Guru, the Cubs had the easiest schedule remaining of all 30 MLB teams after the All-Star break.

However, there's a caveat to that, too.

Much of the "easy" part of the Cubs' schedule, in theory, is back-loaded. Including the series they just played against the Diamondbacks, six of the Cubs' eight series coming out of the break are against teams who are over .500 entering Monday.

In addition to the Diamondbacks and Brewers at home, the Cubs also have to play the Royals in Kansas City for three games, the Cardinals at home for four, the Twins at home for three and the Guardians in Cleveland for three, all prior to Aug. 15.

So the Cubs can't afford to simply tread water or run in place until the supposed "soft" part of their remaining schedule arrives in mid-August.

Starting this week against the first-place Brewers, the Cubs have to start winning at a high clip, or it'll be too late.
