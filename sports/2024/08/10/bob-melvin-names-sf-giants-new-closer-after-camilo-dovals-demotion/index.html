Bob Melvin names SF Giants’ new closer after Camilo Doval’s demotion

SAN FRANCISCO — A tied ballgame heading to the top of the ninth is typically when the home team decides to deploy its closer.

Facing these circumstances Friday night, the score even at 2 in their first game against the Tigers, Giants manager Bob Melvin didn’t have a closer to call on.

After Camilo Doval was optioned to Triple-A Sacramento before the game, Melvin tapped Tyler Rogers for the highest-leverage situation of their eventual 3-2 win. The submarining set-up man recorded a 1-2-3 inning, but afterward the manager named a different reliever when asked about ninth-inning duties going forward.

Ryan Walker will serve as the Giants’ closer in Doval’s absence, Melvin said, but the crossfiring right-hander was unavailable after pitching in their previous two games.

“It makes it a little more seamless,” Melvin said of the decision to go with Walker over other options such as Jordan Hicks or the Rogers brothers, all of whom have closing experience. “Hicks will move up a little bit. Everybody will move up a little bit behind Tyler, who will pitch the eighth. We’re trying to keep the roles, and then the lefties can kind of do their thing.”

Melvin had Walker in mind when the Giants made the decision to demote Doval before Friday’s game but wasn’t prepared to share publicly until after he had a chance to inform him of his decision. Upon hearing the news, Melvin said, “He just looked at me. He’s happy. A little smile. It takes a lot to get a smile out of him.”

Walker, a 31st-round draft pick in 2018, said closing is “something I never imagined would come,” but reiterated he was ready for the role.

“I don’t think anybody’s going to say no to that,” Walker said. “I hope not. … You get the pressure of having to close out the games, which I like. It’s very high-leverage. Getting saves is always fun. To see that in your stats. It’s something I’ve always wanted. I’m just excited for that leverage position.”

Appearing in a major-league high 59 games, Walker has posted a 2.24 ERA while striking out 30.5% of the batters he has faced and walking only 5.6%. While he wasn’t on many radars when he debuted last May at 27 years old, the pressure cooker of the closer’s role shouldn’t be too foreign.

He can also lean on the advice of Hicks, who closed in St. Louis and Toronto, and Taylor Rogers, who did the role in Minnesota, Milwaukee and San Diego.

“I’ve learned a lot from the Rogers (brothers),” Walker said. “They’ve both been in high-leverage situations. Tay’s been a closer. I talk to them a lot about differences and whatever. Almost every time, it’s don’t change anything. Do what you do. It’s another inning. Same hitters. Just do your thing.”

In addition to his 21 holds this season and his lone career save that he notched last August in Philadelphia, Walker has an average leverage index of 1.32 (an average situation is 1.0) this season compared to 0.90 as a rookie last year. Entering play, only Doval (2.03) and Tyler Rogers (1.35) had higher average leverage indexes among Giants relievers.

“Walk certainly deserves it,” Melvin said. “He’s been really good. We don’t want to get Tyler out of the eighth. He’s been so good in the eighth. Our bullpen’s been really good at times — the back end — we don’t want to throw it into flux and everybody’s in different roles. The hard part is that (Walker) has been that fireman who comes in with guys on base and oftentimes pitches out of a jam and then the next inning as well.

“But he gets lefties out, he gets righties out, he’s got swing and miss stuff.”

Doval blew his fifth save of the season Thursday against the Nationals and was optioned to Sacramento before Friday’s game.

With a career-high walk rate, Melvin said he hopes Doval, an All-Star in 2023, can harness his command and return to the closer’s role in short order.

“It just needs to be a cleaner game with him. Our expectation – and our best team – is with him closing,” Melvin said before the game. “This affords him the opportunity to go down there, do some of those things and get back here and be the All-Star that he was last year.”

Not to be overlooked: What happens to Doval’s entrance? He was initially hesitant to make a spectacle out of his walk in from the bullpen but was overridden and it has since evolved into light show with with water spewing from the fountains beyond right field.

The best piece of advice Walker said he got from his fellow relievers in adapting to the closer’s role, he said, was “don’t change anything,” and he plans to do just that with his entrance music, “Neon Grave,” a rock/country track by Tyler Braden. Whatever happens with the lights is out of his control.

“I’m not a very ‘Look at me’ kind of guy,” Walker said. “It doesn’t matter to me either way. If that’s what the stadium wants and what the people want, I guess we can keep it. I don’t know if I would choose it, you know what I mean? Let’s get some innings in first and see where it goes.”
