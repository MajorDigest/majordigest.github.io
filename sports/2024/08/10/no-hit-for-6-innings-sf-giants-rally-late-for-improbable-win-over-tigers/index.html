No-hit for 6 innings, SF Giants rally late for improbable win over Tigers

SAN FRANCISCO — On a night where Robbie Ray didn’t have his best fastball or his best command, when their touch-and-go offense was held hitless for more than half the game, the Giants still managed to pull out the unlikeliest of victories Friday to begin a crucial home stand.

Ray turned in his longest outing yet since returning from Tommy John surgery and was still out of the game by the time his run support arrived, but the Giants’ bats woke up just in time to walk off the Tigers, 3-2, by way of Mark Canha’s ninth-inning sacrifice fly and move two games over .500 for the first time since the end of May.

It’s wins like these that will be required if the Giants (60-58) hope to do more than keep pace in the National League wild card race. They risked falling further back of playoff position after the Padres, Diamondbacks and Cardinals all won earlier in the day but instead gained a game on the Braves and Mets, who both lost.

“Obviously getting no-hit that deep into a game is not ideal,” said Ray, who limited the Tigers to two runs over six innings, “but once we got the first hit I think guys just kind of relaxed and we were able to put some runs on the board.”

Unable to come up with a hit against the Tigers’ (55-62) opener, Beau Brieske, or their recently demoted starter, Kenta Maeda, for the game’s first six innings, the Giants put the leadoff man on base and drove him home in each of the final three innings to earn their 12th win when tied or trailing after eight innings.

An 83.5 mph single off the end of Tyler Fitzgerald’s bat snuck through the middle of the infield to break up the combined no-hit bid to begin the seventh, and Canha delivered the walk-off winner with sacrifice fly to deep left field in the bottom of the ninth after the first three batters of the inning reached on an error, a walk and a hit batsman.

“It felt a little low-energy early in the game, but I guess when you get no-hit, it feels like that regardless,” manager Bob Melvin said. “But, like we do here at home, we put together some really good at-bats later on the game.”

It seemed like a minor miracle that Ray recorded a quality start while battling his command and traffic on the base paths all but one time he toed the rubber. But that effort, combined with three scoreless frames from Landen Roupp and Tyler Rogers, set up the late heroics that seem to be becoming routine for this team.

After Fitzgerald snuck his single up the middle, he advanced to third on an even softer hit from Heliot Ramos — 70.5 mph — and scored on a sacrifice fly from Michael Conforto. They used the same formula the next two innings, getting a leadoff hit from Canha and a sac fly from Patrick Bailey in the eighth and benefitting from shortstop Javier Báez’s misplay that allowed Ramos to reach base to lead off the ninth against Jason Foley, who put the next two men on to load the bases for Canha.

With the Michael Conforto representing the winning run on third base, Canha said he was trying to “just make contact, put the ball in play somewhere.”

After recording four hits in their 5½-hour, extra-innings win in Washington on Thursday, including the go-ahead knock in the ninth inning, Canha’s timely contributions were even more satisfying back in the ballpark where he grew up attending games as a student at San Jose’s Bellarmine Prep and later Cal-Berkeley.

“It was a really special moment for me, just awesome,” Canha said. “I couldn’t have asked for more in my first day at home. … I kind of got goosebumps when we got in (from Washington, D.C.). We got in late and all the lights were off and it was just dark. I was like, ‘Wow, I’ve never seen it like this before.’

“I hold this stadium near and dear to my heart. I’m proud to be a part of all this.”

Following two rain delays, a blown save and an inning of extras, the Giants didn’t arrive back at Oracle Park from their cross-country flight until almost midnight. (Team personnel were told they got in the air out of Washington with 2 minutes to spare before the approaching tropical storm shut down the runway for the night.)

Forgive them for being a bit bleary-eyed to begin this one.

“It didn’t feel like there was a ton of energy in our dugout today,” Melvin said. “The longer it goes on, the more maybe you start to press a little bit. But once we got the first hit and then all of a sudden it’s first and third. … A lot of good things happened late. We did not want to go to extra innings.”

Ray walked three batters, allowed five hits and didn’t hit 95 mph once but walked off the mound after recording the final out of the sixth inning with a couple personal accomplishments in his back pocket, completing six innings and exceeding 100 pitches for the first time since undergoing major elbow surgery.

“Getting through the sixth inning, for me, was huge,” Ray said. “My fastball (and) curveball were really good today. Slider felt great. I think it was just a steady mix of all my pitches. I felt great. I didn’t have any fatigue afterward. I still felt strong in that last inning, so it was definitely encouraging.”

Ray was one out away from getting out of the sixth when something got the attention of head trainer Dave Groeschner. He made his way to the mound with Melvin and pitching coach Bryan Price, but Ray waved them away and got Ryan Vilade to fly out on his 105th pitch of the evening.

“I’m still trying to figure this mound out,” Ray said, explaining that the brief concern was only one of a handful of times he lost his footing. “Every mound is different. Going from Cincinnati, where the mound was a little more flat, to come here where it’s a little more of a slope, I’ve got to be able to make those adjustments a little bit quicker.”

Despite his gritty effort, Ray handed a 2-0 deficit to the Giants’ bullpen, which was operating without an official closer after Camilo Doval was optioned to Triple-A before the game. Without a save situation, Tyler Rogers took down the highest-leverage outs of the evening with a 1-2-3 ninth inning and was credited with the win.

After the game, Melvin announced that Ryan Walker would handle closing duties in the interim, though he was unavailable after appearing in the Giants’ past two games and four of their previous five.

Up next

The Tigers plan to throw another bullpen game Saturday against RHP Logan Webb (9-8, 3.42). First pitch is scheduled for 1:05 p.m. PT, but the Giants are advising fans take their seats by 12 p.m. for their 10th anniversary celebration of their 2014 World Series championship.
