SF Giants bats wake up late, provide Snell just enough support to split final Bay Bridge series

OAKLAND — The Giants had every reason to play desperate Sunday.

Their playoff hopes are dwindling. Their schedule only gets tougher. Their offense isn’t just running on fumes; it’s out of them. But they had Blake Snell on the mound, in top form again, and knew if they could push across just one run, it might be enough.

It took more than that — and an extra inning — but the Giants got just enough from their feeble bats to earn a split in the final Bay Bridge series.

The very first pitch out of Dany Jiménez’s right hand to begin the 10th inning found a landing spot over the Xfinity sign in right-center field and gave the Giants their first lead in a 4-2 win in the last time they will ever play the Oakland Athletics with their cross-bay rivals moving to Sacramento after this season.

“Don’t you kind of expect a game like this to end this place? It had to happen like that,” said Giants skipper Bob Melvin, who has been a part of as many games here as anybody. “So many close games with the fans going crazy. Both sides going crazy. I don’t know how it could have been more perfect to end it like that.”

Jerar Encarnacion jumped on the letter-high fastball and, with Matt Chapman as the automatic runner at second base, provided the Giants’ first hit with a runner in scoring position all weekend. Turns out one way to overcome their inability to drive home baserunners is to circumvent them entirely.

With a solo shot from the next batter, Michael Conforto, and a game-tying blast from Heliot Ramos in the seventh, all four of the Giants’ runs came via the long ball.

After seeing Encarnacion’s clear the wall, Melvin said his first feeling was “relief more than anything.”

“We’re not doing much offensively. We were trying to manufacture a run, score a run however we could. It didn’t look good for a while. But then a couple big swings that we had been missing and talking about here recently ended up winning the game for us. And they didn’t go quietly.”

After Ryan Walker recorded a 1-2-3 ninth inning to take the game to extras, Melvin asked his new closer to protect their newfound three-run lead in a second inning of work.

Walker allowed Oakland’s automatic runner to score and loaded the bases, putting the potential winning run on first with nobody out, but recovered by striking out the next three batters to end the game, the second time a Giants pitcher had to work his way out of a late bases-loaded jam.

“I didn’t expect (to go out for a second inning), but I wasn’t opposed to it,” said Walker, who earned the win but not a save. “When the bases got juiced, I honestly felt more calm than having guys on first and second — because you don’t have to worry about timing — so once that happened I wasn’t worried. I just focused on making my pitches.”

Ramos provided a near-immediate answer to the only run on Snell’s pitching line, slugging a 448-foot solo shot of the top of the batter’s eye to lead off the top of the seventh. The Giants had struggled to do anything else, putting six other men on base and running into four outs, against A’s starter JP Sears, who tossed seven shutout innings in a win last month at Oracle Park.

“Oh, my God,” Melvin remarked when asked about Ramos’ home run. “I had just made the comment, ‘How many innings is this guy going to shut us out in a row?’ And just as I make that comment, he hits the homer. I was trying to save that one for another time, but it was (huge). It just didn’t feel like anything we were going to do was potentially going to score a run. And then all of a sudden he hits a homer and it’s a completely different feeling.”

After dominating for six innings, Snell pitched himself into and out of trouble in the seventh and walked off the mound after his 109th pitch with a ferocious yell. He allowed a one-out single, then issued his first two walks of the afternoon to load the bases. He buried a curveball past Max Schuemann for his 10th strikeout but backed himself into a 3-0 count to the next batter, Daz Cameron.

One more pitch outside the strike zone would have forced in a run — and A’s manager Mark Kotsay was ejected arguing correctly that the first strike of the at-bat was above the letters — but Snell fired three straight fastballs, the last of which Cameron rolled to second base to end the inning, leaving the bases loaded.

“I got 3-0 against Daz, and I was like, ‘If I’m losing, that’s because you’re getting a fastball,'” Snell said. “That was kind of my approach and mentality. Let’s give him my best three fastballs and see what he can do.”

Snell allowed one run when the A’s strung together three singles in the sixth, opening a 1-0 lead, but completed his seventh straight quality start and lowered his ERA to 3.67. When he came off the injured list July 9, he owned a 5.79 ERA but has since limited opponents to six earned runs over 52⅓ innings with 70 strikeouts.

“Everything’s starting to come together,” Snell said. “I’m pitching well, locating the fastball. I feel good.”

Sears matched Snell and then some, striking out nine and stranding or erasing all six runners who reached base against him.

The Giants struck out 15 times in total and didn’t draw a walk for only the second time this season.

They had one runner thrown out trying to stretch a single into a double. They ran into two more outs trying to reach second on a steal. Another runner, through no fault of his own, was doubled off first when it looked like the A’s infield had lost a pop fly in the blue skies overhead.

All in desperate attempts just to advance a runner into scoring position.

When they finally got one to third base, on Casey Schmitt’s poke through the right side that allowed Chapman to make it 90 feet from home plate with one out in the fifth, they were so desperate not to waste the opportunity that they didn’t even ask Mike Yastrzemski to swing the bat.

Yastrzemski squared to bunt and popped it up into the glove of catcher Shea Langliers. He slammed his pink Crayola themed bat into the ground. Chapman was stranded after Patrick Bailey flew out to end the inning, another hitless showing dropping his batting average since the All-Star break to .115 (11-for-96).

As a team, the Giants had scored 21 runs over their previous eight games — averaging 2.6 — while batting .199. Until Encarnacion came through in the 10th, they hadn’t recorded a single hit this weekend in 11 chances with runners in scoring position, dropping their average in those situations since July 30 to .159 (24-for-151).

They couldn’t afford to waste baserunners or give away outs, but neither could they sit back and wait for a big hit that might not arrive.

“We’re just trying to push it and score a run,” Melvin said.

Notable

At the conclusion of the 56-year history of the Bay Bridge series, the Giants finished with a record of 72-76 — 32-42 in Oakland — while splitting the four games this season.

Chapman made good on his quest to collect the third-base bag from his final game at the Coliseum. He requested it, and the A’s had it authenticated after the game.

Up next

The Giants host the lowly White Sox for three games beginning Monday in one of the few light spots on their remaining schedule. Besides a three-game series against the Marlins next month, all of the other opponents left on the Giants’ schedule own winning records.
