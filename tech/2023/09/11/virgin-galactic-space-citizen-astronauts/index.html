Behind Virgin Galactic’s Disney-meets-NASA experience for citizen astronauts

Anastatia Mayers couldn’t stop smiling. Rocking nose rings and a cascade of purple box braids, the teenager high-fived her way along a line of 200 cheering guests and press before throwing her hands up with an exuberant “Yeah!” She then turned and hugged her 46-year-old mother, Keisha Schahaff. Steps behind, limping slightly from Parkinson’s disease, waved Jon Goodwin, an 80-year-old British Olympian and adventurer, who had waited 18 years for this moment.

The trio emerged in black flight suits from the soaring atrium of Virgin Galactic’s “Gateway to Space,” the undulating metal and glass terminal at the Spaceport America housing the space tourism firm founded by Sir Richard Branson in 2004. Beyond them, the New Mexican desert stretched toward the horizon, bathed in the golden glow and calm of an early August morning.

From there, luxury SUVs drove them to a six-seater spaceplane that would rocket them at 2,300 mph to the edge of space for three minutes of weightlessness, with a view stretching 1,000 miles in every direction highlighting the curvature of the Earth and her thin blue line of atmosphere. Ninety minutes later, they were back on land as newly minted astronauts.

“That was the most amazing thing I’ve ever done—a childhood dream has come true,” gushed Schahaff, a health and wellness coach from Antigua and Barbuda, before cheekily adding, “And if anyone was wondering, the Earth is round.”

“You stole my line!” laughed Mayers with mock petulance. When Schahaff won two seats through a 2021 Virgin Galactic fundraising raffle for the non-profit Space for Humanity, her daughter, now an 18-year-old physics and philosophy student at the University of Aberdeen in Scotland, insisted on being her plus one. Now she was at a loss for words. “The only thought I had the entire time was, `Wow.’”

Goodwin, a canoeist in the 1972 Munich Olympics, was more quietly overcome. Back in 2005, he’d dropped a then-bargain $250,000 for his seat before Virgin’s extensive delays competed against his advancing age and weakening health.

“Nine years ago, when I contracted the disease, I thought, `That’s the end of me going into space,” he said during a post-flight press conference. But Virgin “never stopped me doing what I wanted to do. I’m hoping I instill in other people with Parkinson’s that it doesn’t stop you from doing things.”

The emotional heart

Since gaining FAA approval for commercial launches in May, the success of this summer’s first commercial flights officially opened the Tustin, California-headquartered Virgin Galactic for business, with monthly jaunts that began September 8. Ahead lies an 800-person, 60-nationality waiting list of aspiring citizen astronauts who’ve shelled out $200,000 to $450,000 for tourist and $600,000 for research flights. Its first paid mission in June, the science flight Galactic 01, had three members of the Italian Air Force and National Research Council of Italy conducting 13 microgravity experiments. The August tourist flight Galactic 02 was more on brand with Virgin’s vision of broadening access to space.

“This flight is the heart, the emotional purpose of Virgin Galactic,” CEO Michael Colglazier tells Fast Company. “Space has been restricted, inaccessible for almost ever, and, as a company, we’re working to change that. We’re trying to widen the voices that have a perspective of the planet. They’re going to end up as ambassadors for communities they represent.”

Indeed, Galactic 02 made notable inroads in representation—among them, the first Olympian and second person with Parkinson’s to go into space; first Caribbean and mother-daughter astronauts; the youngest person in space; and the most women in a single space mission. Its imprint was palpable: A giant watch party in Antigua hosted by its prime minister Gaston Browne and Branson drew thousands of attendees. Medical non-profits seized the opportunity to highlight their Parkinson’s research. A flurry of press spouted inspirational messages to pursue dreams and STEM careers.

“Anna’s got purple hair, she’s a young woman of color, 18 years old—there has never been someone who looks like her who has gone to space,” says Sirisha Bandla, VP of research operations and government affairs.

Bandla experienced the astronaut effect firsthand. An Indian-born aeronautical engineer, Bandla found herself in Christian Dior splashed across the pages of Vogue India after she flew to space alongside Branson in 2021. “I had messages from young women in India who said, `Hey, just you being out there opened my eyes that there are ways to get beyond the village I live in, but never forget where I’m coming from.’ Representation does so much.”

Not your parents’ rocketship

In the terminal’s cavernous hangar, Virgin’s flying machine projects a sleek futuristic presence nearly three decades in the making. Branson licensed and evolved the technology from the 2004 winner of the $10 million Ansari XPrize, a competition announced eight years earlier for a reusable, privately financed, and crewed suborbital spaceship.

It comprises two vehicles: the massive Virgin Mothership (VMS) Eve, a two-fuselage, four-engine airplane with a 140-foot wingspan. The second is the 60-foot, twin-tailed Virgin Spaceship (VSS) Unity, with a 42-foot wingspan and 17 viewing windows, that attaches to the underside of Eve’s wing between fuselages. The mothership flies Unity to an altitude of 45,000 to 50,000 feet and releases it to a 1000-foot drop before Unity’s hybrid rocket engines kick in and thrust her into a minute-long vertical ride to space at Mach 3, three times the speed of sound. At that stage, passengers experience 4 g’s, four times the force of the Earth’s gravity.

“Just a couple of aircraft in history have gone faster than this,” says chief pilot Dave MacKay as he “flies” a group of reporters in the Unity simulator, a mock cockpit with wraparound projections depicting real-time flight views. As MacKay hits the engine, the sky shifts from horizontal to vertical in the side windows, moving rapidly from blue to dark blue to black with a sprinkling of stars; the edge of the Earth increasingly curves as a thin blue line of atmosphere emerges. MacKay idles the engine. “And now, ladies and gentlemen, welcome to space,” he says. “You may unstrap.”

A silent three minutes of weightlessness ensues as the craft crests upside down along a parabolic loop. We’re now 50 miles above sea level—NASA’s demarcation of space—as opposed to the internationally recognized Kármán line space boundary at 62 miles, which would require more fuel and expense to reach without the comparative payoff in views. The Earth is now a convex land mass at the top of the window, hinting at the overview effect, the existential overwhelm astronauts often feel observing the Earth from above.

“The atmosphere is the same relative thickness as an apple skin,” says MacKay, a former Air Force test pilot who has flown to space three times with Virgin. “So, imagine what’s keeping everything on Earth alive is that thin band around the planet. It looks very fragile, and the blackness of space makes you realize how far we are from anywhere else in the solar system, let alone in the universe.”

At apogee, MacKay “feathers” the craft by raising back sections of the twin tails 60 degrees to slow Unity’s reentry and orient her belly first to disseminate the friction heat. Passengers would experience the silence of space giving way to the dull roar of the atmosphere hitting and heating up the exterior. MacKay slowly lowers the tails to stabilize what would normally be a 15-minute glide back to the Spaceport. For us, the abrupt end of the projection jolts us immediately back to Earth.

A long road to the beginning

The vehicle’s innovative design belies its long, tumultuous road to this point. Initially, Branson sought to begin flights in 2006, only to face massive delays from regulatory, logistical, and technical hurdles that included two fatal accidents, relocating flight operations from California’s Mojave Air and Space Port to Spaceport America near Truth or Consequences, New Mexico. Then came the pandemic and a 2021 FAA probe after a test flight carrying Branson veered off-course. Meanwhile, its satellite launch services subsidiary, Virgin Orbit, went bankrupt earlier this year.

Despite its $980 million in cash and investments, Virgin still faces years of losses pouring capital back into its business to develop and expand its future fleet enough to turn a profit—a status that’s impeded stock prices, which have declined since Virgin went public in 2019. “At the projected burn rate, Virgin Galactic is a bit shy of two years’ worth of cash,” notes The Motley Fool. “Investors should expect to see even more stock sales in the future.” Virgin’s third quarter financial report is slated for November 7.

However, Colglazier maintains the business plan is on track. “This is the beginning of an industry,” he says. “What we have are the early flights. We’re past prototypes, but these are the first ships out and the infrastructure hasn’t been built to scale. We’re going to have to make these investments now to get the returns.”

Virgin is rolling out its next-generation craft that will be cheaper, faster, and easier to build and maintain. Two new motherships in 2025 will evolve Eve’s design and each support up to 200 annual launches. Four $50 to $60 million “Delta” class spaceships will begin operating in 2026, carrying six passengers (plus two pilots) instead of Unity’s four. The Deltas will look nearly identical to Unity but be lighter and faster, with improved control systems and thermal protection. Virgin’s Phoenix factory will continue to produce four to six Delta ships a year, with an eye toward weekly flights. At that flight rate, the company projects each Delta to pay for itself in about six months and be reusable for 500 to 1000 flights.

Eventually, Colglazier expects the Deltas to earn $2.7 million per flight, a quarter of which will go toward operating expenses and the rest back into the company. But that’s based on six passengers paying $450,000 each. Virgin first needs to get through the 600 aspiring astronauts who paid early bird rates of $250,000 or less. That could take 150 to 200 flights depending on whether chief astronaut instructor Beth Moses or another trainer will continue to occupy one of the seats, as she did during initial flights.

Merging safety with novel design

The space tourism industry is so new that regulators are scrambling to catch up on consumer protections and liability. The FAA only this summer established a committee to formally outline spaceflight occupant safety measures. Because Virgin’s hybrid vehicle flies in both air and space, it needed licenses from both FAA air and space divisions.

Given aviation’s 100-year history, aircraft manufacturing and operations guidelines for the mothership are more straightforward. “There is a regime within that manufacturing side for experimental aircraft, which are not certified, but you’re still licensed by the FAA,” says president Mike Moses, a former NASA space shuttle program manager who oversees Virgin’s safety and operations.

The FAA’s Commercial Space Transportation division, which approves commercial rocket launch operations, can’t apply the same standardized approach due to spaceship design disparity and lack of data. “However, I can’t just do what I want,” says Moses.

“The FAA doesn’t tell us how to make our spaceship,” he adds. “We make the spaceship, and then we have to show them how it performs. They have to review and approve it. Even if they don’t have authority over me, I still have to talk to them, present it all to them, and they can say, `That doesn’t look right; you might wanna look at that.’ ” The FAA is also involved in Virgin’s mission control operations.

Virgin leaders cite this as the main distinction from the Oceangate disaster that killed its CEO Stockton Rush and four other passengers after Rush flouted manufacturing standards and ignored industry warnings with his submersible design. The proximity of the June tragedy to Virgin’s summer flights in tandem with their unconventional vehicles has invited questions of comparisons, which Colglazier has called “apples and oranges” given the FAA’s involvement.

Virgin additionally benefits from its cadre of pilots, who bring experimental test pilot experience from NASA, the military, and private aviation sectors. Pilots learn how to fly both mothership and spaceship, so they can trade off. The Delta design team has also asked them for input.

“What makes our pilot course so strong is everybody has that experimental test pilot background because these vehicles are so unique,” says Galactic 02 pilot Kelley Latimer, a former Air Force and Boeing test pilot, shortly after her maiden voyage to space. “So, we have different perspectives, not so much on actually flying the vehicles, but more on how you approach testing and operating them. As a matter of fact, we just had a big design review for Delta last week.”

New flying laboratory

Although research excursions comprise just 10 percent of flights, Virgin’s expanded weightlessness presents opportunities for experiments that might otherwise have to queue up for more expensive orbital flights. Virgin flights comparatively offer three minutes of continuous microgravity instead of 20 seconds per parabola on a ZeroG weightlessness-simulation flight or five seconds in a drop tower. And they’re more frequent than manned orbital launches.

“This is a capability that wasn’t accessible before,” says Bandla. “No one could fly to space and then fly again in the next month. It’s that kind of turnaround where scientists can now build campaigns and iterate on their work using a space environment. Instead of handing off instructions to an astronaut, now researchers can go to space with their payloads, and make real-time observations and adjustments.”

Experiments have included fluid control during space surgery, dust particle behavior in microgravity, and upper-atmospheric data collection. The microgravity tiers also complement one another. Galactic 01’s Col. Walter Villadei used his flight for both research and training for an upcoming Axiom Space flight to the International Space Station. The recurrent flights could enable more testing of the atmosphere’s outermost and least-studied layer, dubbed the “ignorosphere,” which plays a role in space weather that can affect satellites.

“There’s this misconception that everyone’s research is so we can go farther out to space,” she adds. “But a majority perform science to be invested back on Earth to create better lives for people here.”

Reducing emissions

Ironically, given the climate crisis, emissions from space tourism undermine that purpose. To that end, Virgin is trying to reduce its carbon footprint through reusable vehicles, carbon offsets, greener fuel, and a LEED Gold-Certified terminal.

Surprisingly, the mothership is the bigger culprit, producing 80 percent of a flight’s emissions—equivalent to a round trip between New York to Heathrow in business class (which takes up more cabin space and therefore fuel). The new motherships will accommodate sustainable aviation fuel—pricier bio and synthetic propellants with lower carbon footprints.

“We’ve offset all of our space flights to date and are looking at where we can do carbon offsets in the future,” says Moses. “At the moment, we’re going through a thorough assessment of our whole footprint.”

Into the future

Ultimately, Virgin envisions a global network of spaceports offering daily flights, assorted Earth views, local community involvement, and partnerships with governments, universities, and foundations like Space for Humanity that could facilitate passengers and researchers who can’t afford tickets.

“It’s not just a spaceport and launches, but an investment into the area,” says Bandla. “So, when we go to another country, it’s not going to be a repeat of what we do here. We’re bringing in the culture of the people and the landscape and making it a unique experience that then transforms into that view of that country from space.”

Meanwhile, the vehicle has future implications for parabolic flight routes through space that might connect far-flung locations in a fraction of the time—an observation Colglazier is not yet ready to address.

“Our job is to get a solid economic engine for suborbital space flights, using both research and private astronauts, that will give us the foundation to grow into additional businesses,” he says.

An experience, not a ride

At present, Virgin is focused on an extended consumer experience that, while peaking in space, begins with a $150,000 deposit (of which $25,000 is refundable) and continues well beyond your flight. That ongoing immersion comes naturally given the Disney backgrounds of Virgin brass. Colglazier helmed Disney Parks International before arriving at Virgin in 2020, while James Willoughby, VP and GM of customer relations since last year, had been a Disney Cruise Line senior executive. Both borrow from its world-building ethos. Think astronaut-lite; a Disney-meets-NASA “taste of space” that grants participants entrée into a community of science enthusiasts and adventurers and imbues them with a sense of wonder and Earth-stewardship.

“It’s so much more than just buying a ticket to fly to space. We’re unifying people,” says Willoughby, who once managed Disneyland’s ultra-exclusive Club 33. “You could actually think about this as being their own private membership club.”

That “membership” grants you access to Virgin’s hangar, pilots, and aerospace engineers to learn more about the technology, as well as curated space education conferences with industry speakers and orbital astronauts. There are also related events like group ZeroG flights. But foremost is the connection with a like-minded society.

“The best part of this whole journey is the people you meet,” says David Mendal, a Miami-based commercial pilot and luxury travel entrepreneur attending the Galactic 02 launch. “There’s an immediate bonding; they have the same synergies and energies to do this adventure that not many want to do.”

Slated to fly in December as the first Colombian-born astronaut, Mendal has attended every test and paid launch since signing up 17 years ago. His early adopter status even earned him a visit to Branson’s Caribbean hideaway, Necker Island.

Mendal hasn’t minded the wait. “I didn’t want to be number one or two,” he laughs. “I feel a lot more confident that I want to do this more than I did before. This is really well done. The engineering behind it, they’ve got it down to a science. That’s what makes me feel more comfortable.”

In keeping with this spirit, the three days of flight week training offer a flourish of pageantry alongside confidence and team building. The entrance greets passengers with a two-story “U”-shaped metal and glass sculpture, Genesis, heralds the dawn of a new space age. A short ride reveals the rolling terminal entrance, curved winglike walls that flank a ramp leading to a seemingly doorless façade. In a scene out of a James Bond movie, a section slides open to reveal a stark white hallway with thin purple LED light filaments. At the far end is a pedestal with a book opened to a short list of Virgin Galactic astronaut signatures. In a few days, the new astronauts will add their names.

“Everybody we fly to space is registered as a commercial astronaut and everything we do is about creating a journey that culminates in your space flight experience and brings you to a new perspective of the planet,” says Colglazier. “You’ll find, from my prior job, that everything here is about creating a journey of experience that’s transformative, not a ride.”

The hallway leads to a sleek blue and white atrium on the building’s third floor with expansive desert views. The focal point is a gathering area for briefings from pilots and Virgin astronauts, with a life-size spaceship cabin replica and small personal quarters for each passenger off to the sides. A large window looks down onto the tarmac where they will take their “hero walk” to the spaceship in a few days. For now, two acrobatic planes await to take them on parabolic flights that simulate up to five seconds of weightlessness and 4 gs in preparation for the real deal.

“It’s enough to get your body acquainted with what may or may not happen,” says Moses. “Our docs can prescribe meds that’ll help with antinausea.”

One floor down is the 15-person Mission Control, which will serve as the nerve center for the upcoming flight. The ground floor houses the hangar and airy two-story chamber that will fill with press, guests, and buffet spreads on launch day.

But the third floor is sacred ground: a haven open only to astronauts in training and Virgin personnel managing their flight expectations and ways to impart their newfound spacefaring enthusiasm upon return.

“Once you do fly, you are an ambassador of Virgin Galactic,” says Willoughby, “We don’t see you ever really leaving us.”
