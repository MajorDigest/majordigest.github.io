Review: Epomaker TH80 Pro is a great mechanical keyboard for newcomers to the hobby

Newcomers to the mechanical keyboard hobby need a place to start. One doesn’t just jump in and assemble a $400 keyboard with full-blown modifications. It’s better to pick a budget keyboard, one that lets the novices experiment with switches, keycaps and features while coming in at an affordable price.

When it comes to a beginner-level device, newbies can’t go wrong with the Epomaker TH80 Pro. It’s a 75% mechanical gaming keyboard that has three ways to connect to a PC or Mac. Users have Bluetooth, a USB dongle and a wired option. Because it caters to gamers, users will find a healthy dose of RGB lighting, but it also boasts other bells and whistles that will improve the work-from-home experience.

KEYCAP AND SWITCH COMBO
It’s a great all-around keyboard for the office or a gaming battle station. Epomaker sent over a white version for review that featured its PBT keycaps with an MDA profile and Budgerigar tactile switches. That combination had mixed results. The PBT keycaps are sturdy and thick offering a heavy-typing experience. With its round edges and tops that are sculpted to the fingertips, they felt bulky. Meanwhile, the legends were printed on and clear though I didn’t like the font.

On the other hand, the Budgerigar tactile switches were a revelation. They have a slot to allow the RGB to shine through, making the keyboard lighting more vibrant, but the best part of the experience was the feel. Outfitted with a two-stage spring, the switch feels unbelievably snappy. After pressing a key, it surges back up ready for another actuation.

Typing on the TH80 Pro felt like I had Pop Rocks on my fingertips. It was effervescent. The experience was bolstered by the solid feel of the stabilizers for the Enter, Space Bar, Shift and Back Space keys. They were heavily lubricated and didn’t have rattle.

When it comes to gaming, the switches feel responsive to the touch. In terms of performance, it won’t do anything like the SteelSeries OmniPoint switches that offer analog functionality and faster response times, but the feel of the switch is arguably better. It’s great for those who pound on their keyboards. If the Budgerigar switches aren’t appealing, users can replace them because the TH80 Pro is hot swappable.

FIXING AN ANNOYANCE WITH THE KEYBOARD
The combination of the two created a hefty typing experience and it did create some problems, in which the space bar and the 8 key would end up stuck. I tried lubricating the switches to stop the issue, but ultimately, I ended up solving it by adding a lighter set of keycaps.

Interestingly enough, the thinner SteelSeries Prismcaps work wonderfully with the Budgerigar switches. These pudding-style keycaps feel flimsy despite being created with two layers of plastic meshed together, but the slightness of the material made the TH80 Pro keyboard feel poppier. The frosted sides of the keycaps also showed off more of the RGB lighting.

Being a 75% keyboard, the TH80 Pro lacks a number pad and it also eschews other dedicated keys for Insert, Home and others. That creates a compact form that won’t crowd a desk. The device has a Delete key and Page Up and Page Down keys, which helps when scrolling pages. If a user does need those other buttons, they are available via Function key. Players have to press down on that and another key to access the End or PrtSc keys.

FIGURING OUT THE CUSTOMIZATION
The Function key also also controls media on the screen and other shortcuts. Users can even choose from 18 RGB styles or colors for the south-facing lighting layout. The Function key is also how users switch among three paired Bluetooth devices. That means they can use the TH80 Pro on a Mac, then move to an iPhone before finding that it also works with a PC. That’s all fine and good, but I appreciated the battery check quick key the most. It clearly showed how much battery was left via the lighting on the numbers.

For those who want more control over the keyboard, the device does support Epomaker’s proprietary software. It’s a clumsy program, but it does let users customize the lighting. The fine-tuning extends to the knob. Normally, it’s an easy way to control the volume or even mute a computer.

Because the TH80 Pro is in the budget category, users shouldn’t expect the best build quality. The device is made of strong but unremarkable plastic. Despite that, it does have a few flourishes such as adjustable feet to change the typing angle. It features a small amount of foam to improve the sound, which isn’t that great. It even has a nice spot on the bottom to store the USB dongle when not in use.

As for the battery life, the mechanical keyboard has a 4,000 mAh battery, according to the manual. It can go days without a charge if the RGB isn’t on, but if your keyboard looks like Christmas came early, don’t expect the battery to last longer than a day.

Epomaker TH80

Three stars
Manufacturer Epomaker
Price: $99.99
