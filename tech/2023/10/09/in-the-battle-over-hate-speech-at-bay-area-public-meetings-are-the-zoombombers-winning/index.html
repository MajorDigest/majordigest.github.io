In the battle over hate speech at Bay Area public meetings, are the Zoombombers winning?

When Zoom meeting attendees start raising virtual “hands” to speak during Walnut Creek City Council meetings, administrators have few options but to sit in mortified silence as extremists and other online trolls spew hate speech, usually under the cover of anonymity.

These “Zoombombing” incidents are on the rise, and officials around the Bay Area are throwing up their hands in frustration — with many unplugging from remote comments altogether.

Last week, Walnut Creek became the latest city to announce that council and commission meetings will no longer be accessible via Zoom — joining a growing list of local governments that are able to legally make the change after California’s COVID-era emergency orders expired. Anti-hate groups are even considering proposing changes to California’s hallowed Brown Act, which guarantees public access to civic meetings, to try to deal with the problem.

Elected officials in Antioch, El Cerrito, Fremont, Modesto, Sacramento, Santa Rosa and Sonoma County have also decided to cut off virtual public comment.

While video conferencing platforms helped keep public meetings accessible during the pandemic, the technology has also allowed anonymous bigots and trolls to interject racist, anti-Semitic and homophobic messages into civic proceedings — even from thousands of miles away.

In Walnut Creek, Zoom comments will be phased out by Oct. 16. Community members will still be allowed to share their views in person at City Council and Commission meetings, or submit comments by email, phone call or snail mail. Meetings will still be available to watch through the city’s website, YouTube streams and cable television.

Walnut Creek City Councilmember Kevin Wilk, who has been personally targeted by anti-Semitic comments spewed through the council chambers’ speakers, said he supports the city’s decision.

“I can’t stress enough to how disappointing and unfortunate it is that we even have to be having these conversations,” Wilk said, adding that he recently joined more than 100 representatives from the California League of Cities in brainstorming ways to combat Zoombombing and other forms of hate speech. “(Zoom and other online platforms) are great tools of communication that we didn’t have 20 years ago. But unfortunately, we also have a segment of the population that will use that for hateful or nefarious means. All it takes is a few people to ruin it for everybody.”

Betsy Burkhart, Walnut Creek’s communications manager, acknowledged that some people may feel public comments are more impactful when spoken directly to elected officials, even if written input technically carries the same weight. However, she said one of the biggest motivations for the change was reclaiming decorum since hateful comments can have a chilling effect on a city’s ability to govern effectively.

“What we’re trying to do is make sure that a council meeting is safe for all of the people that are there to do their business or have issues about what the city is doing,” Burkhart said. “That’s really what the focus is; it’s not just a forum, it’s an actual business meeting.”

Farther south in Santa Clara County, the Morgan Hill City Council was struck this week by four public commenters who read excerpts from a neo-Nazi text about ethnic and ideological cleansing, in addition to spouting anti-Semitic, racist and homophobic rhetoric.

But they are taking a different tack with these incidents than officials in Walnut Creek.

“No matter how awful those grievances may be, or how reprehensible their comments are, they have the right to make them,” City Attorney Donald Larkin said between speakers. “But Morgan Hill (and) our City Council has constantly affirmed this city as an inclusive community, and one that welcomes people of all faiths and beliefs. The comments that we’re hearing do not reflect the views of the city of Morgan Hill.”

The City Council and staff deliberated over the limitations of free speech between the comments, denouncing many of the statements as they came. After the public comment period, Morgan Hill Mayor Mark Turner began his typical thanks to public commenters, then paused and said, “We appreciate you calling in. … Well actually, we don’t appreciate you calling in, I’ll just be honest with you. I think it’s disgusting.”

Though they may appear unconnected, incidents like the ones in Walnut Creek and Morgan Hill are part of a wider, coordinated effort that has taken place across California and the United States, according to August 2023 reporting by the Anti-Defamation League’s Center on Extremism. The efforts largely appear to be led by anti-Semitic and White supremacist groups, who organize attacks using social media and chat rooms, the report says.

Marc Levine, the ADL’s Regional Director for Northern California, said these groups have organized to use public comment periods, especially over Zoom, to take advantage of the First Amendment and California’s open meeting laws and spread their message to a captive audience.

After the public embraced virtual meetings as a convenient way to engage with local government during the pandemic, Levine said hateful antagonists are now “trying to take that space away.”

He said the ADL is currently working to draft legal strategies that could protect free speech while shielding public officials from hate speech. In particular, they are eyeing amendments to California’s Brown Act and Bagley-Keene Act, which together guarantee the right to attend and participate in public meetings of local government and public agencies.

In the meantime, these new procedural changes have sparked some questions about whether public access will be reduced too much, especially for people who are disabled or face other physical and social barriers to attending meetings in person.

Kathi Pugh, a member of Berkeley’s Commission on Disability who is actively pushing for more flexible remote participation options, said Zoom introduced dramatically simpler options for community members to connect with their elected representatives, especially because many people find it easier to ramble on the phone or in front of a computer than write cohesive letters for submission. Additionally, she praised the accessibility that comes with the ability to stream a Zoom meeting from a computer or cell phone, especially when discussions are captioned with live transcripts.

Pugh was pleased to hear that options to share comments via phone, email or snail mail will continue. But she said she hopes that city councils, commissions and boards will be open-minded about potential accessibility concerns.

“It’s really a shame when some people’s speech or behavior cuts (access) off for so many other people,” Pugh said. “That does worry me, but I can sympathize, because I think they (local leaders) are in a tough place.”
