Government Cracks Down on Hidden Fees and Unwanted Subscriptions

WASHINGTON — A new initiative by the U.S. federal government aims to make it simpler for consumers to click the unsubscribe button for unwanted services and memberships.

The effort, dubbed “Time Is Money,” involves a rollout of new regulations across various industries, including healthcare, fitness, and media subscriptions.

Cracking Down on Hidden Fees

The administration is focusing on practices that waste people's time and money, such as holding onto payments for extended periods. Neera Tanden, White House domestic policy adviser, stated that companies often make it difficult for consumers to cancel services, resulting in significant financial consequences.

New Regulations Ahead

A new Federal Communications Commission inquiry will examine whether to impose requirements on communications companies to make it as easy to cancel a subscription or service as it was to sign up. The Federal Trade Commission has also initiated “click to cancel” rulemaking, requiring companies to let customers end subscriptions as easily as they started them.

Healthcare and Insurance

The departments of Labor and Health and Human Services are asking health insurance companies and group health plans to improve customer interactions with their health coverage. This initiative aims to identify additional opportunities to enhance consumers' experiences in the healthcare system.

Additional Government Initiatives

The government has already launched several initiatives aimed at improving the consumer experience. The Federal Trade Commission announced a proposed rule to ban hidden and bogus junk fees, which can mask the total cost of concert tickets, hotel rooms, and utility bills. The Transportation Department finalized rules requiring airlines to automatically issue cash refunds for delayed flights and better disclose fees for baggage or reservation cancellations.

Industry Reactions

Some business advocates have expressed concerns about the government's efforts to crack down on junk fees. Sean Heather, senior vice president of international regulatory affairs and antitrust at the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, stated that the initiative is an attempt to micromanage businesses' pricing structures, often undermining their ability to give consumers options at different price points.

As the government continues to roll out new regulations and initiatives, it remains to be seen how these efforts will impact consumer experiences and industry practices. One thing is certain: the administration's focus on protecting consumer interests is driving significant changes in various sectors.
