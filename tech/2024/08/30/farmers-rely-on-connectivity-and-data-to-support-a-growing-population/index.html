Farming in the Digital Age: How Connectivity Impacts Agriculture

The world's food supply is facing unprecedented challenges as the global population surges towards 10 billion by 2050. Farmers, who feed around 166 people per year through their operations, are working against numerous challenges such as labor shortages, weather variability, and limited time windows to complete tasks.

One of these challenges is connectivity, or rather the lack thereof in rural areas where many farms are located. However, a stable internet connection can be the difference between success and failure for farmers who rely on technology to monitor their work and plan operations.

Harnessing Data on Farms

Data is used in two key ways on farms: analyzing past seasons to inform future decisions, and using real-time data to manage day-to-day operations. The average farmer plants and harvests a crop around 40 times during their career, giving them the opportunity to refine their techniques based on previous results.

By leveraging digital databases accessible through mobile applications, farmers can now get more insights from their data by comparing jobs, years, and input data based on weather conditions. This digitization enables farmers to make informed decisions that impact their bottom line and increase productivity year-over-year.

For example, during harvest season, yield data provides crucial information about the farmer's past decisions, such as how much money their business will earn. Farmers can analyze different combinations of fields, seeds, soils, and fertilizers, using this data to inform their plans for the next season.

Real-Time Data Collection: A Game-Changer

Farms are complex operations with many moving parts during planting and harvest seasons. Managing these operations efficiently is made possible by farmers knowing where every piece of equipment is, thanks to real-time data collection.

This technology allows farmers to remotely control their machines, making adjustments as needed without needing technicians on-site. For instance, a farmer working in a rural area can use remote diagnostic technology to troubleshoot issues with their machine, saving time and money.

Additionally, connected machines can communicate with each other, enabling seamless coordination during tasks like spraying. This ensures that work isn't duplicated, time is not wasted, and excess product is not sprayed.

The Importance of Connectivity

Connectivity is crucial for farmers to reap the benefits of data and remote management. It's essential for their businesses and ultimately, for all of us who rely on them to provide food, fuel, and fiber for our growing global population.

Farmers need support in providing the technology and connectivity they require to meet these demands. By doing so, we can ensure that farmers have what they need to continue providing for us as the world's population grows.
