TikTok's Bittersweet Journey: From Ban to Blessing

Summary

The story of TikTok's tumultuous journey in the United States, from being on the brink of a nationwide ban to becoming a prized possession for former President Donald Trump, is a fascinating tale of politics, power, and public opinion. Despite the executive order signed by Trump, the app has managed to stay alive, with ByteDance challenging PAFACA in court.

Introduction

In a stunning turn of events, former President Donald Trump has transformed from being a fierce critic of TikTok to its unlikely savior. Just four years ago, Trump was at the forefront of efforts to ban the popular social media app, citing national security concerns and advocating for its sale or shutdown within the United States. What sparked this drastic change in his stance on TikTok?

Trump, the Attempted TikTok Killer

In August 2020, Trump signed an executive order mandating the sale or shutdown of Bytedance-owned TikTok and Tencent-owned WeChat within 45 days of his signature. The move was part of a broader effort to address national security concerns related to Chinese-owned apps. However, the fate of TikTok hung in the balance as ByteDance explored potential buyers and court battles delayed the ban.

The executive order sparked a series of high-stakes negotiations between ByteDance and potential buyers, including Microsoft and Oracle. Despite the efforts, a full sale never occurred, and TikTok continued to operate within the United States. Trump's stance on TikTok was unwavering, with him stating that the US would not be an M&A country.

1. The Rise of PAFACA

In 2022, Congress passed the Protecting Americans from Foreign Adversary Controlled Applications Act (PAFACA), which had many of the same principles as Trump's executive order: ByteDance had to sell TikTok by 2025, or the app would face a countrywide shutdown. Meanwhile, Trump began to see the value in courting Gen Z men, many of whom are avid users of TikTok.

2. Trump's Shift on TikTok

As PAFACA moved towards becoming law, Trump changed his tune entirely. Appearing on CNBC's Squawk Box in March, Trump argued that a TikTok ban would give Facebook too much power. In a September Truth Social video, he told voters: "For all of those who want to save TikTok in America, vote for Trump. The other side’s closing it up, but I’m now a big star on TikTok."

3. The Current Status of TikTok

Under PAFACA, ByteDance needs to divest TikTok by January 19, 2025, in order for the app to continue its American operations. Some key players have stepped up to consider an acquisition, including billionaire Frank McCourt and former treasury secretary Steve Mnuchin. However, ByteDance has challenged PAFACA in court, with the US Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit planning to hear arguments on September 16.

4. Public Sentiment on a TikTok Ban

A national TikTok ban remains unpopular, with only 32% of American adults supporting it, according to Pew Research Center. Many believe that a ban is unlikely, with 50% of adults thinking so, compared to only 31% who believe it's likely.

Conclusion

The story of TikTok's journey in the US is a complex one, marked by shifting stances from politicians and the challenges faced by ByteDance. As the deadline for divesting TikTok looms closer, one thing is certain – the future of this popular social media app will be shaped by politics, public opinion, and the determination of its stakeholders.
