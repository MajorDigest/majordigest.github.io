The most innovative defense tech companies of 2026

Silicon Valley was seeded with giant military contracts, but it’s taken three decades for the tech sector and the Pentagon to really rekindle their complicated love affair. Today, defense tech is blowing up. The $2.7 trillion global industry is being propelled by a hype-fueled, hypersonic flywheel: multiple wars and growing geopolitical threats, which also drive budget increases. (The Trump administration wants to bump up the Pentagon’s to a record $1.5 trillion.) Procurement reform at the Department of Defense—or War—has accelerated deployment of new tech. Successes, along with the more visceral threats of the Russian and Chinese military, attract tech talent previously hesitant to work in defense. Rising budgets draw more capital and entrepreneurs to the sector, even those who previously vowed not to touch it.

No wonder the value of venture capital deals hit a record $49.1 billion last year, up from $27.2 billion a year earlier, according to data compiled by PitchBook. That includes companies that provide dual-use technology to both civilian and defense markets. Equity funding for defense tech more than doubled to $17.9 billion, according to CB Insights, outpacing overall equity funding, which rose 47% to $469.3 billion on the back of an AI frenzy. 

Most of the defense deals are going toward autonomous flying drones, which Ukraine has proved are now essential for deterrence and simply survival: surveillance, strikes, and shooting down other drones. See the Dutch interceptor maker Destinus, which was founded by a Russian emigré, or Anduril, the granddaddy of the new prime contractors, which is gearing up to build what could be one of the Pentagon’s first self-flying fighters. Boston-based Merlin, which is now raising money from the public markets, aims to bring autonomy to almost any conventional plane. But the drone deluge shouldn’t distract from other frontiers. A number of companies are using AI to push the boundaries of technology into harder domains, from patrolling space (LeoLabs and Slingshot) to sending drones out to sea for weeks at a time (Saronic, Vatn, and HavocAI). Less visible but no less essential are the cyber assurances of Shift5; the hardened navigation of Xona and Astranis; and the augmented reality of Red6, which brings flight sims to the skies.

As they grapple with technical and bureaucratic challenges, these startups need to prove to investors—and the war department—that they can turn their flood of cash into actual production at scale. (They’ll also need to balance their own principles with the demands of an administration that calls for “maximum” engagement authorities, and branded Anthropic a national security chain risk for refusing to budge on a contractual dispute.) The buildup won’t be easy: The country’s current manufacturing processes for shipbuilding, munitions, and almost everything else are decades-old and rely on slow, unsustainable techniques and concentrated supply chains that can’t surge to meet demand. By innovating production itself, with robotics, software, modular design, and vertical integration, many of these startups hope to disprove the skeptics and fortify not only national security but a critically fragile industrial base.

1. Saronic

For reviving a Louisiana shipyard to build autonomous ships of the future

In just four years, Saronic has transformed into a defense tech leviathan aimed at closing a critical strategic gap: While China dominates over half of global shipbuilding, and can build a destroyer in three years, U.S. destroyers currently take seven years to construct. Saronic is making software for autonomous vessels and swiftly building the boats itself, starting as small as the six-foot Spyglass and up to the Marauder, an 180-foot autonomous ship designed for battle.

Founded by Dino Mavrookas—a former Navy SEAL Team Six operative with a Wharton MBA—and following the high-risk, software-first playbook of SpaceX and Anduril, Saronic doesn’t wait for government contracts to innovate. The results are disruptive. Backed by a $600 million Series C that quadrupled its valuation in 2025, Saronic bought a 60-year-old shipyard in Franklin, Louisiana, where workers completed the first Marauder in about nine months—the fastest American ship construction since World War II. The Navy has taken notice: in December it awarded Saronic its biggest deal yet: a $392 million production contract for the 24-foot Corsair, a mid-size vessel that costs under $2 million and moved from prototype to production in less than a year. Now the company is looking for a site for Port Alpha, a giant, next-generation shipyard that it says could be twice as large as Newport News Shipbuilding, the country’s biggest yard.

Read more about Saronic, No. 36 on Fast Company’s list of the World’s 50 Most Innovative Companies of 2026.

2. Anduril Industries

For loudly and proudly laying the groundwork for defense tech’s AI era

Anduril has blown apart the sluggish, custom, cost-plus approach of defense manufacturers, turning billions in venture capital into a vertically-integrated stream of off-the-shelf drones, boats, and AI software. That’s the narrative at least, and a good story has always been part of the brand, thanks to brash founder Palmer Luckey, the Oculus inventor who turned his dismissal by Meta in 2017 (in part over his support for then-candidate Donald Trump) into the ultimate revenge. Starting with AI-enabled cameras for Trump’s border wall and millions from Facebook’s first investor Peter Thiel, Anduril followed Palantir’s lead to build a new category of “neoprimes,” VC-funded startups competing to upgrade the Pentagon’s hardware and software. In the process, Luckey became the Hawaiian-shirted front man for a louder, more militarized Silicon Valley.

Since he launched it, just months after his exit from the Metaverse, Luckey’s company has steadily racked up contracts (and acquisitions) in pursuit of the same goal as the newly rechristened Department of War and its allies: fielding lots of low-cost drones and munitions with the help of rapid manufacturing and AI software. Profits may be far off, but investors are piling in: This month, the Wall Street Journal reported Anduril was raising another $4 billion from a16z, Thrive Capital, Lux Capital, Founders Fund, and others. That would value it at $60 billion, double what investors said it was worth in June.

Amid all the buzz, skeptics say the company’s move fast and break things approach hasn’t demonstrated the scale and reliability needed for combat. But Luckey and Anduril are moving quickly to disprove them. Last year, it successfully flight-tested its autonomous fighter-style drone, Fury, and announced an Air Force contract to start building at its new megafactory in Ohio. Thanks to a $99 billion contract for its Lattice command-and-control platform and a decade-long $642 million deal with the Marine Corps, Anduril’s also helping the U.S. take drones out, too. (While it’s not officially operating in Ukraine, it claims to be helping take down Iran’s Shahed drones as part of Operation Epic Fury.) The Army now has so many contracts with the company that it recently announced it would bundle them together under a deal valued at as much as $20 billion.

Perhaps Anduril’s most symbolic coup lately was winning the contract for the Army’s new augmented reality helmet. The $159 million deal marked the demise of a years-long effort by Microsoft, and a truce of sorts—or a reversal of fortune. To help build the displays, Luckey brought on a partner that’s already spent billions of dollars on his own headset technology: Meta.

3. Destinus

For building Europe’s next generation of drone offense and defense

Last year, Ukraine’s president Volodymyr Zelensky announced a new first: Drone pilots had zipped 150 miles across the Russian border, bypassing a phalanx of air defenses to strike an oil rig deep inside the country. The attack was another validation for Destinus, one of Europe’s quietest but fastest-growing defense tech unicorns.

Russian-born physicist Mikhail Kokorich started it in 2021 to build hypersonic aircraft, but after Russia invaded Ukraine in 2023, he gave up his citizenship and pivoted to drones. Since then, thousands of its systems have been deployed in combat, and the company has rejiggered its business into an Anduril-like, vertically-integrated defense tech beast.

To enhance the “brains” of its Ruta drones, it recently acquired Swiss autonomy pioneer Daedalean for $223 million, and partnered with Shield AI to integrate the San Francisco startup’s Hivemind software. As it continues testing a new turbojet engine for high-speed drones, the company has also unveiled a new Ruta, the Block 2, which further blurs the line between a “drone missile” and a “cruise missile,” with a heavier warhead, a reported range of over 450 kilometers (280 miles), and an AI guidance system.

To fund a European expansion and meet growing interest from NATO customers, the firm picked up $162 million in convertible instruments and loans last year, adding to the $231 million it has raised in equity. That makes Destinus one of a handful of European defense tech companies—alongside fellow drone makers Quantum Systems, Helsing and Tekever—now valued at more than $1 billion.

4. Forterra

For building mission-ready systems for an autonomous future

Forterra, which began as Robotic Research in 2002, has extended far beyond R&D, becoming the only provider of all major Pentagon ground autonomy programs. Its most recent contract calls for the company’s advanced autonomous stack, AutoDrive, to be integrated into the Army’s Infantry Squad Vehicles, allowing them to conduct convoy operations, resupply missions, or casualty evacuation without human drivers.

In 2025, Forterra pursued partnerships with BAE, Volvo Defense, Raytheon, Oshkosh and CHAOS Industries, through which it’s pursuing a new autonomous armored vehicle and an autonomous air-defense system to defeat small drones. It also purchased GoTenna, a mesh networking startup that began life in Brooklyn after Hurricane Sandy with a focus on iPhones and the outdoor market; now it’s used by special forces and other military operators to maintain comms in denied environments. After raising a $238 million Series D round, the Clarksburg, Maryland-based company is now valued at more than $1 billion. Forterra says the investment will expand production of its edge-compute and autonomy platforms, including for applications beyond defense, like load handling and port logistics.

5. Merlin

For turning old aircraft into drones

Merlin’s mission is to build the AI-powered operating system that allows aircraft of all sizes to fly autonomously. Racing to meet a growing global demand for self-driving planes, the eight-year-old company has flown hundreds of autonomous missions across five different mission types with its AI pilot. In addition to retrofitting the C-130J cargo plane for U.S. special operations, it’s now converting more C-130s for the Air Force along with the KC-135 Stratotanker, the military’s mid-air refueling workhorse. A team-up with GE Aerospace will combine Merlin’s AI-powered autonomy software with GE’s Flight Management System (FMS), which already supports more than 14,000 aircraft worldwide, providing a direct pathway to bring autonomy to both new and legacy platforms.

The company is also getting closer to certification after achieving an experimental certificate of airworthiness in New Zealand and receiving FAA approval for its custom data system, which provides a critical avionics backbone for autonomy. This year, Merin moves into a new flight-test hub at Hanscom Field near its Boston headquarters, where it will continue testing following its latest waypoint: on St. Patrick’s Day, Merlin landed on the NASDAQ via a SPAC merger, raising $200 million and making it one of the first publicly traded prime contractors specializing in autonomous flight software. Get ready for more.

6. Astranis

For making geostationary communications satellites cheaper and more resilient

Out of a shipyard in San Francisco, Astranis makes a new class of smaller satellites that orbit around 35,000 km above Earth to provide communications coverage for internet, phone, and satellite‑to‑satellite relay, even when adversaries are messing with your signals. After Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, which turned the latter into a radio-frequency battlefield, that kind of resilient communication has become a high priority. To tackle the high costs of satellites, Astranis builds small: its MicroGEOs are about the size of a commercial washing machine, downsized from the typical school bus-sized satellites that populate geostationary orbit, or GEO. Software-defined radio turns the satellite from a static piece of hardware into a dynamic, reconfigurable platform, and gives customers—the U.S. and partner defense agencies, telecoms, banks—more control over their bandwidth in ways that aren’t possible when sharing a large, traditional satellite.

With smallness (and software) comes speed: the company says its advanced manufacturing processes reduce the timeline from order to orbit to as little as 18 to 24 months, versus the years-long timelines for the giant satellites that tend to fly in geostationary orbit. In 2025, two of Astranis’s satellites for Anuvu—a provider of mobile Wi-Fi whose customers include Southwest Airlines—reached orbit and operation, and the U.S. Space Force selected Astranis as a prime contractor for its short-lived Resilient GPS program. Since it was part of the Winter 2016 cohort of Y Combinator’s accelerator, Astranis has raised more than $350 million in venture funding from firms such as BlackRock, Venrock, and Andreessen Horowitz. 

7. Epirus

For taking out drone swarms with one big shot of microwaves

The swarm of 49 quadcopters that tumbled out of the sky above Camp Atterbury, Indiana, last August, was a watershed moment for a global counter-drone fight, and for Torrence, California-based Epirus. There are lots of ways to stop a drone if you’re defending a military base or an airport or stadium: You can try to shoot it, nab it with a net, hack it with electronic warfare, or even slam it with another drone. But these techniques can be very hit-or-miss. Epirus’s high-powered, ping pong-table sized microwave weapon, Leonidas, can disrupt the electronics on a drone at a distance, knocking it out of the sky at a fraction of the cost of other options. (That distance is undisclosed, but the Army is seeking a system that can reliably work at a range of one to two kilometers.) A Stinger missile can cost a few hundred thousand dollars each; each Leonidas is priced in the low eight figures, with what Eprius says will be a cost-per-kill in the pennies.

CEO Andy Lowery worked on nuclear reactors on aircraft carriers before he became the chief engineer for Raytheon’s Next Generation Jammer in 2010. Rather than wait for a traditional defense contract, he raised cash to spin off development and build prototypes for a new kind of “forcefield” weapon. (One early investor was Joe Lonsdale’s 8VC, backer of Palantir and Anduril.) Last summer, the Army awarded Epirus a $43.5 million contract to deliver two of its next-gen systems, and began deploying other units overseas for counter-UAS patrols.

In March 2025, Epirus banked another $250 million in a Series D funding round, bringing its total funding to more than $550 million and keeping its valuation hovering at over $1 billion. The money will help the company improve the power and range of the Leonidas and develop smaller versions: one designed for handheld use, one for mounting on top of the Army’s Stryker combat vehicles, and a miniature unit that could be attached to another drone. 

8. HavocAI

For bringing drone swarms to the high seas

HavocAI is taking aim at two yawning gaps at once. The Pentagon wants to be able to quickly field “a hellscape” of drones specifically to counter China’s mass advantage, as well as more boats to keep up with Beijing’s boatbuilding lead. (The United States builds less than 1 percent of commercial ships globally, while China produces approximately half.)

Like Saronic, Havoc is moving quickly to develop a range of vessels, from waverunner-like drones to larger ships that can autonomously sail long distances through rough weather and electronic interference, controlled by the swarm through an AI enabled “collaborative autonomy” platform. In a Department of Defense demo last year, the company showed one operator simultaneously controlling 25 autonomous vessels deployed across over 5,000 miles; and in a test requested by Ukrainian officials, it completed a live test of aerial and surface drones coordinating without GPS.

To meet a geopolitical urgency, Havoc’s manufacturing approach bends the cost curve to what it says is one-tenth the price of competitors, and shortens manufacturing timelines to months. Through partnerships with giants like Hanwha, PacMar, Lockheed Martin, and SAIC, the company is rapidly integrating with other platforms, incorporating missiles and sensors and developing a new electric propulsion system. Along with a steady stream of contracts (U.S. sailors and Polish naval commandos are already incorporating Havoc’s small Rampage vessels into their exercises) the Providence, Rhode Island-based company raised $85 million last year, bringing total funding to just under $100 million, thanks to investors like In-Q-Tel, Scout Ventures, and Vanderbilt University. The tides will keep rising: The House-passed spending bill allots $1.5 billion to expand small USV production and $1.8 billion to prototype, buy, and integrate “purpose-built” medium USVs. 

9. Onebrief

For streamlining and automating military plans

Military operational planning has long relied on reams of Powerpoint slides, emails, and Post-it Notes. Honolulu-based Onebrief builds an AI-fueled platform that digitizes all that, speeding up and simplifying planning and other defense workflows to keep forces efficient and competitive. After launching the company in 2019, Grant Demaree, a former Army captain and West Point graduate, began cold emailing majors and colonels on LinkedIn. Upon graduating from YCombinator in 2021, the company raised a seed round just as the Pentagon was starting to push for more software and AI.

Onebrief says average daily usage of its platform is growing fifteen times year-over-year, with the software deployed inside all U.S. combatant commands and across all military networks up to the top secret level. In 2025, Onebrief launched an AI assistant to handle time-intensive, routine staff tasks, such as summarizing complex plans, generating reports, and surfacing relevant insights. Strict guardrails, including a default read-only mode and oversight controls, help meet Pentagon standards and ensure trust, accuracy, and security.

In January, the company raised a $200 million Series D round led by Battery Ventures and Sapphire Ventures, doubling its valuation, from $1.1 billion to $2.1 billion, in a short six months. The cash will help it expand into domains like wargaming: In October, it acquired Battle Road Digital, which is already working on a three-year Army contract to provide the software engine for the agency’s Next Generation Constructive simulation platform. 

10. LeoLabs

For helping keep track of all the stuff in orbit

It’s gotten crowded up there, and tracking what’s in space has become an urgent priority for governments, companies, and researchers alike. It’s not just about managing the heavy traffic of thousands of rockets and satellites and pieces of debris, or avoiding a catastrophic collision. Space is now a battlefield, where communications, GPS, sensors, and increasingly weapons determine what happens down below.

To keep track of it all, LeoLabs builds and runs a global network of modular radar systems that can detect objects in low-earth orbit with centimeter-level precision in near real-time. For the surging number of entities launching stuff into space, that kind of awareness is essential for spotting risky anomalies and mitigating debris as well as finding cost-saving efficiencies: A recent analysis by the World Economic Forum, using LeoLabs’s data, found that even without any major collisions in orbit, congestion in space will cost industry between $25.8 billion to $42.3 billion over the next decade.

LeoLabs’s government work boomed last year, including a contract signed in September by the U.S. Department of Commerce and the Space Force to license access to the company’s catalog of nearly 25,000 objects in low-earth orbit. The Menlo Park-based startup is also working with the Commerce Department to better understand the period immediately after launch, when there is an elevated window of collision risk, and with NASA, in advance of a slew of new space launches. And, with backing from the Air Force Research Laboratory, LeoLabs is upgrading its truck-mounted, containerized Scout radar to track and scan the skies for the next emerging threat, and a major focus of the Trump administration’s multi-trillion-dollar Golden Dome: the hypersonic missiles that could easily defy existing air defenses.

11. Slingshot Aerospace

For tracking everything in orbit—as well as growing threats from earth

Keeping tabs on the growing traffic in space is hard enough. Making sense of all that data and extrapolating from it means orchestrating exquisite sensors, torrents of data, and good machine learning. Melanie Stricklan, a 21-year U.S. Air Force veteran with experience commanding experimental spacecraft missions and developing space control technologies, cofounded Slingshot Aerospace to meet a demand at DARPA: how to identify hidden anomalies in orbit.

Bad actors in space tend to hide some of their activities in large constellations. Slingshot’s Agatha AI is very good at identifying abnormalities, with a precision good enough to know that, for instance, out of a 3,000-satellite constellation, one particular satellite has two thrust motors that are inoperable. On top of its daily data collection of about 10 million observations per day from a global array of proprietary telescopes and ultra-wide field of view sensors, the El Segundo, California-based company taps an ever-growing trove from its acquisition of Seradata, a contextual database for all objects in space going back to Sputnik. If it doesn’t know who launched or who made or who bought any single space asset, its platform can make attributions once it shows up in orbit. It can also detect PNT (position navigation and timing) signal jamming and its origin, an application that Space Force Space Systems Command now uses daily under its PNT-SENTINEL mission.

In July, the company—which was also a 2024 MIC in the space category—launched TALOS, the Thinking Agent for Logical Operations and Strategy, an agentic AI framework that helps simulate autonomous orbital flight operations, a critical need as space become a more active battleground. The system was validated in January when the Space Force awarded Slingshot a $27 million contract to help modernize its Operational Test and Training Infrastructure (OTTI), which will involve replacing scripted exercises with AI-driven adversaries that can think, adapt, and maneuver like real satellites operated by rivals.

12. Red 6

For training military pilots with AI and AR

Training fighter pilots is costly and dangerous, and even the U.S. Air Force faces an annual deficit of 60,000 sorties just to meet minimum readiness requirements. Daniel Robinson knows this well, because he spent a decade as a fighter pilot in the Royal Air Force, and, as an instructor, became the first non-American to fly an F-22 Raptor. In 2018, he started Red 6 to address the training problem, by moving the simulator into the actual cockpit.

The company developed ATARS (Advanced Tactical Augmented Reality System), a virtual platform that lets pilots experience fully immersive training worlds while they’re airborne. That immersiveness adds precious realism to combat and other training scenarios. By networking with other aircraft and drones, humans and autonomous systems can also train together at scale, while generating valuable data to measure and improve combat readiness.

Of course, making AR work in the field, and at speed—and with sun glare—is no easy feat. After years of testing with the Air Force and aerospace contractors like Boeing and Lockheed, Red 6 decided to tackle the technical challenges head-on, by developing its own visor. Before the hardware starts rolling out (by the end of the year, says Robinson), the company is embarking on its biggest contracts yet: testing ATARS on the F-16 and the Apache helicopter, which would be the first installation of augmented reality in a rotorcraft. Robinson’s goal is to become a Pentagon program of record, and set a new standard for safer and more frequent flight training. And far cheaper too: If implemented across the force, he says ATARS could save taxpayers approximately $10 billion over five years. Someday, ATARS applications might even extend beyond war games. One investor in Red 6’s $70 million Series B round in 2023 was an older Orlando-based developer of immersive experiences: Disney.

13. Vatn Systems

For taking autonomous vehicles underwater

The Navy is on a robot shopping spree, part of an upgrade that could help forestall—or fend off—a flotilla of warships in the South China Sea. Ukraine’s success with unmanned kamikaze speedboats against Russian ships in the Black Sea has further fueled demand for both surface and underwater vessels.

Founded in Bristol, Rhode Island, in 2023, Vatn Systems builds a pair of torpedo-like submarine drones that are capable of traveling up to 200 nautical miles at up to 30 knots (34.5 mph). In 2025, it deepened its cooperation with the U.S. Navy and Marine Corps, signed its first overseas contract with Singapore’s defense agency, and raised a $60 million Series A led by BVVC with participation from defense giants Lockheed Martin Ventures, Airbus Ventures, and Hanwha. (RTX Ventures and CIA-backed In-Q-Tel also participated in the company’s $13 million seed round in 2024.)

Vatn is using the cash to develop a new drone and refine its GPS-free inertial navigation systems. In January, it also acquired Crewless Marine, which builds sensor systems that help with homing, seeking, and simply listening for ships, subs, and other underwater vehicles. The purchase also gives the company more control over its tech stack and supply chain, helping it drive down costs and scale up production. Using vertically integrated processes, modular assembly, and analytics from partner Palantir, the firm hopes its new factory will enable it to produce up to 2,000 vehicles annually, a tenfold increase over average manufacturing timelines for remotely operated vehicles. 

14. Shift5

For protecting the onboard systems of military and civilian planes

GPS spoofing and cyberattacks are now a constant in battle zones, putting aircraft and vehicle navigation at serious risk. Shift5 has pioneered analytics that sit on top of onboard systems to provide near real-time anomaly detection, from the Air Force’s giant fighter planes to your local bus. The platform’s versatility across all types of systems and domains—defense and commercial, rail, automotive, or industrial—has quietly set a new benchmark for onboard operational technology cybersecurity, and provided mechanics with the granular observability they need to keep fleets in shipshape. The Arlington-based company says that its torrents of data also help with things like fuel efficiency analysis, meaning demonstrable savings for fleets. All of that appeals to new partners like Boeing, AWS, and Carahsoft, who helped it expand vehicle deployments by more than 100% last year. It also secured a $75 million Series C funding round led by Hedosophia, bringing its total funding to $185 million.

Last year, the company launched a new capability that compliments its existing anomaly detection tech. With an industry-first radio frequency-based approach, the system can monitor multiple protocols across both legacy and modern platforms, analog and digital; flag corrupted or non-standard messages; and use machine learning to identify and track hardware-level weirdness. Shift5 says the system gives military and commercial operators early warning of GPS spoofing attempts up to 200 nautical miles away.

15. Xona

For building the space-based future of navigation

We take it for granted, but 50-year-old GPS has never looked more fragile. A surge in spoofing and jamming in recent years has upended warfare and put commercial aircraft, agricultural machines, and untold numbers of critical systems at risk. Xona is launching a global constellation of small navigation satellites to bring real-time, centimeter-level certainty to any device, anywhere on Earth, interference be damned.

Last June, six years after a group of Stanford-trained aerospace engineers started the company, Xona launched its first production class satellite, Pulsar-0. The team recorded resilient signals more than 100 times stronger than GPS, accurate to the centimeter and capable where GPS fails today, such as under trees and inside buildings. Next came commercial agreements with leaders in distributed timing systems, who are already testing Pulsar-0 in their products, and a new Software Development Kit, meant to encourage other satellite providers to join in. Satellite provider Astranis (see above) is relying on Xona’s algorithms for its own GPS initiative, aimed at creating a layer of redundancy within the broader GPS infrastructure.

Boosted by a $92 million in Series B financing, as well as an expanded Space Force contract, the company began construction on a 10,000-square-foot manufacturing site in the Bay Area, with a capacity to build 100 satellites per year. And it readied to expand its fleet up above, ordering up eight satellites from Belgian satellite manufacturer Aerospacelab, with launches set to begin late this year.
