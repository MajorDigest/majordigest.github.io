The most innovative energy companies of 2026

As electricity demand surges—driven in part by the growth of data centers—this year’s most innovative companies in energy are finding new ways to meet that demand without driving up power bills or emissions. Others are tackling different pieces of the energy transition, from materials to electric mobility.

Redwood Materials, which started in battery recycling, now also uses second-life EV batteries to store energy for the grid. Several startups are building virtual power plants: Renew Home uses smart home devices like the Nest Thermostat to shift demand, Base Power is building a fleet of home batteries that support the grid while lowering electric bills, and We Drive Solar stores power in a fleet of carshare EVs.

LuxWall makes ultra-efficient windows that cut energy use so significantly that they can reduce peak loads on the grid. King Energy owns and operates solar and battery systems at large commercial buildings, helping shrink bills for small business tenants. Aira’s subscription-based model makes it affordable for European consumers to shift to heat pumps; AtmosZero makes a cost-competitive industrial heat pump to help factories shift from fossil-powered equipment.

On the power generation side, geothermal leaders XGS Energy and Zanskar developed new ways to build clean power that’s available 24/7, while Euclid Power’s AI tools help renewable energy developers manage complex projects. Lilac Solutions and Phoenix Tailings are scaling up cleaner production for critical materials, from lithium to neodymium-praseodymium. And in Africa, Zeno is tackling another energy challenge—helping drivers transition to electric motorcycles with a sleek design that includes a swappable battery that can also provide power at home.

1. Redwood Materials

For giving used EV batteries a second life powering data centers

When Tesla cofounder JB Straubel launched Redwood Materials in 2017, he set out to build a circular domestic supply chain for lithium-ion batteries to reduce reliance on mining and overseas processing. The company has since grown into the largest battery-pack recycling operation in the U.S. In 2025, Redwood added a new line of business: giving used electric-vehicle batteries a second life as energy storage for the grid. Through a partnership with AI infrastructure company Crusoe, Redwood helped rapidly build a solar microgrid to power Crusoe’s modular data centers.

The system pairs more than 800 former EV batteries with 20 acres of solar panels, creating the first in what the companies say will be a series of similar microgrids. Batteries that still retain sufficient capacity can continue operating for years in stationary storage, while others are routed into recycling. The recycling business continues to expand. In November, Redwood began operations at a new $3.5 billion factory in South Carolina. Along with its recycling operations in Nevada, the new plant makes the company the only major domestic producer of cobalt in the U.S. The company also produces nickel, lithium, and copper recovered from spent batteries and refines those elements into battery-ready materials for new cells. In 2025, Redwood raised $425 million in a Series E funding round to support its next phase of growth.

Read more about Redwood Materials, honored as No. 14 on Fast Company’s list of the World’s 50 Most Innovative Companies of 2026.

2. Renew Home

For turning millions of homes into a nationwide virtual power plant

In heat waves last summer in Texas and across large parts of the Eastern U.S., a new virtual power plant reduced electricity demand at critical moments, helping the grid avoid outages. The system is run by Renew Home, a company formed through the merger of Google’s Nest Renew and OhmConnect, a home energy management provider. Renew Home now connects more than 5 million households nationwide. The company works with smart home devices, including the Nest thermostat.

When setting up a device, customers can opt into an “Energy Shift” program, which automatically reduces energy use during the most expensive and grid-strained hours of the day. Customers set their own comfort preferences in advance, and the system makes small, often unnoticeable adjustments to save energy. They can also participate in Rush Hour Rewards, which triggers larger reductions during events like heat waves, giving customers additional financial incentives for helping the grid. The result: lower bills for households and real capacity for the grid. In California last summer, participating customers saved an average of $30 on their electricity bills and earned an additional $15 from utilities in exchange for helping reduce peak demand. As utilities struggle to meet growing demand for electricity, Renew Home’s virtual power plant offers a cheaper way to add capacity than running gas power plants or building large battery installations.

3. Base Power

For quickly growing a network of home batteries to support the grid

To add new energy storage to the electric grid, Base Power is building a fleet of home batteries that it owns and operates. Homeowners pay a small monthly fee in place of their electric bill, and the batteries provide backup during outages. Without the large upfront cost of typical home systems, adoption can grow faster: In less than two years, Base deployed more than 100 megawatt-hours of residential capacity in Texas. Its software charges batteries when electricity is cheap and sells power back to utilities during peak demand.

Base has been approved for Texas’s Aggregated Distributed Energy Resource (ADER) program, allowing it to combine distributed batteries and bid them directly into the grid. The program boosts reliability across the state, generates additional revenue, and helps lower costs for homeowners. Unlike large utility-scale batteries, which face long interconnection delays, distributed systems can be deployed quickly. The company has expanded to multiple cities in Texas and plans to grow nationally, raising a $1 billion Series C in October 2025 to support that growth.

4. King Energy

For unlocking solar for multi-tenant commercial buildings

Commercial rental buildings face a split-incentive problem that often keeps owners from installing solar panels: Tenants benefit from lower electricity bills, while property owners have to pay the upfront costs. King Energy tackles this by installing, owning, and operating large solar and battery systems on multi-tenant buildings itself. The company underwrites each property—including entire shopping centers and industrial parks, not just individual buildings——and property owners pay nothing for the installation. Software tracks solar generation and energy use from tenants, then generates a single monthly bill that includes local utility charges, streamlining the experience for customers.

The company, which has raised $36 million in venture capital and over $200 million in project equity, tripled revenue year over year in 2025, and has expanded to more than 250 properties in 12 states. Nearly half of installations are in low- and medium-income communities, helping in areas where energy affordability is most needed. Property owners enter into long-term leases for the roof space, boosting their income and commercial property value. In 2025, tenant customers saved more than $550,000 and prevented more than 18,000 tons of CO2 emissions.

5. Heimdall Power

For making high-voltage lines smarter with real-time sensing

Most power lines carry far less electricity than they can handle, relying on static estimates based on the worst-case conditions. But Heimdall Power makes electric grids smarter by helping utilities understand, in real time, how much power their transmission lines can safely carry. Its self-powered sensors clip onto high-voltage lines, continuously measuring current, sag, temperature, humidity, ice, and other conditions. Software-based virtual sensors add more data. The technology precisely calculates the capacity of a power line at any given moment, making it possible to increase transmission by around 40% or more.

In a Minnesota project, for example, Heimdall’s tech unlocked an average of 63% more capacity during peak summer demand across 175 miles of transmission lines in its first year. While other companies also work on this type of “dynamic line rating” tech, Heimdall continues to innovate. It uses drones to install the sensors in remote or hard-to-reach areas, making it possible to minimize the need for new infrastructure. The information that the system gathers can also help utilities in other ways: spotting overheating lines and wildfire risk before problems happen, for example, or better planning how to route power from sources like wind or solar. As the company rapidly grows, the tech is now used by more than 50 utilities in 20 countries. Heimdall opened its North American headquarters in 2024. Revenue has grown by 2.5 times over the past two years, with triple-digit annual growth.

6. AtmosZero

For helping factories generate steam from ambient air

At New Belgium Brewery in Colorado, one step of the brewing process now runs on a new kind of heat pump. The brewery is the first to test AtmosZero’s Boiler 2.0, a machine roughly the size of a shipping container that pulls heat from the air and turns it into steam for factories to use in making everything from beer to snacks to paper—replacing older boilers that run on fossil fuels. In early 2025, three years after spinning out of research at Colorado State University, AtmosZero opened its first 100,000 square foot plant. In the summer, it installed a 650-kilowatt pilot unit at New Belgium. While other industrial heat pumps exist, the new design comes at a lower cost. It’s set a record for the most powerful air-source heat pump capable of making steam directly, and uses the startup’s proprietary compressors to efficiently make steam as hot at 200 degrees Celsius (392 degrees Fahrenheit). The company will launch full commercial production this year. 

7. XGS Energy

For delivering 24/7 clean power from hot rocks

Traditional geothermal power depends on specific geological conditions, but XGS’s next-generation tech works anywhere that there is hot rock, vastly expanding where it’s possible to build. The company’s system—which has been 30 awarded patents and has another 46 pending—works like an underground radiator, using pipes in a closed loop to circulate water deep below the surface to capture heat. In September 2025, the company reached a new milestone, with 3,000 hours of continuous operation at its commercial demonstration site in California. It’s the first company to prove that this type of geothermal technology works consistently and reliably, delivering around-the-clock power with no carbon emissions, no water use, and no fracking. XGS has partnered with Meta to build 150 megawatts of next-generation geothermal power in New Mexico to power data centers. Over the last year, the company doubled in size and expanded its project pipeline.

8. Phoenix Tailings

For producing rare earth metals in the U.S. with zero reliance on China

Rare earth metals are critical for energy technology—from batteries to wind turbines—but most of the U.S. supply chain relies on China. Phoenix Tailings is helping jumpstart domestic production. In October 2025, the company opened a new rare earth metallization facility in New Hampshire, the first major operation of its kind in the U.S. with no reliance on Chinese inputs or technology. The company’s process is designed to be used on mining waste, boosting sustainability. (Initially, the new facility will use a combination of mined and recycled material, but the company plans to begin incorporating mining waste at the new facility in 2028.) The new facility can produce 200 tons of rare earth metals a year, with the capacity to scale to 1,000 tons, enough to the supply the entire U.S. defense industry. Phoenix raised $76 million in a Series B round of funding in 2025. The company also won a grant from the Department of Energy to advance its work to extract critical minerals from wastewater.

9. LuxWall

For making windows that insulate as well as walls

As utilities work to meet growing energy demand, an unexpected tool can help ease pressure on the power grid: windows that insulate like walls. Michigan-based LuxWall, backed by Bill Gates’ Breakthrough Energy Ventures, has commercialized vacuum-insulating glass that acts like a thermos bottle for buildings. The company has secured more than 30 patents on its technology. The windows achieve R-18 to R-23 thermal resistance, roughly five times the performance of typical high-efficiency windows.

Customers include JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Siemens, Procter & Gamble, and other large building owners deploying the windows to reduce energy costs and meet sustainability goals. Utilities are also beginning to offer incentives for the tech, recognizing that the windows can cut heating and cooling energy use significantly enough to shrink peak loads when the grid is under stress. The company opened its first high-volume production facility in late 2024. Despite losing a Department of Energy grant for an expansion, LuxWall is currently building a second, 276,000-square-foot factory in Detroit that will begin running in early 2027.

10. Zanskar

For using AI to discover hidden geothermal power

By combining advanced AI with modern drilling techniques and computational geoscience, Zanskar identifies hidden or underutilized sites for geothermal power plants that conventional exploration methods have failed to detect. In December 2025, the company announced that it had discovered a site in Nevada capable of supporting a new geothermal power plant that would deliver clean, always-on energy to the grid. The site had no prior history of geothermal exploration and showed no surface-level indicators of geothermal potential, making it the first “blind” geothermal system in the United States to be discovered with clear commercial potential in more than 30 years.

Previously, Zanskar purchased Lightning Dock, an underperforming geothermal power plant in New Mexico, where declining well productivity had limited output. Using its AI-driven subsurface modeling and geophysical analysis, the company identified the optimal location for a new production well, drilled deeper into the reservoir, and restored the plant to full capacity. In January 2026, the company announced a $115 million Series C funding round led by Spring Lane Capital, the largest investment to date in AI-powered geothermal discovery. It plans to use the funds to scale its discovery platform and begin developing power plants at its newly identified sites.

11. Aira

For making high-performance heat pumps accessible for Europeans

Aira, founded in mid-2023, is a rapidly growing Swedish startup focused on making it easier and more affordable for European households to switch to heat pumps. The company offers a subscription-style model with no upfront cost: customers pay a monthly fee that is often lower than their previous heating bills, with savings of up to 40%. Aira’s heat pumps are high-efficiency systems tailored to each home. The company uses tools such as AR and LiDAR to assess properties before installation and ensure the system is correctly sized, improving performance and reducing energy waste.

Aira has also developed a compact heat pump designed specifically for small European apartments, where space constraints often limit adoption. The system is smart and adaptive, learning household routines and optimizing heating and hot water schedules to maximize efficiency and comfort. In November 2025, Aira expanded with the launch of a battery and energy hub that integrates with its heat pumps and rooftop solar, allowing customers to manage a full home energy system. The company has reached an annual sales run rate of €200 million ($232 million) and employs roughly 1,200 people across 18 hubs. Four dedicated Aira Academies train installers, addressing a key bottleneck in heat pump deployment across Europe.

12. Zeno

For making affordable electric motorcycles for Africa

Launched by a Tesla alum, Zeno makes a sleek electric motorcycle designed from scratch for emerging markets. Motorcycles are a mobility lifeline in places like Africa and India, but most run on gas. That’s both polluting and expensive; some drivers spend as much as a quarter of their income on fuel and servicing. To keep the cost of Zeno’s electric motorcycle low, the company sells the bike without a battery, offering a battery-as-a-service subscription model. Customers can swap batteries instantly at swapping stations or charge them. The company is also field testing the use of its battery at homes, where it can charge on solar power and then provide energy for three to four days. Both the upfront cost of the motorcycle and the operation cost are lower than a gas-powered bike.

It’s also designed to outperform other motorcycles in terms of durability and its ability to carry heavy loads on rough roads. After three years of research and testing, the startup launched its first motorcycle in 2025 in Africa, quickly growing a long waiting list. It now has 130 charging points in Kenya and Uganda, making it one of the largest charging networks in the region, and more than 1,000 customers on the road in East Africa. This year, the startup closed a $20 million Series A round of funding.

13. We Drive Solar

For using parked EVs as backup power for the grid.

When a fleet of car share EVs in the Netherlands isn’t in use, the car batteries serve a second purpose: storing power for the electric grid. We Drive Solar, a company that makes bidirectional EV chargers, partnered with car-sharing company MyWheels and automakers on the project. Last June in the city of Utrecht, the team rolled out 50 Renault 5 E‑Tech electric cars in the world’s first vehicle-to-grid carsharing service. Another 120 cars were added in December, and the fleet will grow to 500 this year. During the day, the cars can help store wind and solar power—including energy from local buildings, where a third of city rooftops are equipped with solar panels. During periods of peak demand, the cars send power to the grid, avoiding the need for power from polluting gas-fired plants. Software tracks upcoming reservations and ensures that the batteries always have enough charge to be driven. At full scale, the 500-car fleet can deliver between 5 to 10 megawatts of congestion reduction for the grid. The team is now expanding to other Dutch cities, beginning with Eindhoven and Rotterdam.

14. Lilac Solutions

For making lithium production faster and greener

Lilac Solutions has developed a faster, more sustainable method for extracting lithium—the critical raw material used in batteries that power electric vehicles and the electric grid—which could soon face a supply shortage. The company, which has raised $318 million to date, designed tiny, reusable ceramic beads that capture lithium from brine and then safely return the brine to the environment without depleting water levels. In a seven-month-long pilot at Utah’s Great Salt Lake in 2025, Lilac validated its technology, showing that it can recover lithium efficiently while protecting the lake’s ecosystem. Commercial lithium production at the site could potentially begin as early as 2027. Lilac also completed pilot projects on a salt brine in Argentina and an oilfield brine in Germany last year, demonstrating the flexibility of its approach across different brine chemistries. In October, the company unveiled the fifth generation of its technology, which further improves recovery rates and durability. Lilac just finished construction of a commercial-scale factory in Nevada that will begin manufacturing its lithium extraction tech later this year.

15. Euclid Power

For helping developers build big wind and solar projects faster

Euclid develops software and services to simplify the financing, development, and management of renewable energy projects. Its software platform creates a central hub to organize and track the thousands of documents associated with each project so buildout can happen faster. The company’s AI tools extract key information from these documents and convert it into structured data for diligence, budgeting, and compliance tracking, with expert reviewers ensuring the outputs are accurate before finalizing. By reducing manual review time by up to 50% and shortening transaction cycles by 60%, the technology not only accelerates project timelines but also lowers the risk of cancellations.. Since the company was founded in 2021, it has onboarded more than 1,000 solar and storage projects with more than 12 gigawatts of solar and storage capacity, enough to power 2.1 million homes. In 2025, the company doubled its enterprise customer base and expanded major client engagements to multi-million-dollar projects.
