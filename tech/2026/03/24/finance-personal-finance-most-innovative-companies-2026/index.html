The most innovative finance companies of 2026

This year’s innovation honorees in finance and personal finance demonstrate how quickly companies around the world are using AI to build products and features that simplify life for their customers. 

Ramp, at the top of the list, is leading the way. Now worth $32 billion, the fintech unicorn’s tireless focus on saving its customers time and money has pushed it to implement AI agents that dramatically streamline tasks like expense approval. Similarly, startups Flex (based in Miami) and Pennylane (based in Paris) are supporting finance teams and accountants by providing them with copilot-style tools. On Wall Street, Rogo is freeing analysts from crushingly long hours of number-crunching with AI prompts that connect to Excel. 

Then there are companies reinventing consumer finance products. Walmart-backed upstart brand OnePay brings together personal finance needs in one app; already, OnePay boasts 6 million monthly active users. Bilt’s rent-focused approach to rewards has given it a foothold with affluent young professionals, exactly the demographic that rewards-powered credit card companies are eager to engage. Robinhood’s plans for stock tokenization promise to expand market access to retail traders worldwide. In Colombia, buy now, pay later startup Addi is sparking broader e-commerce adoption in a country where the vast majority of transactions still happen in cash. San Francisco-based Aven, meanwhile, has upended the traditional home-equity line of credit market with a secured credit card that blurs categories and helps homeowners access their wealth. 

Of course, there are also innovators operating at the level of financial plumbing that many consumers never see. Plaid, with its wide web of connections between financial institutions, has developed its own proprietary credit assessments and fraud detectors. Credit card infrastructure startup Cardless partners with brands to spin up credit cards that engender loyalty. And Circle Internet Group, the stablecoin pioneer, has created a payments network designed to help bring digital currencies further into the mainstream. 

1. Ramp

For giving businesses unprecedented agency in managing costs

Ramp demonstrated once again this year that helping finance teams save time and money is valuable indeed. Specifically, the startup says that since its 2019 founding it has saved its customers $10 billion and 27.5 million hours of work. Those results have catapulted Ramp to a $32 billion valuation and $1 billion in annualized revenue. Last year’s growth stemmed from Ramp’s swift implementation of AI agents. For example, in October the startup relaunched its bill pay product with AI agents for accounts payable, with the goal of approving and paying invoices in as few as a few clicks. Bill pay quickly became Ramp’s fastest growing product outside of its standard-bearer corporate cards; bill pay customers have tripled their payment volume year-over-year. Similarly, Ramp’s AI agents for controllers have further streamlined expense management, automatically approving low-risk expenses that are within company policy and escalating just 10-15% of expenses for review. Those features have attracted the interest of larger and larger customers, including CBRE, Shopify, Anduril, Figma, and Notion. As is crucial for any fintech, Ramp is also proving that it can cross-sell and engender customer loyalty; the majority of Ramp’s 50,000 customers already use two or more Ramp products.

2. Bilt

For rewarding renters—and now homeowners, too—with neighborhood benefits

Rewards platform Bilt launched in 2022 with a credit card that let people earn perks for on-time rent payments. But over the past year, the New York-based startup has expanded into a much more comprehensive loyalty program. Even as it has amassed more than a million cardholders, Bilt had been quietly building software tools to help landlords process their rental payments and more. Bilt is now the exclusive payment-processing platform for 70% of the top 100 residential landlords in the U.S., and many of these landlords, including Douglas Elliman Real Estate, GID, Greystar, and Related, have invested in the company. (Last July, Bilt announced a $250 million fundraising round at a $10.75 billion valuation.) The startup crafts cleverly designed partnerships with these landlords, offering tenants points for rent and properties new ways to incentivize lease renewals. It’s also expanded its integrations beyond travel providers to include more than 45,000 merchants centered around the neighborhoods its customers call home. In January, Bilt unveiled a revamped credit card program: It now offers three tiers of cards and—in a sign of Bilt’s next big push—rewards mortgage payments as well as rent. During the weeks that followed the card launch, users swamped Reddit forums and Bilt’s customer service line with confusion and complaints. But the company tweaked the cards, expanded its support systems, and weathered the storm. More than 30% of members who opted into the new cards selected one of the options with an annual fee, which Bilt sees as a signal of loyal intent. 

3. Addi

For bringing AI-enabled BNPL to a market still dominated by cash

In the U.S., BNPL companies are disrupting credit cards. In Colombia, BNPL startup Addi is disrupting cash, which still represents for 70% of financial transactions—and in the process, introducing consumers to a cutting-edge, AI-enabled credit experience. The Bogota-based startup serves 2.5 million digital shoppers, offering them installment loans at 27,000 merchants, including Apple and Adidas. Last year, Addi rolled out an AI customer-support agent dubbed Addri, which now handles over two-thirds of customer interactions and has achieved a satisfaction score of nearly 80%, on par with human agents. Similarly, a merchant-facing AI agent built by the startup increased new merchants by 17.5% and boosted recovery of dropped merchants by 28%. Those efficiencies have helped Addi surpass $150 million in annualized revenue, with gross margins of nearly 60%. For four quarters in a row, the company has been profitable. In a country where only 20% of consumers have credit cards (and credit files are thin), Addi is demonstrating that it’s possible to expand access to credit by following a digital-first playbook.

4. Robinhood

For leading the way on tokenization

Last year Robinhood proved that it can post big results while at the same time pushing the boundaries of product innovation in financial services. Its revenue hit a record $4.5 billion in 2025, including $1.28 billion in Q4 alone. Meanwhile, Robinhood has been moving at full speed to embrace tokenization, which cofounder and CEO Vlad Tenev sees at the future of investing. Tokenization creates digital version of traditional assets, like equities, that can be traded 24/7 on blockchain networks. For retail investors, Robinhood argues, tokenization is the best way to gain access to private companies like SpaceX or highly-valued public companies like Nvidia. In the same way, Robinhood has been aggressive in its embrace of prediction markets, viewing them as both a new kind of market and as an information source for its funded customers, now 27 million strong. But Robinhood’s isn’t all brash speed these days: in a sign of the company’s maturity, it has taken a relatively measured approach to the rollout of its credit card, despite the long waiting list of prospective cardholders among its 4.2 million Gold subscribers.

5. OnePay

For giving Walmart shoppers and consumers everywhere a full suite of financial tools, including the opportunity to invest

OnePay’s momentous growth over the past year is a testament to the power of having friends in the right places. Backed by Walmart and Ribbit Capital, OnePay emerged from stealth mode in 2022 by acquiring startups One and Even. The resulting app operates independently but works with Walmart to acquire customers, including Walmart’s own employees. That strategy has paid off: OnePay can now boast over six million monthly active users. They rely on the app for everything from saving and direct deposit to credit-monitoring and point-of-sale loans. Plus, OnePay has been shipping new features at a rapid clip. In November, it added stock trading. In December, it launched an AI-powered financial assistant designed to offer advice on healthy financial habits. Notably, OnePay has been designed to reward any kind of shopper—in addition to the standard credit card rewards offered through the OnePay CashRewards card, for example, OnePay offers rewards to its debit card customers as well.

6. First Street

For putting a number on the true cost of climate change

For property owners in 2026, is there any greater risk than climate risk? Enter First Street, which has become the leading provider of property-level data on the risks associated with flooding, wildfires, hurricanes, extreme heat and more. First Street’s granularity has made it a favorite source for everyone from real estate investors conducting due diligence to first-time homebuyers putting together a bid. At the consumer level, First Street has its critics—in particular, the homeowners who have been blindsided by higher-than-expected risk scores (Zillow, for example, revealed in 2024 that 16.7% of its listings contained major wildfire risk, based on a First Street analysis; following homeowner outcry, Zillow has downplayed the scores.) But, tellingly, institutional customers are eager for First Street’s intel. First Street’s year-old Enterprise Suite contains risk scores for 200 million property worldwide; customers include Blackstone, Norges Bank Investment Management, Huntington Bank, and Nuveen. Previously a nonprofit, First Street raised $46 million in Series A1 financing in 2024 and is backed by investors including General Catalyst and Galvanize Climate Solutions.

7. Forge Global

For bringing the best of public market trading to the private markets

Forge Global, a private markets pioneer, had a big year. In the first half of 2025, Forge took advantage of broad interest in private companies like Anthropic, SpaceX, and Stripe to capture $1.45 billion in trading volume, surpassing its full-year volumes for 2024. In June, Forge launched a revamped version of its trading marketplace that operates 24/7 and mimics many features of the public markets, giving investors greater clarity and control when trading private company shares. And then, in November, Forge announced that it would be acquired by Charles Schwab for $660 million in an all-cash deal. The deal further solidifies Forge’s position as a leader in the evolution of private markets. Companies are staying private for longer, and markets have needed to adapt in order to provide their stock-rich, cash-poor employees with liquidity and retail investors with exposure to their growth. With real-time pricing information and infrastructure designed to help customers navigate complex rules, Forge has helped bridge the yawning, growth-stage gap between early-stage venture and IPO.

8. Entrepreneurs First

For offering European founders a chance to learn Silicon Valley’s secrets

The European startup scene needs a boost, and Entrepreneurs First is here to provide it. The global tech talent incubator was founded in 2011 with the goal of making entrepreneurship a viable pathway to prospective leaders outside of Silicon Valley. The organization’s alumni have gone on to found 500 startups in deep tech, healthcare, AI, space, and other sectors; those companies are collectively valued at more than $13.4 billion and have raised over $3.8 billion in follow-on funding. Now, Entrepreneurs First is launching a residency program, dubbed Bridge 2, designed to expose European talent to the best of the Bay Area. Forty founders will get eight weeks of mentorship and time to build, plus fundraising support and enterprise credits for tools like Azure and GitHub. Entrepreneurs First takes care of room and board, giving participants a chance to grind, American-style. Some of the biggest names from across the pond are Entrepreneurs First backers, including DeepMind’s Demis Hassabis and Mustafa Suleyman, and Stripe’s John Collison.

9. Aven

For unlocking the power of homeowners’ wealth.

An estimated $35 trillion in wealth is tied up in Americans’ homes, and Aven is helping them unlock that value. Founded in 2019, Aven’s flagship product is a secured credit card designed for prime and super-prime homeowners. While the card looks and feels like a regular credit card, it doubles as a home-equity line of credit, and comes with the lower interest rates that traditional HELOCs can provide. Over the last year, Aven has tripled its customer base and issued more than $3 billion in credit lines. Already, the card has saved homeowners more than $215 million in interest. Aven can offer consumers superior economics because it has spent years investing in what it calls its “machine banking platform,” combining software and robotics to replace legacy processes like notarization that add time and cost to many lending products. The startup’s lofty, ultimate goal is to cut the cost of consumer credit in half. In September 2025, Aven announced a $110M Series E at a $2.2B post-money valuation, led by Khosla Ventures.

10. Monarch

For making it easier for families to budget and plan for the future

Even as Silicon Valley startups push the boundaries of what’s possible in AI, one category of consumer need has remained stubbornly resistant to innovation: personal finance, and in particular, family budgeting. Enter Monarch, an award-winning personal finance app with more than 1 million members that centralizes account information and works as well for couples as it does for individuals. Monarch has won over users—its subscriber growth has doubled year-over-year—with its realistic approach to money management. For example, instead of trying to force-fit users into strict and consistent budgets, Monarch last year introduced flex budgeting, a more adaptive solution that allows for flexibility across categories while still providing clarity on how spending compares to goals. Similarly, Monarch’s AI assistant can respond to questions like, “Can I afford to increase my vacation budget?” in a way that’s personalized based on a user’s financial history. As Monarch introduced new features and makes design tweaks, it gets instant feedback from the 34,000 people who visit the Monarch subreddit on a weekly basis. With their help, the app will only get better.

11. CaskX

For making it possible to add whiskey barrels to your retirement portfolio

While many investment platforms these days are spinning up exotic digital assets, CaskX is focused on the finest hooch that retirement dollars can buy. One recent offering featured a rye mashbill with 60% entry proof and a char level of 4: investors could buy the new bourbon whiskey barrels, giving the distillery the working capital it needs to operate, and then hold their position until the barrels mature (CaskX typically recommends holding for four to eight years). To sweeten the deal, CaskX provides storage and arranges for VIP tours of the distilleries where it partners. According to the company, having greater access to capital gives distilleries the freedom to mature barrels for longer, instead of bottling early to book a sale. Allowing distilleries to focus on quality boosts the industry as a whole, helping American whiskey stand up to competition in Japan and Scotland. CaskX is small but growing: revenue increased roughly 30% to hit $28 million in 2025. By building a marketplace that follows SEC guidelines for securities, CaskX has paved the way for retail investors to back the brands they want to sip.

12. Flex

For understanding the financial needs of modern, mid-sized businesses

Automation has arrived in the CFO suite, and Flex is taking full advantage. Founded in 2019, the Miami-based startup has built a modernized financial operating system for businesses that leverages AI to streamline banking, bill pay, expense management, and more. Instead of focusing on small businesses or large corporations, Flex is targeting the companies in between, with annual revenue of $1 million to upwards of $100 million. Flex’s Net-60 credit card, which allows customers to toggle between earning rewards and maximizing float, is its biggest draw. Over time, Flex plans to cross-sell personal banking products for mid-market business owners and their complex needs. So far, Flex’s approach is resonating, with annual payment volume on the startup’s cards now exceeding $1 billion and annual volume on its bill pay offering exceeding $500 million. Revenue quadrupled in 2025. In December, Flex raised $60 million in Series B financing.

13. Cardless

For modernizing the infrastructure that powers branded credit cards

Cardless, a modern platform for powering branded credit cards, has been built from the ground up with the idea that credit cards aren’t just payments tools; they are essential drivers of loyalty and engagement. That approach has helped the San Francisco-based startup, founded in 2019, attract customers including Coinbase, Bilt Rewards, Qatar Airways, and Alibaba, despite its early stage. Cardless says that the cards it has built for clients have grown transactions by 400% year-over-year. Some of that is a factor of speed to market: Unlike banks, which are the traditional operators of co-branded credit cards, Cardless can launch a new card in just 90 days. In September, the startup raised $60 million in Series C funding, led by Spark Capital.

14. Pennylane

For helping European accountants ditch paper and embrace AI

Paris-based accounting and financial management platform Pennylane, founded in 2020, is helping lead a new wave of French AI startups with big ambitions. Just six years in, Pennylane is already serving 6,000 accounting firms and 800,000 small businesses across France and Germany with a solution that encompasses cash flow management, expense tracking, invoicing, and forecasting. Pennylane aims to automate these functions and more, with copilot-style AI, and in the process free accountants and their clients to think more strategically about financial priorities. In January, the startup raised $205 million in Series E funding at a $4.2 billion valuation led by led by new investors TCV and Blackstone Growth; leading Silicon Valley firms CapitalG and Sequoia Capital also participated. According to Pennylane, that backing will support the R&D required to expand the platform into a one-stop solution for accountants. With Pennylane’s help, they might be able to finally bid adieu to paper receipts and laborious data entry.

15. Juniper Square

For applying AI efficiencies to private market fund management

Juniper Square, which makes software and services for fund operations and management, has been growing along with the growth in private markets. Founded in 2014, Juniper Square works with over 2,000 private markets general partners (GPs) representing $1 trillion in investor equity, including H.I.G. Capital, Tishman Speyer, and CBRE Investment Management. Over the last year, the company has unveiled a series of AI-powered updates designed to streamline how GPs operate. Its JunieAI assistant is fully integrated into Juniper’s core platform, helping customers review complex documents and audit for compliance. And its AI CRM is specifically designed for investor relations, with the goal of helping GPs achieve their fundraising goals. Last June, Juniper Square raised $130 million in Series D funding, led by Ribbit Capital, at a $1.1 billion post-money valuation.

16. Plaid

For giving businesses powerful new tools designed to assess credit and payment risk

Plaid, which provides an essential layer of digital infrastructure connecting financial services, has spent the last year building intelligence tools designed to assess transaction risk and creditworthiness. It built Trust Index, a machine learning-powered model designed to flag high-risk transactions before fraud takes place. According to Plaid, Trust Index catches 40% more fraud than older tools. Plaid also built LendScore, a complement to traditional credit scores that analyzes consumer cash flow in order to measure payment responsibility. LendScore provides a 25% lift in predictive accuracy, helping lenders reduce losses to the tune of $1 billion. LendScore has benefits for consumers, too: Subprime and near-prime borrowers can benefit from LendScore’s more complete picture of financial health, helping reduce their APRs. Put it all together, and Plaid is carefully but confidently starting to take advantage of the data—including 500 million daily transactions—that flow through its systems.

17. Circle Internet Group

For creating regulated infrastructure for stablecoin transactions

After years of building digital payments infrastructure and establishing a coalition of supporters, Circle is finally having its moment in the sun. The stablecoin issuer went public in June 2025, its shares soaring to more than double in price during early trading. In Circle, Wall Street investors see an opportunity to further legitimize the use of blockchain infrastructure for cross-border payments and more. Circle’s USDC stablecoin, a digital dollar that it is 1:1 redeemable for U.S. dollars, has been carefully designed alongside regulators; there is now $75.3 billion of USDC in circulation (and roughly the equivalent in U.S. dollars in Circle’s reserves). In Q4 2025, USDC onchain volume hit $11.9 trillion, a 247% increase year-over-year. Now, Circle is focused on facilitating stablecoin adoption and use. Last spring the company launched its payments network, which facilitates stablecoin transactions and has been adopted by both traditional banks, payments providers, and digital asset service providers. The payments network makes real the great promise of crypto, with near-instant settlement 24/7 and global scale. Already, it’s doing $3.4 billion in annualized volume.

18. Rogo

For giving Wall Street’s spreadsheet pros an AI boost

Long hours and late nights are synonymous with Wall Street, but Rogo could shift the paradigm. Founded in 2021, the startup builds AI designed for investment banks, private equity firms, and hedge funds; it already works with 80 top financial institutions, including Jefferies, Lazard, Moelis, Tiger Global, Nomura, and Raymond James. With Rogo, Excel worker bees at these firms can run prompts from the startup’s suggested library of workflows (“proofread my deck” or “earnings comp analysis”) or design their own custom tasks. Rogo can pull in data from a wide array of sources, from SEC filings to private data rooms, and then automate work in spreadsheets and presentations that would have otherwise taken hours. In January, Rogo raised $75 million in Series C funding, led by Sequoia Capital and joined by some of the biggest names in finance, including Henry Kravis, JPMorgan Growth Equity, Tiger Global, and Wells Fargo. According to Rogo, it has saved its 25,000 users over 500 years’ worth of work. Surely there’s a bonus for that?

19. ShopBack

For bridging the gap between online marketing and offline shopping

ShopBack’s success is proof that shoppers worldwide love rewards. Based in Singapore and active throughout the Asia-Pacific region, ShopBack is a centralized rewards platform that seamlessly integrates with e-commerce, as well as with in-store transactions and mobile gaming. The company was founded in 2014 and today serves over 60 million customers. ShopBack powers more than $5.5 billion in annual sales for merchant partners and has awarded more than $800 million back to users—while extending its reach to more and more types of transactions. For example, ShopBack last year added a feature for scanning both digital and physical receipts, giving shoppers a chance to earn rewards on categories like groceries. Since the feature went live in Australia last October, it has attracted more than 300,000 users. Also last year, ShopBack introduced an integration with mobile games called ShopBack Play, which extends rewards for advancing in games like Monopoly Go. Already 500,000 users are playing, with half of those new to the company’s ecosystem.

20. Piana

For creating a fuel-and-pay digital card that helps European companies electrify their fleets

French startup Piana is on a mission to help European companies electrify their fleets. Electric vehicle adoption is on the rise worldwide, but many commercial fleets remain tied to gas-guzzling cars and trucks. Enter Piana, which has developed a card and paired app that drivers can use to pay for charging at kiosks throughout the continent. Notably, Piana is designed to support businesses as they transition to EVs or hybrid vehicles. Its software helps identify which combustion-engine vehicles in a fleet are nearing the end of their usefulness, and could be replaced. Piana also helps customers learn to use EVs with maximal efficiency through insights on battery life, power consumption, and more. All the while, it offers card and app solutions in parallel that are compatible with gas vehicles, ensuring that businesses can manage their fleets in one place. The startup raised Series A funding, led by Fintech Collective, last spring.
