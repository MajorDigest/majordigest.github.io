The most innovative companies in Latin America for 2026

In 2026, it’s no surprise to hear a CEO boasting about using artificial intelligence—even if it really amounts to slapping a new chatbot on a website. But there were companies in Latin America that found genuinely useful ways to apply the latest technology. In Chile, Ainwater provided AI-driven software to help water utilities make better decisions and save energy. In Colombia, Dapper’s platform gives multinational corporations a new tool to navigate a maze of local regulations. In Brazil, iFood’s “Large Commerce Model” helped restaurants create deals that snag new customers. And in Mexico, Niko Energy used AI and computer vision to help assess residences and businesses for rooftop solar, paving the way for “virtual power plants.”

Other companies innovated through acquisitions and new lines of business. Brazilian electric motor giant WEG bought an app to connect drivers to chargers. Petlove&Co, also in Brazil, bought a lab testing company to bring state-of-the-art imaging to its veterinary ecosystem. Colombian citric acid exporter Sucroal turned one of its waste products into an input for beer brewers. Finally, there were companies applying cutting-edge science to stubborn problems. In Argentina, Puna Bio used extremophile bacteria to make next-generation fertilizer and pesticides. In Colombia, NanoFreeze used bio-nanotechnology to make refrigeration more efficient.  

1. Cobre

For helping Colombians move money between banks and businesses seamlessly

Every month, countless Colombians go down to their local corner store and wait in line to pay their cell phone bills in cash. But now they have a new option that’s digital and instantaneous. 

On the one hand, it’s thanks to an initiative by the Colombian government. Following in the footsteps of CoDi in Mexico and Pix in Brazil, Colombia’s central bank launched Bre-B in October 2025, creating the infrastructure to let people and businesses move money instantly from one account to another. 

On the other hand, it’s thanks to the work of Cobre, a fintech company building products that sit on top of that Bre-B infrastructure to enable a slew of digital transactions, including payments from businesses to other businesses. When a customer uses Bre-B to pay their Movistar cell phone bill, it’s Cobre that makes sure the money goes where it’s supposed to. “It sounds very simple. It is extremely complicated,” says Cobre CEO José Gedeón. “And no one else in the market is able to offer that.”

Cobre has announced similar products with the likes of taxi app Cabify, allowing drivers to be paid instantly. Since Bre-B launched last fall, Cobre estimates it has processed over 400,000 Bre-B transactions, making it the largest player in corporate payments through Colombia’s new system. It’s still a small slice of the company’s business, which also includes cross-border payments — it processed more than 28 million transactions in 2025  — but Cobre betting on Bre-B’s importance. “We have believed this is a fundamental change for business for years, and we think this is also the way that we get our foot in the door with the largest companies in the country,” says Gedeón.

2. WEG

For laying the groundwork for more electric vehicles

WEG is the largest maker of electric motors in Latin America. Literally and metaphorically, it’s helping to power the clean energy transition in the region. But it doesn’t make only motors. WEG had a busy 2025. It acquired Tupi Mob, an app that 370,000 people use to find and charge their electric vehicles in Brazil — a sizable segment of the country’s small but growing EV market. WEG also launched a repair and maintenance center for electric vehicles, including the hundreds of electric buses that the company supplies with batteries. It partnered with Volvo to install new EV charging stations in Peru. And it unveiled plans to start selling chargers in Europe this year. WEG says these efforts are all part of “an integrated strategy that combines energy efficiency, operational efficiency, renewable energies, and electric mobility” to fuel the energy transition in South America and beyond.

3. Niko Energy

For speeding up solar installations across Mexico with AI roof mapping

As solar prices have come down, it’s become increasingly easy to power a house or a small business using the sun instead of the grid. In Latin America, that’s created a boom in “energy as a service” companies that act as one-stop shops for solar power and energy storage. Last year, Niko Energy raised $15 million to build Mexico’s first “virtual power plant,” a network of small energy-producing and energy-storage devices pooled together to support the electric grid. The goal is for small businesses and residences to power not only themselves but their neighbors. Niko says it has been working with the Mexican government to help create the necessary regulatory framework to realize such an operation. In the meantime, it’s been scaling up. The company launched an “AI Roof Mapper,” utilizing Google-powered APIs and computer vision to more quickly assess how many solar panels can be installed on building roofs. According to Niko, the tool reduces site assessment time tenfold, enabling analysis of as many as 1,000 sites in a single week. Niko’s revenue quadrupled in 2025 as the company signed new clients that include discount grocery chain Grupo Neto and Grupo Modelo, the beer company. 

4. Dapper

For using AI to help companies navigate the region’s complex bureaucracies

Searching for reliable information about local government regulations in Latin America can feel like navigating a labyrinth. If you’re a multinational corporation working across the region, it can feel like navigating 30-plus labyrinths simultaneously. The result is a market for “regulatory intelligence,” which typically means paying local consultants and lobbyists who can sort through the bureaucracy. 

Dapper, founded in Colombia in 2023, is trying to disrupt this market by replacing consultants with AI-driven software. The result is what CEO Luis Alberto Rodriguez calls “regtech”—regulation technology—that he hopes can emulate what healthtech and fintech have done for banking and healthcare access in Latin America: “turning emerging markets’ greatest weakness (institutional opacity) into a competitive advantage through AI-first infrastructure.” Rodriguez is setting his sights next on expanding into the Middle East, North Africa and Southeast Asia.

The company’s map is already broader than it was a year ago. Dapper branched out from Colombia into four more countries in 2025: Peru, the Dominican Republic, Mexico, and Chile. It also more than doubled its client list from 24 to over 50, including the largest public affairs firms in Latin America and U.S. heavyweights including Coca-Cola and General Motors. 

Major multinationals don’t want “20 different public-affairs processes for 30 countries,” Rodriguez says. “They want one reliable operating standard. Consultants cannot deliver that. Dapper can.”

5. Puna Bio

For increasing farm yields throughout South America with ‘extremophile’ bacteria bio-fertilizers

“Extremophiles” aren’t lovers of extreme sports, like skateboarding or skydiving. They’re organisms that thrive in places with extreme conditions, like deep sea vents, toxic waste dumps, or Earth’s highest and driest desert, the Puna de Atacama.  

Puna Bio is an Argentine company that has built a business on the unique attributes of extremophiles found in this last location. It studies and tests bacteria, looking for attributes that could be useful in agriculture. It says some of the microbial “biostimulants” it’s developing might replace industrial-grade chemical fertilizers that are known to pollute waterways and degrade soils.  

In 2025, Puna Bio closed a series A round that included the Gates Foundation’s first investment in an Argentine company. It also began to deploy Kanzama, a biostimulant that enhances nitrogen fixation for wheat and barley that launched in mid-2024 and aims to reduce the widespread overuse of nitrogen fertilizers. 

But the innovation highlight of 2025, according to CEO Franco Martinez Levis, was the launch of Taryx, the world’s first biofungicide derived from extremophiles. It received regulatory approval in Argentina at the end of the year, paving the way for a 2026 commercial launch. 

“By applying our technology to crop protection, we prove that the robust genetics of extremophiles—microbes from the Puna, Earth’s highest and driest desert—can outperform traditional biologicals and even chemical counterparts in a wide variety of use cases,” says CEO Franco Martinez Levis.

6. Sucroal

For turning the byproducts of its citric acid production into inputs for beer brewers

Sucroal has been manufacturing raw materials for industrial use in Colombia’s Valle de Cauca for over 50 years. One of its main lines of business is turning local sugar cane into citric acid for export. 

But in 2025, the company began to commercialize one of its waste products, a common inorganic salt called calcium sulfate, and sell it to beer brewers, who use it to deal with water hardness. Sucroal said uptake has been strong, owing to its product’s “greater whiteness, higher purity, [and] favorable acidity”—all traits that are ideal for brewing, “particularly in pale ale and lager beers,” says Ana Lucía Gómez, director of investigation and development at Sucroal. 

Sucroal is now selling calcium sulfate not only in Colombia, but to breweries in the Dominican Republic, Ecuador, and Guatemala. 

7. iFood

For helping restaurants surface more affordable meal orders to customers, without hurting margins

Founded in 2011, iFood is now the dominant delivery platform in Latin America’s largest economy, Brazil. Over time, it has extended far beyond “food” to include everything from pharmacy deliveries to financial services. 

In August 2025, the company launched a program intended to make delivery services more accessible: iFood Hits. “It’s not a discount campaign; it is a new delivery format that creates new demand, bringing first-time users into the delivery economy and helping restaurants reach audiences they never accessed before,” says spokesperson Ivanna Palmiero.

The way it works is that iFood takes in data from partner restaurants and then guides operators to select menu items that can be offered at lower prices without harming margins. These recommendations are made by a “Large Commerce Model” that iFood says applies artificial intelligence to billions of past interactions so it can identify diners’ purchase patterns and price sensitivity.

iFood says more than 8 million people have tried iFood Hits since it launched last summer, including 323,000 who placed their first-ever orders. iFood says participating restaurants have seen an average of 32% order growth.

8. NanoFreeze

For reducing emissions and keeping beers cold with bio-nanotechnology

NanoFreeze is a Colombian company that uses bio-nanotechnology to make refrigeration more efficient. Founded by scientists and engineers in 2020, it has been developing  thermal packaging, “chiller” liquids for water-cooled systems, and “Natural Freezers”—energy-saving panels that can be used to retrofit existing refrigerators. 

In 2025, the company took its “Natural Freezer” technology mainstream, collaborating with the beer maker Corona to deploy these retrofitted units in retail networks across Colombia and Mexico. NanoFreeze also took steps last year to break into the growing industrial cooling market (think data centers), with a new bio-nanofluid. The product is made of ice nucleation activity (INA) enzymes, which organize water molecules into an ice-like configuration, combined with highly conductive nanoparticles that together increase water thermal conductivity and thus energy efficiency in industrial cooling systems.  Early tests showed an increase of up to 44% in thermal efficiency and the potential to reduce over 76,000 tons of CO2 annually per plant when fully deployed.

9. Petlove&Co

For making pet health care more accessible for Brazilians

Petlove&Co began in Brazil in the late ’90s as an e-commerce site for pet owners, launched by a veterinarian who’d been forced to close as large competitors expanded. In the decades since, it grew to become a whole pet “ecosystem” that includes grooming, clinics, health plans and now boasts industry-leading diagnostic centers.

In the past, Petlove&Co said its customers saw frequent delays for rescheduling and MRIs. Then in late 2024 it acquired Provet, the first pet laboratory company in Brazil.  In 2025, the company integrated Provet and the state-of-the-art Signa Victor 1.5T MRI machine, doubling exam capacity and improving diagnostic accuracy. 

The integration took a lot of legwork, from redesigning workflows and automating processes in immunological testing to expanding the “home-collection fleet” that pays house calls to pet owners’ homes. But it was well worth it. PetLove says it saw a 116% increase in diagnostic exams and 50% revenue growth. Customers have benefited from fewer delays and higher-quality imaging for the 1 million pets covered by PetLove&Co’s health insurance policies. 

“The acquisition of Provet was absolutely strategic for our business and for democratizing access to high-quality, advanced veterinary care for our customers,” says PetLove&Co spokesperson Bruna Hakan.

10. Ainwater

For applying AI to water treatment in Chile

Ainwater is a Chilean company that, as its name suggests, applies Artificial Intelligence to water management. The sector is ripe for innovation but difficult to break into, as it requires winning over naturally conservative water utilities. 

In 2025, Ainwater introduced a new operating system, Poseidón 3.0, which it describes as a game changer. “Poseidón doesn’t just display information: it predicts behaviors and recommends optimal actions, transforming a historically reactive process into one that is proactive, autonomous, and scalable,” says CEO Camilo Huneeus.

In 2025, Ainwater said it expanded into Brazil and Mexico, and implemented its model to 51 plants in under three months. Poseidón is now in 100 sites in Chile, Mexico and Brazil, helping treat the water utilized by over 1 million people, according to the company. Ainwater estimates that its innovations have saved clients as much as 30% on operational costs through optimized their energy use, aeration, and chemical dosing.
