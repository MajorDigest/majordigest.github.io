The most innovative space companies of 2026

This year’s most innovative companies in space illustrate the scale, ambition, and growing diversity of the commercial space economy. In the realm of space-based communications, Amazon placed over 150 broadband satellites—part of a planned constellation of more than 3,000—into low Earth orbit, establishing Amazon Leo as a viable Starlink competitor. In the race to deliver seamless satellite broadband to land-based mobile devices, AST SpaceMobile and Skylo Technologies demonstrated complementary approaches. AST is leveraging its own constellation of large phased-array satellites to connect directly to standard 4G and 5G smartphones. Skylo is partnering with satellite operators to create a nonterrestrial network (NTN) that lets compatible devices switch seamlessly from ground cell towers to satellite when out of coverage range.

Tomorrow.io takes data from its constellation of cloud-piercing radar and microwave satellites and feeds it through AI to deliver predictive weather planning services to government and enterprise customers. And Varda Space Industries is tapping orbital microgravity to manufacture high-value pharmaceutical ingredients in free-floating capsules designed to return to Earth on their own.  

With two launches of its heavy-lift New Glenn rocket in 2025, Blue Origin proved itself a potential spoiler in SpaceX’s long-running near monopoly in the launch business; a super-heavy-lift version is also in development. Meanwhile, Impulse Space is advancing its fleet of spacecraft targeting “last mile” transportation services. It updated its dishwasher-size Mira “space tug” and is securing customers for its “kick stage” Helios vehicle designed to deliver large payloads from low-Earth orbit into geostationary orbit and cislunar space. And with NASA’s Artemis program on the cusp of its first crewed mission to orbit the Moon, Lunar Outpost unveiled its Eagle “space truck” built for use on the lunar surface and prepared its autonomous MAPP rover for a 2026 NASA mission to explore the mysterious Reiner Gamma swirl.

1. Tomorrow.io

For making better weather predictions from space

Combining a constellation of cloud-piercing microwave sounder satellites with cutting-edge AI, Tomorrow.io has become a top commercial provider of weather data to governments and the military. It also offers targeted weather intelligence to enterprise customers across a broad swath of critical industries. Following the completion of its first constellation of satellites in January 2026, the company can now provide sub-hourly revisits of any spot on Earth. Customers receive detailed and timely data to feed weather prediction models for cyclone-prone areas over oceans and radar data for parts of the previously underserved Southern Hemisphere.

Tomorrow.io’s sounder satellites, which track atmospheric radiation, excel at detecting rapid storm intensification when infrared sensors are limited by cloud cover. The company has provided hurricane monitoring data to NOAA’s Commercial Weather Data Pilot program since February 2025 as well as data to support National Hurricane Center forecasts.

For enterprise customers—including Uber, JetBlue, and numerous big names in logistics, aviation, and insurance—the company taps AI to transform its raw weather data into hyperlocal, industry- and business-specific action plans. It might, for example, divert delivery drivers, preemptively cancel flights, or alert policyholders to protect their property or cars. In July, Tomorrow.io announced a partnership with Palantir to incorporate its data into automated weather-planning tools for Palantir’s government and private-sector customers.  

Read more about Tomorrow.io, No. 49 on Fast Company’s list of the World’s 50 Most Innovative Companies of 2026.

2. Blue Origin

For making the private launches the new space race

Despite losing its first-stage rocket attempting a landing at sea, Blue Origin’s New Glenn became the first company in the commercial era to reach orbit on its first attempt, using a newly designed rocket to reach medium Earth orbit last January. A second launch carried Blue Origin’s first customer payload—deploying NASA’s ESCAPADE (Escape and Plasma Acceleration Dynamics Explorers) Mars mission—and landed the first-stage safely on a barge.

The company plans to launch a more powerful version of the rocket with upgrades to its proprietary liquid methane engines early this year. Longer-term plans include launching its Blue Moon Mark 1 lunar lander for robotic missions.
The company is also developing a “super heavy” rocket, the New Glenn 9×4 (named for its nine engines powering its first stage and four propelling the second), which is designed to deliver 70 metric tons to low Earth orbit. It will directly compete with SpaceX’s Starship.

3. Impulse Space

For connecting the last mile in space mobility

As space gets increasingly crowded, maneuvering satellites and other spacecraft into precise positions has never been more important. Impulse Space, founded by SpaceX first hire Tom Mueller, is tackling this last-mile logistics challenge with multiple vehicle types. In January 2025, the company launched the second, updated version of its dishwasher-size Mira orbital transfer vehicle, aka “space tug,” and demonstrated its rapid response and maneuverability. In December, Impulse completed its landmark Remora mission, an autonomous rendezvous in low Earth orbit where the new Mira docked with its predecessor. The mission relied on a single lightweight camera and closed-loop guidance, navigation, and control software developed by Starfish Space. The operation also underscored the agility of Impulse’s upgraded Mira spacecraft. Its eight Saiph thrusters use nontoxic nitrous oxide and ethane propellant to provide up to 208 newtons of thrust, while a cold-gas reaction system offers full attitude control.

Impulse is also working on a “kick stage” vehicle called Helios, designed to deliver large payloads from low Earth orbit into geostationary orbit and cislunar space. In May 2025, it signed a multilaunch agreement with Luxembourg-based satellite operator SES, which will use Helios to shorten transfer times for satellites into their final orbits. In August, NASA selected Impulse to explore lower-cost ways to deliver spacecraft to hard-to-reach destinations. And in October, Impulse announced plans to build its own lunar lander, which would launch with Helios on a standard medium- or heavy-lift rocket and could put a three-ton payload on the moon as soon as 2028, according to the company. It would bridge a gap in the U.S.’s ability to land midweight payloads on the lunar surface.

4. Varda Space Industries

For enabling miniature pharma experiments in space

Varda Space Industries designs, builds, and operates specialized spacecraft to process hard-to-manufacture pharmaceutical ingredients in microgravity and bring the results safely back to Earth—all at a lower cost and a faster cadence than running such experiments on the International Space Station. Its investors include Khosla Ventures and Founders Fund. El Segundo, California–based Varda achieved significant breakthroughs in 2025. Last June, its fourth mission, W-4, was the first to employ a satellite “bus,” reentry capsule, heat shield, and pharmaceutical processing payload all made and operated by the company. The mission tested precisely crystallizing a proprietary molecule for a drugmaker. Varda is the only commercial operator cleared by the FAA to autonomously bring products to Earth from space.

In late November, Varda used SpaceX to launch W-5, carrying a government payload. It was funded through the Prometheus program, a partnership between the Air Force Research Laboratory and commercial space entities that addresses national security by accelerating novel science and technology experiments in the extreme reentry environment.

5. Iceye

For being the always-on eye in the sky

Finnish–Polish satellite maker Iceye launched 14 satellites in 2025, expanding what it says is the world’s largest constellation of SAR (synthetic aperture radar) satellites that continuously collect 3D images of Earth regardless of weather. The recent launches included Iceye’s Generation 4 satellites, designed and built in-house, which deliver sub-20-centimeter resolution imagery, the most detailed available commercially. The Gen 4 design expands coverage area and agility, meaning Iceye can obtain both finely detailed images and wide-area coverage from the same platform. 

During the January 2025 Los Angeles wildfires, Iceye data helped responders navigate dangerous terrain and gave insurers information that helped them assess claims quickly. Iceye’s expanded Flood Rapid Impact service now supports those battling more than 50 disasters annually across Asia, Europe, Oceania, and the Americas.

The company is playing an expanded role in national security as well, especially in Europe. In 2025, it secured an approximately $185 million contract with the Finnish Defence Forces. It also has contracts with the armed forces of Poland, Portugal, and the Netherlands, which has already launched the first of four planned Iceye satellites. In January 2026, Iceye stepped up its commitment to Ukrainian defense with an agreement to provide the embattled country expedited access to critical, high-resolution satellite imagery.

6. MDA Space

For building the next generation of software-defined satellites

MDA Space, a 57-year-old Ontario-based manufacturer of satellites and robotics systems, continues to define the future of space-based connectivity—and has a $3.2 billion order backlog to prove it. The company’s software-defined MDA Aurora satellites adapt to emerging user needs, dynamically allocating resources to deliver the right amount of connectivity in the right place. For example, they can track aircraft and ships to provide continuous connectivity across the globe, including in areas where terrestrial networks do not reach.

After being tapped in 2023 to build 198 satellites for Telesat’s Lightspeed constellation, which will provide broadband connectivity across Canada, MDA doubled its manufacturing capacity in 2024. In February 2025, after announcing a $768 million contract to build more than 50 satellites for Globalstar’s Apple-backed LEO constellation, MDA began another major expansion to roughly double manufacturing capacity at its production facility outside Montreal. The forthcoming factory, which incorporates AI-enabled robots, collaborative automation, cobots, and augmented-reality tools, will support production of up to two MDA Aurora satellites per day. In December, MDA Space signed a strategic partnership agreement with the Canadian Department of National Defence and Telesat to deliver advanced military satellite communications capabilities for the Canadian Armed Forces.

7. Amazon Leo

For launching the fastest satellite broadband network yet

Code-named Project Kuiper, Amazon’s low Earth orbit satellite constellation got off to a slow start, launching its first two prototypes in October 2023. But after a quiet 2024, the newly branded Amazon Leo completed six full-scale launches in 2025, putting more than 150 of its planned 3,000 satellites into orbit. Taking on SpaceX’s Starlink, the service aims to provide fast, reliable broadband to customers around the world, particularly in traditionally underserved communities.

The satellites, which are designed and manufactured by the company in Kirkland, Washington, use electronically steered phased‑array antennas that can “point” an internet signal wherever it is needed and change that pattern on the fly. They use an optical infrared laser connection to link satellites into a space‑based mesh network that can route traffic without ground‑station visibility. The company cites broadband network throughput of 1 Gbps downstream and 400 Mbps upstream, which would be the fastest satellite broadband option. Amazon has said it expects to start providing services to customers in 2026.

8. Skylo Technologies

For enabling true space-to-cell service today

Partnering with operators of existing geostationary satellites, Skylo runs a cloud-native nonterrestrial network (NTN) platform that lets compatible cellular modems switch seamlessly from ground cell towers to satellite when out of coverage, using the same SIM card and phone number. In March, Verizon customers became the first in the U.S. to use NTN to send text messages to any other customer device (or text 911) via satellite when out of terrestrial cell range using select Samsung and Google phones. In August, Skylo and Google announced the expansion of satellite connectivity to the Pixel 10 Series smartphone. Google’s Pixel Watch 4 became the first smartwatch with integrated satellite communications powered by Skylo’s NTN, offering standalone emergency messaging without a phone or cellular plan. In Germany, Skylo is planning a commercial launch with Deutsche Telekom and is working with the carrier and BMW Group to offer satellite-enabled real-time messaging, vehicle status messages and hazard warnings within vehicles.

In August, Skylo introduced the Skylo Voice Gateway, enabling the industry’s first direct-to-device voice service over satellite using a standard smartphone. The service enables high audio quality at global scale and can support tens of millions of calls per region each month.

9. Lunar Outpost

For roving around the Lunar South Pole

In March, Lunar Outpost made history when its Mobile Autonomous Prospecting Platform (MAPP) became the first commercial rover to reach the lunar surface, aboard Intuitive Machines’ lunar lander. While a lopsided landing prevented the vehicle from exiting the lander and actually “roving” the surface, its subsystems were intact and ready to go, and it gathered data from the surface before and after landing.

This year, the company will get several more chances to show off its four-wheeled rovers. In collaboration with Johns Hopkins University’s Applied Physics Lab and NASA, it will send an autonomous rover to investigate the Reiner Gamma lunar swirl, which has puzzled scientists for centuries.

In April, the company demonstrated a prototype of its four-wheel-drive Eagle “space truck” built for NASA’s $4.6 billion Lunar Terrain Vehicle Services program in collaboration with General Motors, Goodyear Tire & Rubber Company, Leidos, and MDA Space. And in December, it announced that it would provide a surface vehicle for astronauts on the Artemis IV mission, expected to launch in 2028.
The company’s lunar aspirations go beyond rovers: This year, it will launch a mission to evaluate its Mobile Autonomous Robotic Swarm (MARS) software, developed for the U.S. Air Force Research Laboratory and U.S. Space Force’s SpaceWERX program. Testing two small spacecraft running Lunar Outpost’s software in tandem will mark a step toward managing autonomous robotic fleets in space.

10. AST SpaceMobile

For connecting the dots of space-based mobile telecom

AST SpaceMobile is taking an innovative approach in the competitive race to deliver seamless satellite broadband to land-based mobile devices. Unlike constellations that require specialized handsets or equipment for connectivity, its BlueBird satellites connect directly to standard 4G and 5G smartphones, enabling calls, texts, streaming, and internet access from anywhere on Earth. The company’s phased-array satellites are some of the largest and most powerful in use. And thanks to their size, AST SpaceMobile expects to achieve global coverage with approximately 90 satellites, not hundreds or thousands.
AST has also secured partnerships with over 50 mobile network operators, representing nearly 3 billion subscribers worldwide. Since launching its first five BlueBird satellites in September 2024, the company has extensively validated its network—completing the first space-based video calls with Vodafone in Europe and Rakuten Japan. It has also carried satellite-enabled video calls with AT&T and Verizon, validating that it can integrate seamlessly with major U.S. operators. 

The company expects to launch up to 60 more BlueBirds by the end of this year. In February 2026, it announced the deployment of its first BlueBird 6 satellite, featuring a communications array spanning approximately 2,400 square feet— more than three times larger than previous models, with up to 10 times their data capacity. It is the largest commercial communications array ever deployed in low Earth orbit.
