A networking revolution at AWS

Amazon Web Services (AWS) is reshaping its underlying network foundation, a move that could redefine how enterprises approach cloud technology, costs, and operational efficiency. As enterprises contemplate next-generation workloads, from generative AI to globally distributed applications, AWS’s end-to-end custom networking stack is a new calculus for cloud economics, agility, and security.

Let’s take a deeper look at what AWS has announced, why it matters, and how smart enterprise technologists should plan to navigate the landscape of opportunities.

Let’s begin with AWS’s new networking philosophy, which focuses on making network connectivity nearly invisible to users and administrators alike. For AWS, networking needs to be as reliable as flipping a switch—it simply works, and no one notices unless it fails. To meet this lofty goal, AWS spent the past decade moving away from traditional, proprietary network hardware and has built a unified, custom stack that spans everything from silicon to software.

At the heart of this innovation is the decision to use a single switching application-specific integrated circuit (ASIC)—the silicon “brain” at the center of every network switch—across its aggregation, core, and border networks. Rather than the old industry practice of mixing and matching hardware from various vendors (each with its own firmware, bugs, and scaling challenges), AWS has network engineering and operations teams focus on a single, consistent foundation. This not only simplifies procurement and troubleshooting but also enables scalable, reproducible deployments that surpass the speed of conventional enterprise or cloud architectures.

All these switches run NetOS, a Linux-based operating system that AWS built to optimize security, automation, and rapid patching. If a bug appears, AWS can fix it everywhere, instantly; there’s no waiting for vendor patches and no risk that yesterday’s firmware problems could spiral into tomorrow’s outage.

The numbers are staggering. The current AWS switch handles 51.2 terabits per second (Tbps) across 64 ports, each operating at 800Gbps. But the pace of innovation is accelerating: AWS’s next-generation switch, coming soon, will reach 102.4Tbps, with ports running at 1.6Tbps. Combined, AWS’s network comprises roughly 2 million devices, 50 million to 60 million optical links, and more than 20 million kilometers of fiber—enough to circle the moon 25 times.

Enterprise complexity is exploding

Why should enterprises care? Networking remains a hidden driver of cloud costs, reliability, and agility. As your application portfolio grows more complex—from AI-powered clusters to globally distributed databases—the more you pay for bandwidth, latency, downtime, and management overhead.

AWS’s custom stack means network reliability, speed, and security are baked into the infrastructure. As AWS upgrades its hardware and software, enterprises receive these improvements instantly. One standout is the introduction of hollow-core fiber. By replacing traditional fiber with tubes of glass surrounded by air (or a vacuum), AWS reduces network latency by 30%, making data center regions larger, more flexible, and better able to meet low-latency demands—at a premium price for workloads where latency is the difference between winning and losing.

AWS’s high-precision time network is another game-changer for workloads such as distributed databases and financial trading platforms. By synchronizing clocks to within microseconds, AWS enables globally consistent transactions, unlocking new application architectures for enterprises that were once forced to pay steep premiums for specialized hardware and software.

Even more important is the “invisible” network approach. With ownership of hardware and software, AWS can quickly patch bugs, fix vulnerabilities, and optimize performance. For enterprise workloads, this reduces risk, shortens maintenance windows, and accelerates the onboarding of emerging technologies such as generative AI. The new UltraCluster network topology also reduces the number of switches between servers, shrinks latency, and supports gigantic clusters, which are vital for enterprises betting big on AI training and inference.

Capture cost savings and avoid pitfalls

Enterprises planning to adopt AWS’s latest networking platform should start preparing now, even if adoption is months away.

First, conduct a thorough analysis of current and future networking demands. If your workloads are increasingly distributed, real-time, or bandwidth-intensive (think AI, Internet of Things, database sharding, or financial trading), you’re likely a prime candidate to reap the benefits. Map your existing architectures to high-performance, low-latency network regions within AWS, and evaluate whether your cloud-native or hybrid workloads can leverage these innovations without major changes to code or data architecture.

Second, rethink your operational model. With AWS handling so much of the network stack, IT teams must shift focus from deep infrastructure management to strategic workload placement, cost optimization, and performance tuning. What was once a highly specialized skill—fine-tuning vendor-specific hardware and software—gives way to a model driven by visibility, automation, and integration across AWS services. Enterprises should invest in training and tools that enable their teams to respond quickly to network upgrades, new feature rollouts, and data locality shifts, so they remain agile and competitive.

Finally, evaluate partnerships, security, and compliance models. AWS’s custom stack offers enhanced patching and rapid response to vulnerabilities, but enterprises must ensure their own security controls and audit trails are up to the task. This includes reviewing incident response plans, compliance certifications, and monitoring capabilities, especially as the network backbone becomes “invisible” and less directly controlled.

Where AWS’s platform fits

AWS’s innovations are ideal for enterprises with high-performance, distributed workloads, for companies scaling out in ways that strain legacy architectures, or for those seeking to minimize downtime and operational friction. If speed, reliability, and ultra-low latency are major competitive advantages, AWS’s custom stack will almost certainly be a fit.

However, not all enterprises or workloads need the bleeding edge. Enterprises with stable, low-volume workloads, little geographic distribution, or limited reliance on real-time data may find the premium pricing associated with leading-edge network infrastructure unnecessary. For some, multicloud or hybrid deployments retain strategic value for flexibility or compliance reasons; for these organizations, careful architecture is required to avoid lock-in and to ensure workloads can span multiple providers or on-premises environments.

At the end of the day, AWS’s new networking platform isn’t just about speed or capacity. It signals a shift toward operational agility, security, and cost optimization for enterprises willing to adapt—and a warning to those who lag behind. The days of wrestling with patchwork hardware vendors and waiting for network upgrades are fading. To capitalize on AWS’s networking revolution, enterprises need to develop their strategies today by aligning technology, people, and processes to gain a competitive advantage tomorrow.
