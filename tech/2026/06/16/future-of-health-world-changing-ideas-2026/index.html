New diagnostics, new treatments: These 21 projects are ushering in the future of healthcare

With the host of challenges facing the healthcare industry—particularly in the U.S.—it can be easy to forget that serious progress is being made in everything from diagnostics and drug discovery to health monitoring and facilities design.

The World Changing Ideas honorees below have focused their impact on bringing forth a future of healthcare that is more accurate, equitable, and climate-conscious. That includes Atmo Biosciences’ ingestible capsule that can help identify GI issues; an AI-fueled tool for drug discovery that outperform’s Google DeepMind’s AlphaFold; and several honorable mentions innovating around health platforms, neurological episode tracking, and more.

Winners

AI-powered outpatient surgery, Oath Surgical
Read more about Oath Surgical’s AI-powered effort to improve the outpatient surgery experience for patients and surgeons.

Atmo gas capsule system, Atmo Biosciences
Traditional methods of diagnosing functional gastrointestinal issues can be invasive or require patients to be stationary for long periods of time. Atmo Biosciences’ ingestible capsule is designed to make diagnosing these GI disorders more straightforward. The sensor-filled capsule is taken orally and passes through the GI tract, collecting data during its transit time using measurements of temperature, hydrogen concentration, and other metrics that inform the company’s proprietary comprehensive test of gut motility. The product achieved FDA 510(k) clearance in June 2025 and launched the next month. Since launch, more than a dozen clinics have begun using the Atmo capsule to diagnose patients, and research institutions have adopted it to help study drug absorption, microbiome interactions, and more.

Augmented reality for trauma care, Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory
A traumatic injury can be difficult enough to treat in a well-equipped hospital setting, but in a remote or resource-limited one the challenges multiply and impact survival rate. To help emergency personnel, the Johns Hopkins Applied Physics Laboratory is using augmented reality to create medical guidance technology that can allow first responders understand and treat life-threatening injuries in challenging settings. The lab has used data from CT scans to enable detailed predictions of anatomy using an AR headset, making it possible to visualize internal organ placement during patient assessments. The lab has also built an AR-based heads-up display for ultrasounds, giving visually guidance and voice commands for eFAST exams, which can identify internal bleeding or collapsed lungs.

Extend artificial womb, Vitara Biomedical
Among the challenges that a prematurely born baby faces is the fact that their lungs may not be developed enough to breathe air. That’s why Vitara Biomedical has been developing the Extend System, an artificial womb that offers infants born as early as 22 weeks (roughly half of a full-term pregnancy) a fluid-filled environment to develop in for up to 28 days. The company has tested the system on more than 350 animals and is preparing for human testing, submitting its Investigational Device Exemption submission to the Food and Drug Administration.

Human milk fat analog, Checkerspot
Human breast milk contains a lipid called OPO, which provides most of the calories for babies who nurse. Many formulas use palm or coconut oil as a stand-in for this fat. Checkerspot is taking a different approach: The company ferments microalgae, which produces a lipid with the same structure as OPO. The process reduces the environmental toll that palm oil-based formulas have, using fermentation tanks that make production possible anywhere. In October 2025, the company signed an agreement with fermentation-based manufacturer Huvepharma to scale the product to commercial volumes that can be sold to global formula manufacturers.

NeuraLight eye-movement test, NeuraLight
As more treatments for neurological disorders like Parkinson’s, ALS, or multiple sclerosis are developed, there’s a growing need to quickly measure brain function to evaluate disease progression and, ultimately, drug efficacy. NeuraLight has used clinical data on eye movement and its links to brain function to build a test that can evaluate eye movement using a tablet. In 2025, the company presented data to the World Congress on Parkinson’s disease showing that the test detected statistically significant progression of lowered brain function within nine months—compared to conventional tests, which showed no progression. The tech has now been deployed in four commercial pharmaceutical trials and 16 clinical research partnerships at institutions that include Mount Sinai and Oxford.

PainVisible, VML
Pain is difficult to treat because it can be difficult to describe and identify. Nonverbal patients—infants, people with autism, patients who have had a stroke—face a higher risk of being misdiagnosed or having treatment delayed. PainVisible wants to address this. The tool uses heat patterns from pain-related temperature changes to create a thermal map that corresponds to pain intensity and location. In the past year, Spain’s largest hospital network, MVIVI, began testing the tool’s efficacy in identifying and treating pain in nonverbal patients, with use expanding beyond Spain to Dubai, Peru, and Colombia via MIVI’s global network. It’s now being tested in the U.S. to explore potential for regulatory approval here.

Pearl drug discovery model, Genesis Molecular AI
A critical part of creating new medicines is ensuring that their molecules properly bind to proteins in the body. AI such as Google DeepMind’s AlphaFold has proven effective at helping speed up the process of evaluating a molecule’s protein-binding potential. In October 2025, Genesis Molecular AI unveiled Pearl, a new molecular AI foundation model that outperform’s AlphaFold with an 85% success on standard benchmarks for interaction prediction, compared with AlphaFold’s 74% success. It’s already powering Genesis’s internal drug pipeline and is being used in partnership with major pharma partners.

Quinte Health Prince Edward County Memorial Hospital, HDR
Not every breakthrough in health has to be a specific treatment, diagnostic tool, or research breakthrough. Canadian health system Quinte Health’s forthcoming hospital in Prince Edward, Ontario, is showing that there’s plenty of room in healthcare for innovation around what hospitals look like and how their design influences the care they provide. The hospital is being constructed entirely of exposed mass timber—a material that stores carbon. Set to open in 2027, the project’s major construction phase, including its foundation, geothermal drilling, and the erection of the full timber structure, was completed at the end of 2025. The building’s ability to use mass timber while still being compliant with fire codes and safety practices could serve as a model for future climate-friendly healthcare infrastructure.

Turner Healthcare Facilities Funds, Turner Impact Capital
Impact investment firm Turner Impact Capital’s Healthcare Facilities Funds are focused on developing healthcare offerings in underserved communities. Over the past year, the funds have helped turn a vacant lot in Compton, California, into an 18,000-square-foot elder healthcare facility. Located in one of the country’s most medically underserved communities, the center offers outpatient medical care, behavioral health services, and social support—enabling older patients (average age 77) to live independently at home rather than in a nursing home.
