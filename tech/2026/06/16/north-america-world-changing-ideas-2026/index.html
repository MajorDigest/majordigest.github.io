How these projects in North America and Latin America are innovating beyond their hemisphere

The five honorees in North America and the honoree from Latin America illustrate the diversity and scope of the problems being solved across the western hemisphere. Some are standout local and state initiatives, such as climate-proofing homes in California and improving access to healthcare in the rural Midwest; others are exceptional innovations with global reach, such as breakthroughs in food tech and copper mining.

Latin America Winner

A new approach to copper extraction, Ceibo
Copper is an integral part of the technology we use every day, but producing it at scale is a growing challenge. Santiago, Chile–based Ceibo has developed a way to extract copper from other ores using sulfide leaching, an approach that works faster than other leaching methods and can recover up to 80% of the copper in a given ore. Designed to fit within existing copper mines, the technology uses five times less water than other methods, with one-third to half of the carbon emissions. Toward the end of 2025, the company began operating a demo plant on site at the Compañía Minera San Gerónimo in Chile, running continuously for six months and producing its first copper cathodes within 90 days.

North America Winners

ChompSaw, Chompshop
Originating as a master’s degree project at UPenn—and with a boost from a 2024 Shark Tank appearance—ChompSaw is a child-safe power tool that allows kids aged three and older to shape intricate 3D designs out of cardboard. It operates with a high-speed, oscillating hole punch that “chomps” through cardboard without sharp blades and has a protective safety guard. It teaches spatial awareness to young learners to create the next generation of inventors and problem-solvers. Since selling out multiple times in the 2024–2025 holiday season, the company has positioned itself as a classroom tool, offering a free curriculum on its website with downloadable instructions for building complex objects like airplanes, puppets, and puck slingshots.

Climate-resilient homes, HomeRES.ai
The Los Angeles wildfires in January 2025 destroyed more than 16,000 homes. Many of those devastating losses could have been prevented with simple climate-resilient building methods, but residential contractors are typically not trained in such techniques. HomeRES.ai is an AI platform that translates complex building codes into step-by-step guidance for California’s residential contractors—a unique offering when other AI tools are targeted to commercial contractors or homeowners themselves, who lack the critical building knowledge. It helps them identify rebate opportunities, upgrades that can slash bills, and features that increase property value, including tempered windows, auto-shut-off gas valves, and ember-resistant vents. Launched in 2025 after just five months of development, the platform has notched up various partners—including Habitat for Humanity, which will use the tool to support resiliency for low-income housing projects, expanding access beyond homeowners who can afford consultants. With the LA28 Olympics ahead, the company sees the urgent need to improve infrastructures, and it aims to work with 50,000 contractors by 2030.

Redefining sodium reduction, MicroSalt
The average person consumes twice the recommended limit of salt per day, and the cardiovascular risks are such that the World Health Organization aims to reduce that intake globally by 30% by 2030. That aligns with the mission of MicroSalt, a food tech company that produces salt crystals containing 50% less sodium than conventional salt for food manufacturers to incorporate into customers’ favorite snacks, soups, and baked goods. Last year, the company partnered with alternative food producer Daiya Foods to integrate MicroSalt into its dairy-free cheese, gluten-free pizza dough, and frozen desserts. To date, MicroSalt estimates it has offered 634 million servings with reduced salt—and is on the way to making a dent into a preventable global crisis.

Rural Health Innovation Initiative, Ōmcare
Around 60 million Americans, including several million over 65, live in rural communities where access to healthcare is spotty at best. They experience long distances to clinics and pharmacies as well as frequent provider shortages. In a recent survey, two-thirds of respondents expressed a desire for telehealth.

Via a six-month pilot program that rolled out in May 2026, Ōmcare is hoping to prove that such a model is possible. The program, administered in Lanesboro, Minnesota, population 744, was in preparation throughout last year, with partners in Blue Cross and Blue Shield of Minnesota and PharMerica. The virtual-first offering will provide triage nurses, clinicians, pharmacy home delivery, and 24/7 support all in one place. University of Minnesota’s Center for Healthy Aging and Innovation will lead the study to evaluate the pilot’s impact and help assess its feasibility to become permanent and to replicate around the country.

Unlocking housing investment with state law, California YIMBY
For decades, new housing development stalled in California due to the NIMBY movement, where vocal activists and homeowner groups opposed it under the guise of protecting neighborhoods and preserving local character. In 2017, California YIMBY was founded to counter that movement, and over eight years it has lobbied to successfully pass 34 bills into state law to unfreeze the bottlenecks on new housing production, including curbing zoning restrictions, parking mandates, and excessive fees. But 2025 was particularly momentous, as two landmark bills were passed and signed by Governor Gavin Newsom—one that overhauled a legacy law frequently used by NIMBYs, and another that legalizes more housing near public transit stations. Such effective legislative change has led to outcomes like the recent boom in accessory dwelling units (ADUs) in Los Angeles, and according to Mother Jones, the playbook is starting to be used by national “progressive superstars” like Zohran Mamdani and Elizabeth Warren. More generally, the wins are helping the group realize its long-term vision for a more equitable California with abundant, affordable housing and more vibrant communities.
