Uvalde officers 'need to face charges,' victim's guardian says

(NewsNation) — The Justice Department’s report on the law enforcement response to the Uvalde, Texas, school shooting in 2022 painted a damning picture of chaos, confusion and a lack of courage on the part of police.

Brett Cross is the uncle and legal guardian of Uvalde victim Uziyah Garcia. The 10-year-old died in classroom 111 at Robb Elementary School.

As Cross mourns the loss of his nephew, he is demanding accountability and seeking justice. Cross thinks the officers who responded to the school and waited more than an hour to subdue the gunman should face criminal charges.

“They knew that children were dying, and they did absolutely nothing,” Cross told NewsNation host Elizabeth Vargas, later adding, “They need to face charges.”

Cross said at the very least, responding officers should not be allowed on the job again.

“If they’re not going to save our kids, do you think that they’re going to save yours? They don’t ever need to be cops again,” Cross said.

The Justice Department’s report released Thursday found “cascading failures” and said police “demonstrated no urgency” in their response. The miscommunication and confusion that’s outlined in the report are vividly documented in video released after the shooting.

Footage showed officers waiting in hallways as the gunman went back and forth between two classrooms shooting at students and teachers.

“In other bodycam footage, you will see they’re like, ‘Oh, how do we go in? We don’t want to get shot.’ While our children are literally bleeding out and dying,” Cross said. “They’re more worried about their own personal health.”

It took 77 minutes for police to enter the school and fatally shoot the gunman. Andy Bershad, a retired NYPD emergency services unit detective, thinks officers had “no reason or excuse” to not keep trying to reach the children.

“With potential victims bleeding out, we wonder how many could have been saved from more rapid of a response to neutralizing the active threat that's in there,” Bershad told Vargas.

According to the Justice Department’s report, the greatest failure was that officers did not recognize the scene as an active shooter situation and instead treated it as a barricaded suspect response even though they had confirmation children were at the school.

“It should have never been deemed a barricade. It was clearly an active situation,” Bershad said.

He continued: “There was clearly a dire emergency to get in with a known shooter … to gain access, to stop the threats and free the victims, as well as the children that were in there. At 11:30 in the morning, there’s no secret that there’s children in the school on a weekday.”

A lack of communication was also listed in the report. While the shooting happened just before 11:30 a.m., Cross said he didn’t learn that Uziyah had died until around 8 o'clock that night.

“The only time that we found out that anybody had passed was when Governor Abbott got on there and said that there was 14 dead. We weren’t even told anything. You started looking around and you’re counting families, and you’re like this is it,” Cross said.

The shooting left 19 students and two teachers dead. Uziyah Garcia is remembered by his loved ones as a sweet, energetic boy who enjoyed jumping on trampolines and playing his Nintendo Switch.

Feeling that Uvalde failed 19 of its children, Cross is glad the Justice Department has released a report detailing what happened for others to see for themselves.
