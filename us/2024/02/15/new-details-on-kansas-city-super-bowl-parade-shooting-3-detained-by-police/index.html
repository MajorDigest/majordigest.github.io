New details on Kansas City Super Bowl parade shooting; 3 detained by police

KANSAS CITY, Mo. — Police have detained three people and recovered at least one gun as part of the investigation into the deadly Super Bowl parade shooting in Kansas City on Wednesday that left 21 people wounded, including at least eight children, and a mother of two dead.

At one point, video shows fans tackling a person that may have been a suspect in the shooting.

Kansas City Police Chief Stacey Graves could not confirm that fans tackled a suspected shooter, but she did confirm at a Wednesday evening press conference that three people have been detained and firearms were recovered as part of the investigation.

The shooting happened outside Union Station in Kansas City, where tens of thousands of people gathered Wednesday to celebrate the Chiefs' win over the San Francisco 49ers on Sunday in Super Bowl LVIII. Kansas City radio station KKFI confirmed that DJ Lisa Lopez-Galvan, a mother of two and host of "Taste of Tejano," was killed in the shooting.

The city had prepared for a turnout of as high as a million people. School was canceled as Chiefs fans flocked together to celebrate their team's second straight Super Bowl title and third in five years. Eight-hundred police officers were on site for the Super Bowl celebration.

But the cheers turned to screams at the end of the rally, witnesses describing the moments that sent thousands of fans running for cover.

"We just heard this big, loud bang, and everyone was just told to get down on the ground," one witness told reporters.

Said another: "When we saw it, my wife looked at me like — there were no words even spoken. We just tucked and rolled.

"... We just had to get out of there."

Joy turns to terror

Among those shot were at least eight children who had gathered to celebrate a happy occasion. Instead, it turned to horror.

"Fear," said Stephanie Meyer, chief nursing officer for Children's Mercy Hospital Kansas City. "The one word I would use to describe what we saw and how they felt when they came to us is fear."

Meyer said her hospital was treating 12 patients from the rally, including 11 children between ages 6 and 15, many of whom suffered gunshot wounds. Fortunately, Meyer said, all were expected to recover.

In a press conference, Kansas City Mayor Quinton Lucas said he was inside Union Station when the shooting occurred.

"We heard something, a number of us start running, and I see a stream of officers going the exact other direction with guns drawn, ready to address danger," Lucas said.

"I'm angry at what happened (Wednesday)," Graves said. "The people who came to this celebration should expect a safe environment.

"... Because of bad actors, which were very few, this tragedy occurred. Even in the presence of uniformed law enforcement officers who, again, ran toward (the suspects) and took them into custody."

In a statement, President Joe Biden said: "(Wednesday)'s events should move us, shock us, shame us into acting. What are we waiting for? What else do we need to see? How many more families need to be torn apart? It is time to act."

Chiefs players, including star quarterback Patrick Mahomes and star tight end Travis Kelce, released statements of support for those impacted by Wednesday's shooting, Kelce saying on X: "I am heartbroken over the tragedy that took place (Wednesday). My heart is with all who came out to celebrate with us and have been affected.

"KC, you mean the world to me."

The investigation is ongoing. The FBI is asking anybody with information, videos or phots of the incident to submit them.

Support from Highland Park

If there's another municipality that can relate to the tragedy that unfolded in Kansas City on Wednesday, it would be Highland Park, a north suburb of Chicago.

On July 4, 2022, seven people were killed and 48 wounded in a mass shooting at the Highland Park Fourth of July parade. Highland Park Mayor Nancy Rotering said the shooting in Kansas City on Wednesday brought up sad reminders of what happened in Highland Park.

"It reminds me of the nauseating, sinking feeling that I had when I found out that we had been hit by a mass shooting," Rotering said. "Nobody thinks it's going to come to their community. Every single person has said to me, 'Well, I don't think it will happen in my community.'

"And I'm like, 'Who would've thought it would've happened in Highland Park, as well?'"

Rotering says she has reached out to Lucas and will be available for support.
