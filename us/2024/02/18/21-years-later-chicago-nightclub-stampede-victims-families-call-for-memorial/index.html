21 years later: Chicago nightclub stampede victims' families call for memorial

CHICAGO — Saturday marks 21 years since 21 people were killed and more than 50 were injured in a stampede at E2 nightclub in the South Loop in Chicago.

Over two decades later, the grief is raw for many of those who lost a relative in the disaster on Feb. 17, 2003.

Family members of the victims gathered on Saturday for a somber vigil. They say they will continue to honor their loved ones, but some are now calling on local leaders to acknowledge them by creating a memorial at the site.

"His biggest accomplishment was my niece," Anjenita Myers, a victim's sister, said.

For the first time ever, Myers and her family came to the old E2 nightclub where her brother, 22-year-old Antonio Myers, and twenty other men and women lost their lives 21 years ago.

Antonio's daughter, Aniya Myers, who was just weeks old when he died, came too.

"I just wish somehow, someway, he could have still been here. There's no comfort being here," Aniya Myers said.

On Saturday afternoon families who lost a relative in the disaster braved the cold to comfort one another and remember the lives lost.

They say they want to see a memorial built at the site to honor the victims.

"Maybe what that memorial looks like is bricks in the sidewalk with 21 names in them whatever we have to do to get a memorial out here so next year it looks different that's what we are pushing for," Dawn Valenti, a family friend of an E2 nightclub victim, said.

Shapara Hicks, who lost her mom Latorya McGraw in the disaster, said she agrees that a memorial is needed.

"Every single victim that passed away needs to be acknowledged," Hicks said.

While Alderman Pat Dowell (3rd Ward) agrees with the idea of building a memorial to honor the victims, he said no plans have been put forward.

"I have met with E2 family members in the past and am fully committed to the idea of a memorial for the young people who tragically lost their lives that fateful evening. I have not, however, been presented with a proposal from family members nor have I been contacted by the property owner to discuss anything specific," Alderman Pat Dowell (3rd Ward) said.

On Feb. 17, 2003, E2 nightclub was jam-packed. Things then got out of hand when security guards inside deployed pepper spray to try and stop a fight. That's when people panicked and rushed to a narrow stairway, leading to Michigan Avenue.

Demetricta Carwell was one of the 21 who didn't make it.

"Having my grandmother was a blessing, but not having my mom around, it's like a lot of lost pieces without her," Laneisha Crawford, Carwell's daughter, said.

Less than a year before the tragedy a Cook County judge ordered the second floor of the club to close due to code violations.

Prosecutors brought involuntary manslaughter charges against the club's two owners, but they were ultimately cleared of those charges.

"I just ask that you put your hands together and pray for us too because even though it was back in 2003, it feels like 2024," Hicks said.

Two decades later the pain remains for family members.

"We need a memorial there's nothing else to talk about," said Walter Green, the father of an E2 nightclub victim, said.

In addition to the 21 people killed, over 50 were injured.

A number of wrongful death and personal injury lawsuits were filed in the wake of the stampede, leading to millions in settlement payments.
