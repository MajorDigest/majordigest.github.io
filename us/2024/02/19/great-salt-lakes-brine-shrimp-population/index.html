Great Salt Lake's brine shrimp population booms, impacting future of Utah

SALT LAKE CITY (KTVX) -- Brine shrimp, a crustacean native to the Great Salt Lake and recently named the official state crustacean, have seen an incredible population growth over the last year, symbolizing hope for the Great Salt Lake and serving as evidence of the state's efforts to improve the lake's health.

NewsNation's affiliate KTVX spoke with John Luft, program manager of the Utah Division of Wildlife Resources' Great Salt Lake Ecosystem Program, regarding these small crustaceans and their vital role in our ecosystem.

"Essentially everything eats brine shrimp," Luft said. "The migratory birds reap the benefits of this highly productive food source available. You could also say that brine shrimp actually feed people around the world, since brine shrimp are the food that table shrimp -- that you and I would eat -- that’s what those table shrimp consume that are grown in aquaculture industry."

Luft said that over half of the shrimp people consume are grown in the aquaculture industry, rather than caught wild in the ocean. And the Great Salt Lake is a huge contributor to this industry, supplying almost half of the worldwide demand for brine shrimp.

This makes the Great Salt Lake not just a factor for commercially grown shrimp, but an invaluable player.

"Great Salt Lake supplies almost half of the worldwide demand for brine shrimp cysts," Luft said. "So, Utah is an extremely important player in the global aquaculture industry,"

These "cysts" -- another word for eggs -- are what go to commercial growers for hatching. And hatchability is quite important for large buyers.

"What Great Salt Lake cysts are famous for is having hatchability at 90% or even better, which is much better than what we had last year, which hovered around 60%," Luft said. "A lot of aquaculture industries like that 90% hatch rate."

So, what changed to allow for such an immense increase in the health of Utah's globally beloved state crustacean?

Last year, the Utah Division of Forestry, Fire and State Lands -- in partnership with the Utah Division of Water Resources and Union Pacific Railroad -- changed the "causeway berm" that splits the northern and southern parts of the Great Salt Lake. They did this in an effort to address the lake's salinity, or the amount of salt in the water, after reportedly having a breach in the causeway that created a channel connecting the two sides.

Due to the ongoing drought causing less and less water to go into the Great Salt Lake in recent years, the salinity levels in both arms of the Great Salt Lake had risen significantly. Rising salinity levels caused last year's dismal hatchability rate of 60%. And even when they did hatch, the "health" of the eggs was not nearly as positive as scientists and experts would've liked.

While an average harvest typically garners around 25 million to 35 million pounds of cysts, the 2022-23 harvest season saw only about 19 million pounds of cysts harvested.

After the berm was raised, this past season saw roughly 29 million pounds of cysts harvested, a jump of around 10 million eggs.

And, for wildlife's sake, the Great Salt Lake and its associated wetlands serve as a hugely important food source to around 10 million migratory birds, including about 330 different species. One of these species of migratory birds is the Eared Grebe.

"The Eared Grebe -- every fall, Eared Grebes come to Great Salt Lake, and they’ll exclusively eat brine shrimp when they’re here," Luft said. "Each Eared Grebe will eat about 25,000-30,000 brine shrimp per day. And at times, we’ve had over 5 million Eared Grebes, which is about 95% of the Eared Grebe population."

The importance of Great Salt Lake's brine shrimp population cannot be overstated, making the lake a hub for wildlife and source for global commerce.

"We thank our sister agencies and other partners for taking quick action to address salinity issues," Luft said. "It allowed the brine shrimp to quickly rebound, and hatch rates were around 90% this year, as opposed to 60% last year when the salinity levels were higher. The greater-than-90% hatch rates are what give the Great Salt Lake brine shrimp cysts their reputation for being some of the highest quality cysts in the world."

Since October 2022, the water levels in the Great Salt Lake have already risen nearly four feet, showing promise for one of the largest saline lakes in the western hemisphere, and Utah's ecological powerhouse.
