Free school meals proposed for TN students

RUTHERFORD COUNTY, Tenn. (WKRN) — Rutherford County Schools (RCS) is among districts statewide that returned to no longer serving free meals when a pandemic-era federal program ended. The district's bad meal debt is nearing half a million dollars so far this school year.

"We give meals to prisoners, but we cannot even give them to our own kids, which, to me, is just an absolute travesty," said state Rep. Kevin Raper (R-Bradley County), a longtime educator. "We have to find a way to feed these kids so that then optimal learning can take place."

According to Feeding America, one in eight Tennessee children face hunger. A nonprofit organization, the Tennessee Justice Center, posted on social media, "School meals are a first-line response to this crisis. With the support of the Tennessee Justice Center and partners, legislation has been filed that could make school meals accessible to all students at no cost to their families!"

Over 30% of TN families skipping meals as food insecurity continues to rise

The situation being faced in Rutherford County is similar to a lot of districts in Tennessee. RCS offered free meals for all students during the pandemic through a temporary federal program. When that program ended, the district had to switch back to the traditional federal program. If parents can't afford to pay for their kids' meals, officials said they must apply and qualify for the federal free or reduced lunch program.

Raper felt students shouldn't be categorized like this in the first place.

"There's still those stigmas that are involved as far as the kids scared of being identified and so on," he said. "You know, they've done everything from stamp your hand to, there are just numbers of options. I'm not sure which of those are the most effective, but there have been numbers of options to try to avoid that stigma of somebody that's qualifying for free or reduced."

Right now, more than half of RCS students qualify for the free and reduced lunch program. District officials told News 2 they don't deny any student a meal and students who don't pay get the same meal as students who are paying. Currently, RCS has $452,000 in accumulated bad meal debt.

"We send regular notices to parents if they have accumulated debt and encourage them to apply for the F/R program. Some choose not to apply for the program, even though they likely qualify," a school district statement to News 2 read. "Any remaining balance at the end of the fiscal year must be absorbed by the school district."

Robertson County schools offering meals at no cost

Raper is proposing a bill that would give free breakfast and lunch for all public school kids in the state of Tennessee. He said they're still ironing out the cost and how to pay for it.

"We looked to California and they were right at $200 million, so we thought, 'Well, ours surely will be below that.' Well, when it came back to that large amount, then I immediately took it off notice and decided, we were going to work on that, tried our best to get our fiscal note down, which we have," he said.

Metro Nashville Public Schools and Robertson County Schools are two Middle Tennessee districts that continued offering free meals after the pandemic-era federal program ended.

"One of the reasons why that you'll see some systems that are currently getting free and reduced and the one that I'm from, which is Bradley County, they are currently getting free breakfast and free lunch for, it's about a three and a half year period. And the reason why is the federal laws have changed somewhat and made it a little bit more friendly," said Raper. "One of the things that will help out on the fiscal note on our bill is if everyone in the state that qualifies under the new federal guidelines applies and receives that, then that automatically reduces the pot of money that we're gonna have to come up with."

He added that one complaint he's received was how this benefits families with higher incomes.

"I have had a little bit of blowback as far as what about a kid that comes from a household income that's half a million dollars? Why do they need something like this?" he recalled. "Well, it's not necessarily based on need, then it's like a tax break. We are right now as all the legislators trying our best to save taxpayers money."

Raper said he and state Sen. Adam Lowe (R-Calhoun) began working on Senate Bill 208 and House Bill 255 after seeing a need in Tennessee.

"I am a lifelong educator, retired educator, I saw the need many, many years at different levels. I was the principal at one point," he explained. "I was teacher for over 30 years, I saw this need in so many occasions, and so many types of situations. And I knew that just going by poverty level wasn't covering it at all."
