Up to 3K Colorado cases to get DNA retests during investigation

DENVER (KDVR) — A forensic scientist previously employed by the Colorado Bureau of Investigation is costing the state millions to review her work after "anomalies" were found in her DNA testing analyses.

According to a state funding request obtained by FOX31, the Colorado Department of Public Safety is asking for nearly $7.5 million to spend on reviewing and retesting DNA samples processed by a longtime state forensic scientist, Yvonne "Missy" Woods.

The request includes funding for an accountant to manage the payments required for impacted district attorneys' offices. The offices will be responsible for reviewing the legal ramifications of impacted cases — which could total nearly 2,300 — and possibly presenting the cases for retrial.

Woods worked in CBI's Forensic Services division for 29 years, according to CBI, and left the position in November. She is now subject to an internal affairs investigation in addition to a criminal investigation for the anomalies in her work.

As FOX31 has previously reported, Woods has been connected to some high-profile cases in Colorado, including the Kobe Bryant sex assault case that was eventually dropped in 2004 and the Alex Ewing hammer killings case, which was prosecuted in 2021. She also was recognized by the Polis administration in March 2022, receiving the Tom Clements Award for "Outstanding Service in Government."

An attorney for Woods told FOX31 in November that she intended to work with investigators.

"She's worked with and trained generations of prosecutors, scientists and law enforcement agents over those years," attorney Ryan Brackley said in a statement, adding that Woods "expects nothing less than a full, complete and professional investigation into these allegations and has already communicated her intent to cooperate with CBI’s inquiry."

CBI said this incident is isolated to Woods and is not a systemic issue.

Thousands of cases may be impacted by DNA test issues

In the funding request, the Department of Public Safety said the DNA anomalies could lead to retrying hundreds of even thousands of criminal cases.

"The CBI is conducting a comprehensive review of the scientist’s full body of work to determine the impact — if any — on court findings on the DNA testing performed by the employee," the department wrote in its funding request. "The Department takes this issue extremely seriously and is taking immediate action to address the allegations."

The department noted that if Woods' conduct impacted the outcome of a case, causing evidentiary hearings and testimony from DNA experts to be needed, that would require approximately 40-80 hours of work per case.

"Cases that may be resolved without an evidentiary hearing will take less time," the department stated. "This estimate includes only the post-conviction process, and cases that will result in
re-trial will require an additional 100-200 hours of work for multiple professionals on each case."

DNA will be retested for an estimated 2,500-3,000 cases, the department stated, and the testing will be completed by a third-party laboratory. Testing per case is estimated to cost about $1,000 — equaling about $3 million.

So far, the department has reviewed approximately 10% of Woods' total cases, according to the funding request.

"Of those, 229 cases are impacted by this employee’s actions. Based on this initial review it is estimated the full review of 100% of this former employee’s work will identify approximately 2,290 affected cases," the department said.

That means about 76% of Woods' cases could need to be revisited in the courts.

According to the Colorado District Attorneys’ Council, judicial districts will need to rework any cases using Woods' DNA test results. In a letter to the CBI, the council said they "conservatively estimate 100 cases per district attorney in the eight largest offices and 25 in the smaller offices ... we are looking at about 72,000 hours of work statewide. At an estimated attorney salary of $61 per hour, the anticipated costs are about $4.4m...."

CBI is requesting nearly $4.4 million to reimburse DA expenses.
