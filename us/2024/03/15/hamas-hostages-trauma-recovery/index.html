Hamas hostages face 'profound' behavior change: Expert

(NewsNation) — The hostages violently kidnapped and held in the Gaza Strip face trauma most people can hardly imagine. Their visible scars, cuts and bruises may heal, but emotionally and mentally, they'll never be the same.

In her first TV interview since being abducted by Hamas on Oct. 7, 2023, Chicago-area mom Judith Raanan shared the story of her captivity on NewsNation's “Elizabeth Vargas Reports."

“We had to ask permission to get up. … We could not go to the toilet without asking,” Raanan said. “It’s like a casino. There is no clock.”

Raanan and her daughter Natalie, 18, were among the first hostages to be freed.

James Phillips, a licensed mental health counselor specializing in trauma, discussed the challenges hostages face once they are freed, adjusting to life back home and knowing dozens remain behind.

"We're looking at people that have been in captivity for a large amount of time," Phillips said. "And it's not just one trauma that they're experiencing. They're experiencing trauma event, after trauma, after trauma event.

"It is very hard to come back into society and not experience profound behavior change because of what you've seen."

Phillips referenced Raanan and her daughter having had their wrists zip-tied behind their backs, explaining that everyday items may all of a sudden be triggering, scary or uncomfortable.

"How often do we get a bracelet to go into an amusement park? Every time that happens for her, she's going to be reminded of that time," he said.

A study in the Journal of the Royal Society of Medicine revealed women and children, in particular, have a harder time recovering from being held hostage.

"The rule in therapy is generally the more vulnerable, the more traumatized," Phillips said as Raanan repeatedly talked about how her daughter was terrified of being raped while held in captivity.

The United Nations had previously found “clear and convincing” information hostages have been raped and sexually abused in Gaza.

Raanan recalls armed men bursting into the room while she and Natalie were still in their pajamas.

“My girl was afraid. She said, ‘Mom, I’m afraid to be raped.’ I said, 'Nobody’s going to do nothing to you,'” Raanan said.

Phillips stressed that the first few hours after a hostage is released is very important.

"Those few hours can be very traumatizing to the person just coming out because, remember, they've been in an enclosed space with a limited amount of people over a long period of time," he said. "And you come out, there's going to be everyday things that are going to startle you, maybe a family member wants to run to you and hug you. That may not be a good thing."

Raanan said she has had trouble sleeping since her ordeal.

"The symptoms of PTSD (post-traumatic stress disorder), unfortunately, are very long-reaching," Phillips said. "Sleep disturbance is a big one. Hypervigilance is another one. And the unfortunate thing about PTSD is you see the symptoms of those traumas on through your life through years to come."

Now that she's home, Raanan has spent the last few months learning to cope, and she says painting is one of her most therapeutic activities.

"It's a great thing to be out, but it's also hurtful to know there's people you have bonds with, people you're connected with that are still there," Phillips said. "That can be just as just as brutal as the trauma itself."
