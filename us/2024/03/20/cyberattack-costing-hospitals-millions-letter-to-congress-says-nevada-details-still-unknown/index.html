Cyberattack costing hospitals millions, letter to Congress says; Nevada details still unknown

LAS VEGAS (KLAS) -- Four out of every five hospitals in the U.S. have been affected by a cyberattack that the American Hospital Association (AHA) is calling the "most significant and consequential in American history."

And while Nevada hospitals continue to provide medical treatment, communications systems that were "significantly impaired" have not been fully restored, according to an update released Wednesday by the Nevada Hospital Association.

The lingering effects of the Feb. 21 cyberattack are causing cash flow problems at 82% of the nation's hospitals. Two-thirds of all hospitals report a revenue impact greater than $1 million per day, according to NHA's report.

The Nevada hospitals affected by the cyberattack have not been disclosed. 8 News Now has reached out to state government officials for that information, but it has not been released.

"The hospital system’s ability to provide medical treatment, care for trauma patients, perform surgeries, and administer other treatments remains in good condition," according to NHA's report. But the business of running a hospital is complicated by this new problem, and national officials are asking for help.

AlphV BlackCat has been identified as the group behind the attack, but two weeks ago it was revealed that the free-lance hacker who originally stole personal data -- an affiliate known as "Notchy" -- still has the information. BlackCat disappeared after receiving a $22 million payment, according to krebsonsecurity.com. Subsequent reports by Menlo Security and The Register tied Notchy to China, and indicated Notchy wanted the money paid to BlackCat.

The information is reportedly 4 terabytes of personal data -- "a wide array of personal and medical details, notably including data from critical national healthcare programs such as Medicare and TRICARE," according to Menlo Security.

When malware compromised the Change Healthcare system, medical providers were advised to disconnect. That created disruptions in ordering prescriptions, patient billing and access to medical records. Providers have been reconnecting systems since then, but the damage has already been done, according to the letter from AHA's president and CEO, Richard J. Pollack.

About three out of four hospitals responding to an AHA survey indicate that patient care is being affected. The most common problem, reported by 40% of hospitals, involves "access difficulties, prior authorization delays, and utilization requirements."

Cash flow problems are threatening some businesses' ability to pay employees and acquire supplies to provide medical care, according to AHA. More than half the hospitals reported "significant or serious" financial problems.

"The staggering loss of revenue means that some hospitals and health systems may be unable to pay salaries for clinicians and other members of the care team, acquire necessary medicines and supplies, and pay for mission critical contract work in areas such as physical security, dietary and environmental services," Pollack's letter said.

The Service Employees International Union (SEIU), which represents Nevada hospital employees, was not aware of any disruptions in payroll here.

The research firm Compass Point estimates that healthcare is losing between $500 million and $1 billion in daily revenues, according to information distributed by NHA.

Pollack's letter asks Congress for more resources to fight cyberattacks, as well as policies that take into account the severe impact cyberattacks have on hospitals' ability to meet current Medicare requirements.

"The AHA cannot support proposals for mandatory cybersecurity requirements being levied on hospitals as if they were at fault for the success of hackers in perpetrating a crime. Many recent cyberattacks against hospitals and the health care system, including the current Change Healthcare cyberattack, have originated from third-party technology and other vendors," according to Pollack.

"No organization, including federal agencies, is or can be immune from cyberattacks. Imposing fines or cutting Medicare payments would diminish hospital resources needed to combat cybercrime and would be counterproductive to our shared goal of preventing cyberattacks. The Administration’s budget proposal for hospitals is misguided, and it will not improve the overall cybersecurity posture of the health care sector," Pollack said.

Source: Greg Haas, KLAS, CBS News
