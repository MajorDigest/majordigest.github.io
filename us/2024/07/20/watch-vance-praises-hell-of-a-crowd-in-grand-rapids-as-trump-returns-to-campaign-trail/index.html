Back on campaign trail, Trump talks shooting, trade, more in Grand Rapids

GRAND RAPIDS, Mich. (WOOD) — Welcomed with cheers, applause and chants of "USA, USA," former President Donald Trump returned to the campaign trail Saturday with a rally in Grand Rapids — the first since the attempt on his life one week prior.

"Let me begin this evening with a very special thanks to Americans nationwide and to you here today for your extraordinary outpouring of love and support in the wake of the horrific last Saturday event," Trump said as he began his remarks at Van Andel Arena downtown. "I stand before you only by the grace of almighty God. That's true."

Trump, whose injured ear was covered by a small flesh-colored bandage rather than a large white one as it had been earlier in the week at the Republican National Convention, praised the doctors who treated him immediately after the shooting, the people of Butler, Pennsylvania, and the doctor caring for him now.

"We continue to pray for the recovery of the two citizens who were wounded in that evil attack, David Dutch and James Copenhaver, two really incredible people," Trump said. "Corey Comperatore was the brave firefighter who died ... shielding his wife and daughters from bullets. He shielded them from the bullets of this horrendous person. Corey was a hero and we will carry his memory in our hearts for all time."

The crowd chanted "Corey, Corey, Corey."

TRUMP HITS FAMILIAR NOTES

There has been talk of "lowering the temperature" of campaign rhetoric to condemn political violence and encourage national unity. But after talking about the shooting, Trump projected the same tone he has since his first run in 2016: He was upbeat, bombastic and scornful of his opponents.

He praised Michigan, a key state on the electoral path to the White House, and its people as "hardworking patriots."

"We will never stop working to deliver a magnificent future for our people and together we will fight, fight, fight, right? And we will win, win win," Trump said. "Less than four months from now, we are going to win Michigan, we are going to take back the White House with a monumental landslide. And with your help, we are going to make America great again."

He pointed out the sense of unity on display at the RNC and contrasted it with the apparent disarray of the other party as an increasing number of Democrats (including U.S. Rep. Hillary Scholten of Grand Rapids) suggest President Joe Biden should step aside. Biden has so far rejected such calls and promised to be back on the campaign trail next week, though he has been isolating after being diagnosed with COVID-19 this week. Trump's speech included a few swipes at Biden, questioning his fitness to serve, and he used the familiar "crooked Joe" moniker.

Turning to campaign issues, he promised to combat inflation, cut taxes, produce more oil domestically and support law enforcement. He made references to culture wars that have driven conservative voters, promising an executive order to cut funding for schools "pushing critical race theory, transgender insanity, and any other inappropriate racial, sexual or political content onto the shoulders of our children."

A large chunk of his speech was about trade and the automotive industry, topics important to the Michigan crowd.

"Most importantly for the people of your state, this great state, I will rescue the U.S. auto industry from obliteration," Trump said, saying that it should be the market that drives a transition to majority electric cars rather than policy.

He cited the moves he made on trade when he was president, including agreements with Mexico and Canada and tariffs on Chinese imports. He promised more trade reform if he is reelected.

"When I'm back in the White House, the way they will sell their product in America is to build it in America. They have to build it in America and they have to use you people to build it, thank you very much. It's very simple. We will build American, buy American, we're going to hire American. We're going to get it going. We're going to get the auto industry back, it's ridiculous," Trump said.

Saying he would crack down on illegal immigration at the southern border, he again argued other nations are sending their criminals to the United States — a claim that the Associated Press reports there is no evidence to support — and blamed immigrants for crime, a claim he has used to criticize Biden's immigration policies. Federal crime statistics show violent crime dropped nationwide in 2021 and 2022 following a spike in 2020.

He made repeated references to election cheating by his opponents, a repeatedly debunked claim he has been making since he lost to Biden in 2020. He said if elected, he would "secure our elections," requiring one-day voting and voter identification.

He also railed against the criminal cases against him as politically motivated.

Trump distanced himself from the conservative Project 2025 plan, calling some of the things it includes extreme. He rejected others' characterizations of himself as an extremist and a threat to democracy.

"Last week, I took a bullet for democracy," he said.

Trump sustained a gunshot wound to the top of his right ear in the shooting in Pennsylvania on July 13. His campaign released an update on his health Saturday afternoon, with his doctor saying he has has checked Trump's wound every day.

"There is intermittent bleeding requiring a dressing to be in place," Dr. Ronny Jackson — also a U.S. representative from Texas — said in the release. "Given the broad and blunt nature of the wound itself, no sutures were required."

He concluded that "former President Trump is doing well."

PENSLER DROPS OUT OF SENATE RACE

Cognizant of how down-ballot November races will affect his and vice-versa, Trump highlighted former U.S. Rep. Mike Rogers, the candidate he has backed to fill Michigan's U.S. Senate seat being vacated by retiring Sen. Debbie Stabenow, a Democrat.

"Mike Rogers is going to be a great senator. He's got gravitas," Trump said. "Highly respected in Washington. I think it should be an easy race."

Trump ceded the podium to businessman Sandy Pensler, who announced he was withdrawing from the Republican Senate primary and threw his support behind Rogers, saying he would "make a heck of a senator."

"My campaign was always about making America better. The best way to do that is to enact President Trump's policies. ... To do that, we need control of the Senate. A divisive continued primary effort hurts the chances of that, and that's not OK. So tonight, I'm doing the right thing and ceasing my Senate candidacy," he said. "President Trump endorsed Mike Rogers. Tonight, so am I."

Rogers thanked Pensler for his support.

"I am honored to have you stand with us and all Republicans to win the state of Michigan," Rogers said.

Rogers blamed Democrats for an "unsafe America" and said he would work to "bring back law and order.

"Let's flip the Senate. Let's elect Donald J. Trump and Mike Rogers to the Senate and let's get busy," Rogers said.

VANCE: 'WE WANT AN AMERICA THAT WORKS FOR AMERICANS'

Trump was preceded by his running mate U.S. Sen. JD Vance. Speaking at his first rally as the Republican nominee for vice president, he praised the large and energetic crowd.

"I've got to come to Grand Rapids more often. Wow," he said. "I find it hard to believe that a week ago, an assassin tried to take President Trump's life, and now we have got a hell of a crowd in Michigan to welcome him back on the campaign trail."

Vance, who as Ohio's junior senator is not particularly well known to voters, sought to introduce himself, talking about how he was raised and his family and saying he was a veteran and business owner.

"I gotta be honest, it's still a little bit weird to see my name on those (campaign) signs," Vance said. "Such an honor, such an incredible honor. You think about how I grew up and you think about how nobody in my immediate family had gone to college — and here I am, getting to represent this ticket in the great state of Michigan, getting an opportunity to earn your vote as the next vice president of the United States."

"I chose him because he's for the worker," Trump said of Vance. "He's for the people that work so hard and perhaps weren't treated like they should have been."

Hitting core platform issues, Vance touched on immigration and energy independence and promised to encourage the creation of domestic manufacturing jobs, an issue particularly important to many voters in Michigan, Vance's home state of Ohio and Pennsylvania, another key state in November. He encouraged voters to compare the four years of the Trump presidency to the four years of Joe Biden's presidency and said Trump's term was better.

"We want an America that works for Americans, and the only way to do it is to reelect Donald J. Trump president of the United States. I'm going to do everything I can. I know you will, too," Vance said.

SPEAKERS EMPHASIZE IMPORTANCE OF MICHIGAN

Trump supporters who wanted to attend the rally started lining up as early as Thursday outside Van Andel Arena. On Saturday afternoon, the line extended more than a mile. Doors opened at 1 p.m., and Michigan Republican Party Chair Pete Hoekstra took the podium as the first speaker at 3 p.m., even as people continued to be seated. Trump started speaking around 5:20 p.m. and wrapped up around 7:10 p.m.

With Michigan considered a swing state fewer than 110 days from the November general election, the state is expected to see increasing attention from both candidates. President Biden was on the southeast side of the state last week and Vice President Kamala Harris was in metro Kalamazoo a few days ago.

Biden won Michigan in 2020, but Trump won it in 2016. Polls show a tight race this time around. A late June poll conducted by EPIC-MRA before the two met for a debate showed Trump with a 4-point lead over Biden in Michigan. The poll had a margin of error of plus or minus 4%. An Emerson College poll conducted after last week's assassination attempt showed Trump leading Biden by 3 points in Michigan; this one has a margin of error of plus or minus 2.1%.

"JD Vance did admit he needs Michigan. That might be the first time we've seen that from a person from Ohio," U.S. Rep. Bill Huizenga, R-Zeeland, joked at the podium before Vance's remarks. "The road to the White House runs through Michigan."

"We are going to win Michigan, and when we win Michigan, we will have elected Donald Trump as the next president of the United States of America," Hoekstra, the Michigan GOP chair, said to cheers and applause. "We invite you to join the Republican party, a team that's committed to winning."

Hoekstra was followed by a number of other Republican lawmakers and candidates. They hit on key issues, talked about Michigan priorities and encouraged people to vote for Trump, whose performance in November will likely affect those down the ballot.

"We all know how important this election is. We don't have a lot of last chances left. We have to elect President Trump and Sen. Vance, return them to the White House, elect a Republican majority in the House of Representatives and the United States Senate, secure our border, protect our neighborhoods, back our police and finally put our foreign adversaries back in check where they belong," Tom Barrett, a candidate for the U.S. House, said.

"Here's what I know for sure: When President Trump sets his mind on doing things, they get done. He promised us in Michigan that he'd build a new Soo lock, and following his aggressive prompting, the project is well underway. He promised to secure funding for the Great Lakes and he made sure it happened," U.S. Rep. Jack Bergman, R-Watersmeet, said.

The final $479 million in federal funding for the second large lock at the Soo was passed as part of the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act in 2021 and signed into law by Biden. Trump's administration twice proposed cuts to the Great Lakes Restoration Initiative before backing it in 2019.

—News 8's Corinne Moore contributed to this report.
